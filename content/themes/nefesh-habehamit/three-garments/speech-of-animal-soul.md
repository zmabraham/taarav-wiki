# Speech of the Animal Soul

## English Explanation

*Dibbur* (דִּבּוּר, speech) is the second of the three garments of the animal soul
in the Hemshech Taarav's framework. Speech is unique among the three garments because
it bridges the inner world (thought) and the outer world (action) — it is the first
externalization of the soul's inner life, giving thought a presence in the world while
remaining more fluid and reversible than action.

The animal soul's speech is characterized by its tendency toward gratification and
self-assertion: gossip, idle chatter, boasting, complaining, flattery, and (in its
worst expressions) dishonesty and harmful speech. The Rashab notes that each of these
represents a misuse of the divine faculty of speech — the same faculty through which
God created the world and Torah was given.

Speech has disproportionate spiritual weight compared to thought, precisely because
it enters the external world and affects other people. A thought that remains
entirely internal causes no external harm; speech that exits the mouth cannot be
recalled and affects its hearers. This is why the prohibition of lashon hara (harmful
speech) is treated with such seriousness in Jewish law and in the Rashab's teachings.

At the same time, the faculty of speech, redirected toward Torah and prayer, becomes
extraordinarily powerful in a positive direction. The Rashab follows the tradition
that establishes speech as the uniquely human quality (*midaber*, מְדַבֵּר, "the
speaking one") — redirecting this faculty to its divine purpose is a complete
transformation of the animal soul's speech-garment.
