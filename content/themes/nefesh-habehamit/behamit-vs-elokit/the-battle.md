# The Battle Between the Two Souls

## English Explanation

The Hemshech Taarav's account of the internal battle between the *nefesh ha'elokit*
(divine soul) and the *nefesh habehamit* (animal soul) is one of its most practically
relevant teachings. The Rashab, building on the framework established in the Tanya,
describes this as a genuine, ongoing war — not a metaphor but a real contest for
dominance over thought, speech, and action.

The battlefield is the *body* — specifically, its three garments (thought, speech,
action). Each soul seeks to express itself through these garments. The divine soul
seeks to fill them with Torah thoughts, holy speech, and mitzvah actions. The animal
soul seeks to fill them with thoughts of self-interest, idle or harmful speech, and
actions driven by physical desire.

The key strategic principle the Rashab articulates is that neither soul can be
completely destroyed in this world (this would be the achievement of the complete
tzaddik or the complete rasha, respectively — extreme spiritual poles). For the
*beinoni* (the intermediate person, which is the Rashab's primary audience), the goal
is *control*, not elimination: the divine soul must maintain governance over the body
and its expressions even when the animal soul continues to exert its pull internally.

The Rashab also notes that the battle is never uniformly intense. There are moments
of relative ease (after prayer, after Torah study) and moments of heightened assault
(during periods of spiritual weakness, physical illness, or temptation). Recognizing
the rhythms of the battle and preparing accordingly is part of sophisticated spiritual
practice.
