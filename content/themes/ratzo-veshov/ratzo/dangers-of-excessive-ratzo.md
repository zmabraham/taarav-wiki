# Dangers of Excessive Ratzo

## English Explanation

The Hemshech Taarav takes seriously the spiritual *danger* of excessive *ratzo*
(running/rushing toward the divine) without the counterbalancing *shov* (return).
This is not a merely theoretical concern — the Rashab invokes the paradigmatic case
from the Torah: the deaths of *Nadav and Avihu*, the sons of Aaron the High Priest.

Nadav and Avihu entered the Holy of Holies on the inauguration day of the Tabernacle
with "strange fire" (*esh zara*, אֵשׁ זָרָה) — fire they had not been commanded to
bring. The Rashab interprets this as an act of excessive ratzo: their soul's love
(אַהֲבָה, ahavah) for the divine was so overwhelming that it overwhelmed their
embodied existence. They "died" not as punishment for sin but as the natural
consequence of ratzo without shov — the soul's upward rush so completely overwhelmed
the physical self that it could not return.

The Rashab uses this as a teaching about spiritual practice: the goal is not to
achieve the maximum possible divine intensity but to sustain a *livable*, ongoing
divine relationship. A ratzo so intense that it destroys the capacity for shov is,
paradoxically, spiritually counterproductive — the divine purpose is *habitation*
within the physical (dira betachtonim), and that requires the physical to survive.

The practical lesson: deep prayer and mystical experience must always include a built-in
return — a moment of *shov* that brings the person back to embodied, functional
existence with their divine experience integrated, not consumed.
