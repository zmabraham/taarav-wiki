# The Balance of Ratzo and Shov

## Neither Alone Is Sufficient

The deepest teaching about *ratzo v'shov* is not about either movement in isolation but about their irreducible interdependence. A spiritual life of pure *ratzo* — unrelenting ascent, yearning without grounding — consumes itself. The soul that only surges upward becomes estranged from its mission in the world, and its ecstasy, however genuine, fails to accomplish what G-d actually desires: the elevation of matter through embodied action. Conversely, a life of pure *shov* — duty without longing, action without the fire of transcendent yearning — becomes mechanical and hollow. The *mitzvot* become rote, the Torah study lifeless, the prayer perfunctory. It is the *ratzo* that charges the *shov* with meaning, and the *shov* that gives the *ratzo* its purpose.

## The Candle-Flame Metaphor from Tanya

The *Tanya* (Chapter 19) offers the foundational metaphor: a candle flame. The flame perpetually strains upward — this is its *ratzo*, its nature, its beauty. Yet if it were to detach entirely from the wick, it would die instantly. The wick anchors the flame and provides the fuel that sustains its upward surge. This is the *shov*: the tether that does not diminish the flame but enables it to burn at all. The healthy flame is neither the one that clings so tightly to the wick that it barely flickers, nor the one that leaps so high it separates from its source. The living flame is the one in perpetual, productive, beautiful tension between the two.

## The Breath Metaphor — Inhale and Exhale

A complementary metaphor, favored in Taarav teachings, is breath. The inhale (*ratzo*) draws the world inward, concentrates the self, takes in the oxygen of divine vitality. The exhale (*shov*) releases, expands, gives back — embodying in the outer world what was absorbed in the inner moment. No one breathes only in or only out. The rhythm itself is life. When either movement stops, existence stops. The healthy spiritual life is a continuous, natural alternation — not a crisis at each transition but a flow, a dance, a pulse.

## The Rashab's Synthesis in Taarav

What distinguishes the Rashab's treatment of *ratzo v'shov* in Hemshech Taarav from earlier formulations is his insistence that the synthesis is not a compromise between two opposing forces but a higher unity that contains both. He grounds this in the structure of divine emanation itself: even within *Atzilut* (the world of divine emanation), the *Sefirot* pulse in a *ratzo/shov* rhythm. This means the balance is not merely a human spiritual strategy — it mirrors the inner life of the divine process. The soul that achieves true *ratzo v'shov* is not managing a tension; it is resonating with the deepest rhythm of reality.

The Rashab further teaches that in the messianic era, the opposition between *ratzo* and *shov* will be resolved at a higher level — not by the elimination of either movement but by their full transparency to one another. Even now, the *tzaddik* who has internalized both movements experiences them not as alternating but as simultaneous: present in the world and present before G-d in the same moment.

## Key Hebrew Terms

- **אחדות** (*achdut*) — unity; the synthesis of *ratzo* and *shov* is a higher unity
- **תנועה** (*tenu'ah*) — movement, motion; the dynamic quality of the *ratzo/shov* rhythm
- **עצמות** (*atzmiut*) — essence; the deepest integration happens at the level of essential soul
- **שלמות** (*shleimut*) — wholeness/completion; neither movement alone is whole
