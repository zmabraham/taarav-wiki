# Yechida as Atzmus: The Soul's Deepest Identity

## English Explanation

The most radical claim the Rashab makes about Yechida in the Hemshech Taarav is
that Yechida is not merely *close to* the divine Atzmus (Essence) — it is, in
some meaningful sense, an expression of Atzmus itself within the soul. While all
five soul-levels are ultimately divine in origin, Yechida is specifically identified
with the Atzmus-level of divinity, making it categorically different from the
lower four soul-levels.

The Rashab uses the analogy of the relationship between the infinite and the finite:
the four lower soul-levels (Nefesh, Ruach, Neshamah, Chaya) are all, in different
degrees, *finite* expressions of the divine — they are created entities that receive
divine light. Yechida, however, participates in the *infinite* quality of the divine
— it is the point in the soul that is not truly "created" in the way the lower
soul-levels are, but rather is an *extension* of the divine Atzmus into the human.

This means that at the Yechida level, the soul has a quality that is beyond change,
beyond sin, and beyond exile. The lower soul-levels can be affected by the
person's actions, choices, and circumstances — they can be elevated or degraded.
Yechida is immune to this: it remains pure Atzmus regardless of what the person
does at the lower levels.

This teaching has profound implications for understanding the Jewish soul's
relationship to God: at the Yechida level, there is no separation. The separateness
that characterizes the soul's relationship to God at lower levels is overcome at
the Yechida level, where the soul *is* divine Atzmus in its particularized form.
