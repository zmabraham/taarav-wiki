# Portion and Unity: How the Part Relates to the Whole

## English Explanation

The concept of *chelek elokai mimal* raises a profound paradox in the Hemshech
Taarav: if the soul is truly a portion of the One God, how can there be many souls
without violating divine unity? In a physical system, a "portion" that is divided
from the whole makes the whole smaller. But God is infinite and indivisible — how
can there be portions of the Infinite?

The Rashab resolves this through a careful analysis of how *yechidut* (singularity/
unity) operates at the level of the Infinite. The Infinite, by definition, cannot be
reduced by the emergence of portions from it — the whole remains whole. The portions
are not pieces cut away from the Infinite but *expressions* of the Infinite within
a domain of apparent multiplicity.

The analogy the Rashab employs is that of a thought in the mind. A thought is,
in a sense, a "portion" of the mind — it emerges from the mind's substance — but
it does not diminish the mind by being thought. The mind's capacity to generate
thoughts is itself infinite; each thought is a full expression of the mind's
capacity without depleting it.

In this framework, each Jewish soul is a unique expression of the divine being —
not a fragment that leaves the divine whole incomplete, but a full divine expression
in a particular individuated form. This is why the Rashab can say simultaneously
that there is absolute divine unity (אַחְדּוּת ה', achdut Hashem) and genuine
multiplicity of souls — the unity is not threatened by the expression of multiple
portions.

## Hebrew Key Terms

| Term | Transliteration | Definition |
|------|-----------------|------------|
| אַחְדּוּת | Achdut | Unity; divine unity not diminished by portions |
| הִתְגַּלּוּת | Hitgalut | Expression; how portions emerge without diminishing |
| פְּרַט | Prat | Individual; each soul as a unique divine expression |
| כְּלָל | Klal | The whole; undiminished despite individual expressions |
| מַחֲשָׁבָה | Machshava | Thought; metaphor for how portions emerge from unity |
