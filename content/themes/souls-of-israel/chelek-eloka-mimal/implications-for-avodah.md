# Implications of Chelek Elokai for Spiritual Practice

## English Explanation

The teaching that the soul is literally *chelek elokai mimal* (a portion of God)
has profound implications for how the Rashab understands divine service (*avodah*,
עֲבוֹדָה) in the Hemshech Taarav. If the soul is truly a portion of the Infinite,
then the soul's natural state is divine — its "homesickness" for God is not a
metaphor but an accurate description of what a part feels when separated from
its whole.

This means that the motivation for divine service need not be externally imposed.
The soul does not serve God because it is commanded to (though it is) or because
it fears punishment (though there are consequences to sin). At the deepest level,
the soul serves God because this is its *nature* — a portion of God naturally
tends toward reunion with God, just as a flame naturally tends upward toward its
source.

The Rashab uses this teaching to explain the origins of *ahavat Hashem* (אַהֲבַת
ה', love of God). This love is not something manufactured through meditation
practice — it is the soul's natural response when it becomes aware of its own
identity as a chelek elokai. When a person truly knows what they are (a portion
of the Infinite), they naturally love the Infinite from which they come.

This also transforms the experience of doing mitzvot: they are not external
obligations but expressions of the soul's deepest nature. The soul that performs
a mitzvah is not obeying a foreign law but acting in accordance with its own
divine essence.

## Hebrew Key Terms

| Term | Transliteration | Definition |
|------|-----------------|------------|
| עֲבוֹדָה | Avodah | Divine service; transformed by chelek elokai |
| אַהֲבַת ה' | Ahavat Hashem | Love of God; the soul's natural response |
| טֶבַע | Teva | Nature; divine service as the soul's natural state |
| חֲזָרָה | Chazara | Return; the soul's drive toward its source |
| מִצְווֹת | Mitzvot | Commandments; expressions of the soul's divine nature |
