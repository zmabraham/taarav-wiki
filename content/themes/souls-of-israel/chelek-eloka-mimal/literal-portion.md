# A Literal Portion: Understanding Chelek Elokai Mimal

## English Explanation

The phrase *chelek elokai mimal* (חֵלֶק אֱלוֹקַּ מִמַּעַל, "a portion of God from
above") appears in Job 31:2 and is interpreted in the Tanya and Hemshech Taarav as
a description of the divine soul. The Rashab emphasizes, following the Alter Rebbe,
that this phrase is to be understood *literally* (כַּפְּשׁוּטוֹ, kaf'peshuto) — the
Jewish soul is not merely *like* a divine portion, not merely *inspired by* the
divine, but is *actually* a portion of the infinite divine being.

This radical claim — that the human soul is literally a part of God — is theologically
significant and spiritually transformative. It means the relationship between the soul
and God is not merely a relationship between a creation and its Creator (which would
be an infinite difference) but something more intimate: the relationship between a
part and the whole, a ray and its source, a flame and the fire.

The Rashab develops this through an extensive analysis in the Hemshech Taarav of
what "portion" (*chelek*) can mean when applied to the Infinite. A portion of a
physical thing is a piece that becomes separate from the whole. A "portion" of the
Infinite, however, cannot become truly separate — the Infinite has no true parts in
the usual sense. The divine soul is a chelek in the sense of a specific *revelation*
or *expression* of the divine being — not separated but *expressed* in an individuated
form.
