# מלכי אדום — Kings of Edom

**The Scriptural Basis for the World of Tohu in Hemshech Taarav**

---

## Overview

The Kabbalistic framework of the World of Tohu — including the concept of its "rulers" who "reigned and died" — is derived from a striking passage in *Bereishit* (Genesis 36:31-39), which lists the kings of Edom who ruled before any king reigned over Israel. The Ari z"l identified this genealogy as the scriptural encoding of the cosmic events in the World of Tohu, and the Rashab in Hemshech Taarav develops this interpretation with considerable depth.

---

## The Scriptural Passage

Genesis 36 lists eight kings of Edom who reigned sequentially: "And these are the kings who reigned in the land of Edom before any king reigned over the children of Israel." Each king is said to have "reigned" and then is followed by his death, replaced by the next king. The passage concludes with the last king, Hadar (or Hadad), for whom no death is recorded.

The Ari z"l's interpretation: This passage is not merely historical — it encodes the cosmic drama of the Sefirot of Tohu. The "kings" are the Sefirot (specifically the seven lower Sefirot from Chesed through Malchut), who "reigned" — existed — and "died" — shattered — in the Shevirat HaKelim.

---

## The Rashab's Development in Taarav

The Rashab does not merely cite this interpretation — he draws out its conceptual implications:

**1. Why "kings"?**
In Kabbalah, "kingdom" (*malchut*) implies absolute, unrestricted rulership — the quality of each sefirah expressing itself without any mediation or limitation from the other sefirot. This is precisely the characteristic of the Tohu-Sefirot: each was a "king" unto itself, ruling its domain absolutely, without subordination to or integration with the others. The metaphor of kingship perfectly captures the isolated sovereignty of each Tohu-sefirah.

**2. Why "Edom"?**
Edom (*אדום* — red) is associated with *Gevurah* (strength/severity) — the quality of boundaries, separation, and intensity. The Rashab connects Edom's redness to the *din* (judgment/severity) that characterized the Tohu world. Tohu's lights were "strong" and "red" — intense forces of divine energy without the moderating quality of love (*Chesed*) that would characterize Tikkun.

**3. Why Edom "before Israel"?**
The verse says these kings ruled "before any king reigned over the children of Israel." In the Rashab's reading: Tohu (*Edom*) preceded Tikkun (*Israel*). The history of the spiritual worlds has a sequence — chaos precedes order, intensity precedes integration, Tohu precedes Tikkun — just as wildness precedes cultivation in the development of a field.

**4. The Eighth King — Hadar/Hadad**
The eighth king, for whom no death is recorded, represents the aspect of Tohu that survived the breaking and became the foundation of Tikkun. In some analyses, this is associated with the sefirah of Malchut (Malchut being the eighth counting from Keter through the Middot). Malchut's vessel, though severely damaged, was not completely destroyed — it "survived" and became the seed of the world of Tikkun.

---

## Edom and Esau in Chassidic Teaching

The Rashab also connects the Kings of Edom to the figure of Esau (*Eisav*) — who is the progenitor of Edom in the Torah narrative. In Chassidic psychology, Esau represents the *Nefesh HaBehamit* (animal soul) — powerful, impulsive, and capable of great intensity but lacking integration with the divine. This connection allows the Rashab to apply the Tohu framework to personal spiritual psychology: the animal soul, with its intense drives and lack of internal integration, is itself a reflection of the Tohu pattern.
