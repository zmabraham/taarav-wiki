# Tohu as Divine Excess: The Paradox of Too Much

## English Explanation

The Rashab introduces a paradox at the heart of the World of Tohu: it failed not
because of deficiency but because of *excess*. The lights of Tohu were too great,
too pure, too intense. This is a radical inversion of ordinary thinking about
spiritual failure — we normally assume that problems arise from a lack of holiness.
Tohu reveals that even an overwhelming excess of divine light can produce catastrophic
results when not properly channeled.

This paradox is central to the Hemshech Taarav's understanding of why the current
world order (Tikkun) is more spiritually sophisticated than the original Tohu. Tikkun
represents not a "full" divine light replacing a "deficient" one, but a more nuanced,
modulated system that can actually sustain divine presence within finite structures.
In this sense, Tikkun involves a kind of divine self-limitation (just as Tzimtzum was
a divine self-limitation) applied at a structural level.

The Rashab uses this insight to address a recurring challenge in mystical thought: if
Tohu was more directly connected to the divine light, why is Tikkun preferred? The
answer is that the divine purpose was never maximum divine intensity — it was *habitation*
(דִּירָה, dira) within the finite. Tohu's excess made habitation impossible. Tikkun's
modulation made it possible.

There is also a spiritual practice dimension: the person who seeks maximum divine
intensity at the expense of all practical grounding is spiritually in Tohu-mode. The
Rashab consistently promotes balanced engagement: not the maximum of any one quality
but the productive cooperation of all qualities in service of God.

|------|-----------------|------------|

| שִׁקּוּל | Shikul | Balance; what Tikkun achieves |
