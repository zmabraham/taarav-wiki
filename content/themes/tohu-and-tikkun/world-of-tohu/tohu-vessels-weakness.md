# The Weakness of Vessels in the World of Tohu

## English Explanation

If the World of Tohu's lights were characterized by overwhelming intensity, its
vessels were correspondingly characterized by fundamental weakness (חֻלְשָׁה, chusha).
In the Hemshech Taarav, the Rashab explains that the weakness of Tohu's vessels was
structural, not accidental: the very design of the World of Tohu positioned them to
receive light at a level they could not sustain.

The vessels of Tohu were each designed to hold one pure, undiluted divine quality.
They were narrow in their focus — each vessel held the light of one Sefirah alone,
with no capacity to blend or moderate it with other qualities. This narrowness was
their weakness: they had no resilience, no ability to adapt to changes in the light's
intensity, no recourse to complementary divine qualities when their own quality became
too intense.

The Rashab compares the Tohu vessels to brittle glass: extraordinarily pure and
clear, but shattering precisely because of their rigidity. A more flexible vessel —
one that can bend without breaking — is less pure but more durable. The vessels of
Tikkun (the successor world) are explicitly designed with this kind of resilience,
each Sefirah able to receive from and give to the others in a cooperative structure.

Understanding the weakness of Tohu's vessels is important for understanding human
spiritual life. A person who has one extraordinary virtue developed in isolation —
overwhelming intellectual brilliance, or overwhelming emotional intensity — but
without the moderating influence of other qualities, is spiritually in a Tohu-like
configuration: bright but brittle.
