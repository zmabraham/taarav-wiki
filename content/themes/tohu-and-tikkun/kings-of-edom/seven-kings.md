# The Seven Kings of Edom

## English Explanation

The "Seven Kings of Edom" is a Kabbalistic reading of a passage in Genesis (36:31-39)
that lists the kings who ruled in Edom before there was a king in Israel. In the
Hemshech Taarav, the Rashab follows the Zoharic and Lurianic interpretation that sees
these seven kings as representing the seven lower Sefirot (Chessed through Malchut) in
the World of Tohu — the pre-Tikkun divine configurations that "died" (i.e., shattered)
without leaving an heir.

The seven kings are: Bela, Yovav, Chusham, Hadad (twice listed), Samlah, Shaul, and
Ba'al Chanan. In the Kabbalistic reading, each king corresponds to one of the lower
seven Sefirot: Bela = Chessed, Yovav = Gevurah, Chusham = Tiferet, and so on. The
fact that each "died" without a son represents the failure of Tohu's vessels to
transmit or pass on their divine content — each Sefirah in Tohu existed in isolation
and could not propagate its quality into a next generation (i.e., into lower Sefirot).

The Rashab emphasizes that the language "died without a son" (*vayamot, v'ein lo ben*)
is a technical description of the failure of Tohu: not biological death but the
inability to *generate* lower structures. A Sefirah that "has no son" cannot transmit
its qualities to lower Sefirot and thus cannot participate in the chain of causation
that builds a stable world.
