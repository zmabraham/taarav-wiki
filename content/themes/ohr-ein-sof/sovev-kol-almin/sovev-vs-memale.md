# Sovev versus Memale: Two Modes of Divine Presence

## English Explanation

The distinction between *Sovev Kol Almin* (surrounds all worlds) and *Memale Kol
Almin* (fills all worlds) is one of the foundational distinctions in the Hemshech
Taarav's theological framework. These two modes of divine presence correspond to two
fundamental ways the Infinite relates to finite creation: from outside (Sovev) and
from within (Memale).

Sovev is transcendent: it does not enter the worlds but encompasses them from a
perspective that is above all differentiation. Memale is immanent: it enters into
each world and each creature, animating them from within according to their specific
nature and capacity. Sovev illuminates equally; Memale illuminates differentially —
each created being receives Memale according to its particular spiritual structure.

The Rashab emphasizes that both modes are simultaneously present and necessary. A
world sustained only by Sovev would have no inner life — everything would be equally
illuminated from outside but nothing would have genuine interiority. A world sustained
only by Memale would have no transcendent anchor — everything would be internally
animated but cut off from the infinite divine ground.

The human experience parallels this: Sovev-awareness produces the transcendent
experiences of prayer and meditation — the sense of being "beyond" ordinary self.
Memale-awareness produces the experience of finding divine presence in the details of
ordinary life — in food, in nature, in relationships. The Rashab's ideal is a life
that cultivates both: transcendent awe and immanent love simultaneously.

## Hebrew Key Terms

| Term | Transliteration | Definition |
|------|-----------------|------------|
| מְמַלֵּא כָּל עָלְמִין | Memale Kol Almin | Fills all worlds; immanent divine presence |
| סוֹבֵב כָּל עָלְמִין | Sovev Kol Almin | Surrounds all worlds; transcendent divine presence |
| פְּנִימִיּוּת | Pnimiyut | Interiority; what Memale produces |
| מַקִּיף | Mekif | Encompassing; what Sovev produces |
| אַהֲבָה וְיִרְאָה | Ahavah v'Yira | Love and awe; the experiential poles of Memale and Sovev |
