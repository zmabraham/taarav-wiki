# ספירות חב"ד — The ChaBaD Sefirot

**Chochma, Bina, and Da'at in Hemshech Taarav**

---

## Overview

The **ChaBaD** sefirot — **Ch**ochma (חכמה — Wisdom), **B**ina (בינה — Understanding), and **D**a'at (דעת — Knowledge) — are the intellectual triad that forms the "brain" of the divine structure and the primary focus of Chabad thought. In Hemshech Taarav, the Rashab provides the most detailed and philosophically nuanced analysis of ChaBaD in Chabad literature, revealing the inner logic of each sefirah and the profound significance of their interrelationship.

---

## Chochma — The First Point

**Chochma** (חכמה — Wisdom) is the first *differentiated* emanation from Keter (or from the Ein Sof in the view that Keter is really above the Sefirot). The Rashab in the Taarav describes Chochma as:

**The "nekudah rishonah" (first point)**: Chochma is the initial contraction of infinite divine thought into a specific, identifiable "idea." Before Chochma, there is only the undifferentiated divine mind (Keter/Ayin — nothingness). With Chochma, there is the *first* something — the primordial point of divine wisdom.

**The quality of bitul (self-nullification)**: Chochma's defining spiritual quality is *bitul* — the sefirah closest to the "nothingness" of Keter, Chochma retains the greatest degree of self-nullification. Chochma does not assert itself; it simply flashes with divine insight and immediately passes it to Bina.

**The "flash" of insight**: Chochma corresponds to the sudden, pre-verbal insight — the *aha moment* — before it is developed into articulated thought. The Rashab uses this to ground the Chabad practice of meditating on *yesh me'ayin* (the emergence of something from nothingness) — the experiential Chochma.

---

## Bina — The Womb of Understanding

**Bina** (בינה — Understanding) is the sefirah that receives the point of Chochma and develops it into fully articulated understanding. The Rashab's key analyses:

**The "palace" that receives the "point"**: In the Rashab's imagery, Chochma is the "king" (masculine, initiating) and Bina is the "palace" (feminine, receiving and developing). Bina receives Chochma's point and develops it into the full understanding — all the distinctions, implications, and applications of the initial insight.

**The river analogy**: Chochma is the underground spring; Bina is the river that flows from it. The spring is hidden and concentrated; the river is visible, distributed, and flowing — Bina's understanding is accessible and expressible in a way Chochma's flash is not.

**Bina and teshuvah**: The Rashab connects Bina to *teshuvah* (return) — Bina's characteristic quality is the capacity to fully process an experience and draw it back toward its source. Understanding (Bina) of one's distance from God leads naturally to the desire to return (*teshuvah*). This is why Bina is called in the Zohar the "great teshuvah."

---

## Da'at — Internalized Knowledge

**Da'at** (דעת — Knowledge/Intimacy) is the third of the ChaBaD sefirot — but it occupies a unique position. In some enumerations, Da'at replaces Keter (since Keter is above the standard ten) in the counting. The Rashab's distinctive analysis:

**Da'at as the "connection" between Chochma and Bina**: Da'at is what enables the *union* of Chochma and Bina — the integration of the initial insight with the developed understanding. Without Da'at, Chochma and Bina would remain separate; with Da'at, their product is integrated into a single, cohesive understanding.

**Da'at as internalization**: While Chochma provides the insight and Bina develops it, Da'at is the process of *making it one's own* — of the understanding becoming not just intellectually grasped but emotionally real and personally significant. The Rashab emphasizes that Da'at is the bridge from intellect to emotion — a concept that reaches Da'at level generates the emotional responses of love (Chesed) and fear (Gevurah) naturally.

---

## The Practical Significance: The ChaBaD Path of Avodah

The Rashab's analysis of ChaBaD is directly connected to the Chabad path of divine service: *avodah through da'at* — understanding the divine deeply enough (Chochma and Bina) that this understanding becomes personally internalized (Da'at) and naturally generates the appropriate emotional responses (love and fear of God). This is the distinctive Chabad approach as opposed to avodah that begins with the emotional middot directly.
