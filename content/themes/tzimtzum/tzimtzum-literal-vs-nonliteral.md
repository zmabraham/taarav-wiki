# צמצום כפשוטו ושלא כפשוטו — Literal vs. Non-Literal Tzimtzum

**The Great Debate and the Rashab's Resolution in Hemshech Taarav**

---

## Overview

One of the most significant controversies in Kabbalah and Chassidus concerns whether the Tzimtzum should be understood *literally* (כפשוטו — *kipshuto*) or *non-literally* (שלא כפשוטו — *shelo kipshuto*). The Hemshech Taarav engages this debate deeply, and the Rashab's position represents the mature Chabad resolution — one that is philosophically more sophisticated than either extreme.

This discussion is particularly relevant in the context of Chabad's insistence on divine immanence (*Elokus everywhere*) versus the apparent implication of the Tzimtzum that there is a space "empty" of God.

---

## The Literal Position

Some early Kabbalists (certain followers of the Ari z"l, and later the *Mitnagdim* who criticized Chassidus) understood the Tzimtzum as describing a genuine, albeit metaphysical, withdrawal — God's light actually *left* a region, creating a space that is in some sense truly empty of the direct divine presence.

**Implications of the literal view**:
- God is not equally present everywhere — there is a "here" (where God is) and a "there" (the chalal, where God has withdrawn)
- This view preserves divine transcendence but risks implying that creation exists in a space genuinely separate from God
- The Vilna Gaon's school sometimes emphasized this reading to distinguish the Kabbalistic framework from Chassidic "pantheism"

---

## The Non-Literal Position (Chabad's Position)

The Baal Shem Tov and the Alter Rebbe (in Tanya) explicitly taught that **the Tzimtzum is not to be understood literally**. God did not "actually" withdraw — the divine essence (Atzmut) remains fully present everywhere, even within the chalal. What "withdrew" was the *revelation* of Or Ein Sof — its manifest radiance — not the Atzmut itself.

**Implications of the non-literal view**:
- *Kol melo ha'aretz kevodo* ("The whole earth is full of His glory") — God is genuinely present in all of creation
- The chalal is not empty of God but empty of *revealed* divine light
- This view enables Chassidic *Achdus Hashem* (divine unity) — there is truly no space empty of God

---

## The Rashab's Advance in Taarav

The Rashab in Hemshech Taarav goes beyond simply restating the non-literal position. His contribution is a more precise analysis of *what* was withdrawn in the Tzimtzum:

**The distinction between Or Ein Sof and Atzmut Ein Sof**:
- *Atzmut* (divine essence) — Never withdraws, never changes, completely transcends all relationship to creation. The Tzimtzum says nothing about the Atzmut.
- *Or Ein Sof* (divine light/radiance) — This is what the Tzimtzum affects. Specifically, the revealed, manifest aspect of the Or Ein Sof withdrew; the Or Ein Sof's most essential dimension (its connection to Atzmut) remains.

This means the Tzimtzum is not about God vs. non-God but about two aspects of Or Ein Sof: that which can be revealed in a finite framework, and that which cannot. The Tzimtzum is the withdrawal of the "revealable" aspect, creating space for finite creation — while the "unrevelable" aspect (Atzmut) continues unaffected.

**The practical implication**:
Even within the chalal, within the created worlds, within physical matter — the divine Atzmut is present. Nothing has independent existence apart from God. But this presence is *concealed* — it does not manifest as overwhelming, obliterating light. Hence the chalal is a space of divine concealment, not divine absence.

---

## Why This Matters

The resolution of this debate has profound implications for:

- **Avodah**: If God is genuinely absent from the physical world, the goal of spiritual work is *escape* from the world. If God is present-but-concealed, the goal is *revelation* — revealing the hidden divine presence within the world itself.

- **Tikkun Olam**: The Rashab's non-literal Tzimtzum grounds the Chabad emphasis on physical mitzvot — since the physical world is not empty of God, engaging with it through Torah and mitzvot is genuinely meaningful, not merely a concession to the body.

- **The soul's mission**: The soul descends into the world not to flee it but to reveal the Atzmut that is hidden within it.
