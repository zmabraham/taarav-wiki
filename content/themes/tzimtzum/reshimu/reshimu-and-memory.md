# Reshimu and Divine Memory

## English Explanation

One of the Rashab's most evocative descriptions of the Reshimu in the Hemshech
Taarav is its function as divine "memory" — the way the period *before* the Tzimtzum
continues to be "remembered" and present even after it. Just as a person who
experiences something profound retains traces of that experience even when the moment
has passed, the Makom HaPanui retains traces of the infinite divine light that once
filled it.

This "memory" function is not passive. The reshimu actively shapes what can exist
within the vacated space. Because the reshimu remembers, so to speak, the quality of
Or Ein Sof, anything that comes into existence within the Makom HaPanui carries that
quality in a latent, compressed form. Creation is not made from nothing in an
absolute sense — it is made from the divine memory embedded in the reshimu.

The Rashab uses this concept to address a profound question: how can finite creation
have any relationship with the infinite divine? The answer is that creation never
fully loses its connection to the divine source. The reshimu — the divine trace within
creation — is what makes *teshuvah* (return) possible. When a person does teshuvah,
they are not creating a new relationship with God; they are *remembering* and
re-activating the divine trace (reshimu) that was always present within them.

This also explains why the Rashab says that the souls of Israel have an unbreakable
connection to Ein Sof — even when a Jew is in the deepest spiritual exile, the
reshimu within their soul retains the "memory" of the divine.

|------|-----------------|------------|
| זִכָּרוֹן | Zikaron | Memory; the reshimu as divine trace |

| גָּלוּת | Galut | Exile; does not erase the reshimu |
| קֶשֶׁר | Kesher | Connection; what the reshimu maintains |
| נְקוּדָה | Nekudah | Point; the compressed form of the reshimu within the soul |
