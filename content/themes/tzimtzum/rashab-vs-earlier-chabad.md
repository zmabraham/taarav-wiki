# הרש"ב לעומת חסידות חב"ד הקדומה — Rashab vs. Earlier Chabad on Tzimtzum

**How Hemshech Taarav Advances Beyond the Alter Rebbe's Treatment**

---

## Overview

The Hemshech Taarav represents a significant advance in the Chabad treatment of Tzimtzum beyond what the Alter Rebbe (Rabbi Shneur Zalman of Liadi) presented in the Tanya and in his other writings. The Rashab (Rabbi Shalom DovBer Schneersohn) does not contradict his great-great-grandfather's approach but deepens, systematizes, and extends it in ways that constitute qualitatively new contributions to Chabad thought.

Understanding these advances helps locate the Taarav's place in the chain of Chabad transmission and reveals the unique contribution the Rashab was making in these maamarim.

---

## The Alter Rebbe's Position (Tanya, Sha'ar HaYichud VeHaEmunah)

The Alter Rebbe's foundational teaching on Tzimtzum (primarily in *Sha'ar HaYichud VeHaEmunah*, Tanya chapters 1-12) establishes:

1. The Tzimtzum is *not* literal — God's presence fills all worlds
2. *Sovev kol almin* and *memaleh kol almin* are two aspects of divine presence
3. The world's apparent independent existence (*yesh*) is an illusion — from God's perspective, all is unified
4. Divine unity (*Achdus Hashem*) means there is literally nothing beside God

This is profound, but the Alter Rebbe's treatment is primarily concerned with the *conclusion* (divine immanence) rather than with the mechanism — the precise *how* of the Tzimtzum and its relationship to the Reshimu and Kav.

---

## The Mitteler Rebbe and Tzemach Tzedek

The Mitteler Rebbe (Rabbi DovBer of Lubavitch) and Tzemach Tzedek (Rabbi Menachem Mendel of Lubavitch) began to develop more detailed analyses of the post-Tzimtzum structure — the Reshimu, the Kav, the relationship between the worlds. But their treatments remained within a framework that was primarily *descriptive* (laying out the structure) rather than *analytical* (explaining the underlying logic of each stage).

---

## The Rashab's Advances in Hemshech Taarav

**1. The Tzimtzum as the condition for Or Chozer**
Earlier Chabad literature treated the Tzimtzum primarily as the necessary condition for finite worlds to exist. The Rashab reframes this: the *ultimate* purpose of the Tzimtzum is not merely to create finite worlds but specifically to create the condition for *Or Chozer* — the upward response from below. This teleological reframing gives the Tzimtzum a positive purpose it did not have in earlier treatments.

**2. Precise metaphysics of Atzmut vs. Or**
The Rashab is far more precise than earlier Chabad authorities in distinguishing between *Atzmut Ein Sof* (divine essence — utterly transcendent, never involved in Tzimtzum) and *Or Ein Sof* (divine radiance — what the Tzimtzum affected). Earlier treatments sometimes blurred this distinction in ways the Rashab found insufficient.

**3. Multiple levels of Tzimtzum**
The Alter Rebbe's Tanya discusses primarily the primordial Tzimtzum. The Rashab in the Taarav develops the idea of *multiple levels of tzimtzum* — each transition between worlds involves a further tzimtzum; each sefirah involves an internal tzimtzum. This fractal understanding of contraction is new in Chabad literature.

**4. Tzimtzum and the Nefesh HaBehamit**
The Rashab develops the idea that the human psychology of the animal soul is itself a form of "tzimtzum" — the divine vitality that animates even the animal soul is present but concealed. This application of the Tzimtzum concept to human psychology is more developed in the Taarav than in earlier Chabad sources.

---

## Why the Rashab Developed These Advances

The Rashab was responding to intellectual challenges of his era — the early 20th century brought sophisticated philosophical critiques of theism, and the Rashab's community faced unprecedented pressures from assimilation and secularization. The Taarav represents in part an attempt to give the most rigorous possible account of Chabad theology — one that could withstand philosophical scrutiny while remaining authentically grounded in the Kabbalistic tradition.
