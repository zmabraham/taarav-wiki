# מקום הפנוי — The Empty Space After Tzimtzum

**The Chalal (Empty Space) and Its Significance in Hemshech Taarav**

---

## Overview

The *Makom HaPanui* (מקום הפנוי — "the empty place") or *Chalal* (חלל — void, empty space) is the "space" created by the Tzimtzum — the conceptual arena within which all finite worlds come into being. In Hemshech Taarav, the Rashab provides a nuanced analysis of the chalal that goes beyond the standard Lurianic description, clarifying both what the chalal is and what it is not.

---

## The Nature of the Chalal

The chalal is a conceptual rather than physical space. The Rashab is careful to distinguish:

**What the chalal is**: A mode of divine self-limitation — the domain within which the Or Ein Sof does not manifest in its overwhelming fullness. Within the chalal, the divine presence is present (through the Reshimu and later the Kav) but in a contained, limited form that allows finite beings to exist.

**What the chalal is not**: An absolute void, devoid of divine presence. The Rashab emphasizes that even within the chalal, the divine essence (Atzmut) is not absent — only its revealed radiance (Or Ein Sof) is contracted. This is analogous to a teacher who withholds the full depth of their understanding when speaking to young students — their wisdom is present but not revealed at full intensity.

---

## The Chalal and the Possibility of Evil

The Rashab addresses a profound question: Does the chalal, as a space of divine withdrawal, become the source of evil? In the Taarav, the answer is nuanced:

The chalal is the *necessary precondition* for the possibility of evil — by definition, any space of divine concealment can become a space of *yesh* (independent selfhood) and therefore of *kelipah* (husk, evil force). But the chalal itself is not evil; it is neutral — the divine wisdom's deliberate self-limitation for the purpose of enabling creation.

Evil (*sitra achra*) arises when finite beings within the chalal assert their independence absolutely, denying the divine presence that sustains them. This misuse of the chalal's freedom is not the chalal's purpose but its danger.

---

## The Chalal's Dimensions

The Rashab in the Taarav describes the chalal as having dimensions that correspond to the structure of creation:

- The **upper** edge of the chalal (closest to the Or Ein Sof) corresponds to the world of Adam Kadmon
- The **inner** region of the chalal corresponds to the four worlds of Atzilut, Beriya, Yetzira, Asiya
- The **lower** edge of the chalal is our physical world

The *kav* traverses the chalal from its upper edge downward, carrying the Or Ein Sof's directive energy through all these levels.

---

## The Chalal and Human Experience

The Rashab draws a striking application: the human experience of *hester panim* (hiddenness of the divine face) — moments of spiritual darkness, doubt, or distance from God — is the experiential analogue of the chalal. In these moments, the person exists within a contracted divine space, much as all created beings exist within the chalal.

The response to *hester panim* in the Taarav's framework is not to deny the experience but to recognize it as the chalal's condition — a condition designed to enable the most profound Or Chozer when the person, from within the darkness, chooses to return to God.
