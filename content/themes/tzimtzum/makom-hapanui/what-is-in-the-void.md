# What Is in the Void: The Makom HaPanui's Contents

## English Explanation

The *Makom HaPanui* (מָקוֹם הַפָּנוּי, the vacated space) is, at first glance, an
absence: the divine light withdrew, leaving a void. But in the Hemshech Taarav, the
Rashab emphasizes that this void is far from empty. It contains the reshimu (residue),
and the reshimu itself is a compressed presence of what came before.

The Rashab draws on the Zoharic teaching that "there is no place empty of Him" (לֵית
אֲתַר פָּנוּי מִנֵּיהּ) to argue that even the Makom HaPanui retains the divine
Essence, even if not the divine *light*. The withdrawal of light does not mean the
withdrawal of Being. The Makom HaPanui thus contains: (1) the divine Essence, which
did not contract; (2) the reshimu, the trace of the contracted light; and (3) the
potential for all subsequent creation, encoded in the reshimu.

This analysis resolves an apparent paradox: if God is everywhere (omnipresent), how
can there be a "vacated" space? The Rashab's answer is that the Tzimtzum vacated the
*revelatory presence* (גִּילוּי, giluiy) of God, not God's essential being. The Makom
HaPanui is "vacated" in the sense that divine light is not *manifestly felt* there —
but it is not empty of divine reality.

This has profound implications for understanding the nature of the physical world:
the material realm, which corresponds to the lowest "depth" of the Makom HaPanui, is
precisely the domain in which divinity is most *concealed*, yet also most essentially
*present*.

## Hebrew Key Terms

| Term | Transliteration | Definition |
|------|-----------------|------------|
| מָקוֹם הַפָּנוּי | Makom HaPanui | Vacated space; result of Tzimtzum |
| גִּילוּי | Giluiy | Revelation; what was withdrawn by Tzimtzum |
| עַצְמוּת | Atzmus | Essence; what remains even in the void |
| רְשִׁימוּ | Reshimu | Trace; the encoded potential of creation |
| הֶעְלֵם | He'elem | Concealment; the condition of the Makom HaPanui |
