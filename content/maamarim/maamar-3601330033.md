## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Miketz, Shabbat Chanukah 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

This is one of the key conceptual building blocks of the hemshech, creating a chain of ideas that progresses from maamar to maamar.

## Deep Dive

### Opening Kushya

The maamar opens with the Talmudic question "מאי חנוכה" — on what miracle was Chanukah established? — and immediately layers on three distinct difficulties. Rashi interprets the question as asking why the holiday is called חנוכה specifically, implying the name itself demands explanation. Beyond that, the Rashab asks: (1) if the miracle of the oil was primary, why is the holiday not called חג הנרות? (2) why precisely eight days — what is the significance of that number? (3) why was the miracle of the military victory (which occurred on כ"ד כסלו) subordinated to the miracle of the lights (כ"ה כסלו)? These three questions drive the entire maamar.

### The Conceptual Argument

The maamar builds on material already established in the hemshech concerning the nature of חכמה (Chochmah) as likened to the color white (גוון לבן). The Rashab's core move is the analogy between white and Chochmah: white is not a composite color but a simple, essential color (גוון עצמי), and for precisely that reason — its simplicity and lack of mixture — all other colors appear in their true form upon it. All other colors are composite; white alone is pure. The parallel: Chochmah is undifferentiated, without any "color" of its own (אין בה גוון כלל), and because it is absolutely simple (פשוט מכל גוון), it is the vehicle through which all the sefirot appear as they truly are.

The Rashab then draws out two implications. First, in avodah: the foundation of all divine service is not intellectual comprehension or emotional arousal but rather "נקודת ההתקשרות וביטול העצמי" — the point of essential self-nullification and attachment to G-d. Just as white has no admixture and therefore reveals other colors purely, the point of bittul reveals all Torah and mitzvot in their true essence, without the distortion introduced when they are motivated by intellectual or emotional experience.

Second, the maamar analyzes שם הוי' as שם העצם — not because other divine names are additions to G-d's essence, but because שם הוי' is in the category of אור (light), which is a revelation of essence rather than a defined, bounded reality (כלים). Other names correspond to כלים — they have "color" and particularity. שם הוי' corresponds to the simple, white light of Chochmah.

### The Chiddush: חנו כה

The resolution of the three opening questions arrives through the etymology חנו כה — "they rested at כה." The particle כה corresponds to יחודא תתאה (lower unity), the level at which the world exists in apparent independent being yet is nullified before G-dliness. The Greeks sought to overwhelm this level with external wisdom (חכמות חיצוניות), symbolized by their defiling all the oil. The Hashmoneans' victory — achieved through מסירת נפש — drew down the level of עצמות אוא"ס that stands above the hishtalshelut entirely. The single cruse of oil sealed with the Kohen Gadol's seal (חותמו של כה"ג, corresponding to אריך אנפין) was intact because it carried this transcendent quality.

The eight days correspond to שמונה פעמים הוי' — the Name of G-d at the level above the seven sefirot of the seder hishtalshelut. Seven lights represent the seven-times Havayah of the hishtalshelut; the eighth represents שם הוי' דלעילא, the Name that transcends the chain of worlds. The Hashmoneans drew this level into מלכות itself (the כה), and that is why the holiday of lights takes precedence over the holiday of military victory: the true victory was not tactical but ontological — a revelation of divine essence in the lowest vessel.
