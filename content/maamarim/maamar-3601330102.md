## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Mishpatim, Shabbat Shekalim 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar develops the hemshech's account of how Shabbat Shekalim — the commandment that every Israelite give a half-shekel — encodes a specific ontological structure: that the divine light descending into the world (Or Yashar) and the soul's responsive ascent (Or Chozer) are not symmetrical, but constitute two distinct grades of attachment whose unity requires a third term above both. The half-shekel, precisely because it is a partial rather than a whole, becomes the vehicle for exploring how incompleteness below generates completeness above.

### The Opening Move

The Rashab anchors the maamar in the paradox of Shabbat Shekalim falling within Parshat Mishpatim: a portion dominated by civil law (damages, servants, property) suddenly receives the overlay of the Shekalim reading, which concerns communal atonement and the census. Why should the legal-rational world of mishpatim require the half-shekel — a contribution that is neither purely rational (it is fixed, not proportional to wealth) nor purely supra-rational (it is obligatory, not voluntary)? The question forces the inquiry into the relationship between chok (supra-rational divine decree) and mishpat (rational law) as correlates of Or Yashar and Or Chozer respectively.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The structural asymmetry of Or Yashar and Or Chozer**: Or Yashar descends through graduated constriction (tzimtzum, kav, reshimu) and arrives in the lower world already contracted; Or Chozer, by contrast, ascends from the lowest point — Malkhut of Asiyah — and by rising through each level actually amplifies its luminosity. The two lights move in opposite directions and have inverse profiles of intensity.
- **Mishpatim as the domain of Or Yashar**: Rational law corresponds to the direct light because it operates through intelligible causality — the light descends through the intellect (Chokhmah-Binah-Da'at) and is received in the emotive faculties (Chesed-Gevurah-Tiferet) as comprehensible norms. The socio-legal order of mishpatim is thus the imprint of Or Yashar in the domain of human relations.
- **The half-shekel as Or Chozer**: The shekel is half, never whole, because the reflected light is by nature incomplete on its own — it requires union with its source. Every Israelite gives equally because Or Chozer, being the soul's root-ascent (bechinas ratzo), is the same in rich and poor; what differs is only the Or Yashar received. The mandatory nature of the shekel (not voluntary) reflects that Or Chozer is not an achievement but a structural given of the soul's constitution.
- **The conjunction of Shekalim with Mishpatim**: The rabbinic institution of reading Parshat Shekalim precisely when civil-rational law fills the Torah portion is not incidental. It signals that rational governance (the mishpat-world) is sustained only by the periodic infusion of Or Chozer — the communal half-shekel — which draws a beam from Keter back into the material structure. Without this, the rational order calcifies into mere human convention.
- **The Keter-level resolution**: The half-shekel's destination is the Mishkan/Temple treasury — the locus of divine dwelling (dira betachtonim). The contribution converts from Or Chozer (ascending) into the material substrate of the Mishkan (receiving Or Yashar), thereby modeling the complete cycle: Or Yashar descends into law, Or Chozer ascends through the half-shekel offering, and the two reunite in the sacred structure of the Mishkan.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that the Torah's legal-rational order and its supra-rational cultic order are not two separate domains but the downward and upward faces of a single divine light. Mishpatim represents Or Yashar's most articulate descent into human reason; the half-shekel represents Or Chozer's most egalitarian ascent from the collective soul. Their conjunction on Shabbat Shekalim enacts precisely what the hemshech has been building toward: the demonstration that dira betachtonim — G-d's dwelling below — requires both vectors simultaneously.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Within the second volume of Taarav, this maamar consolidates the Or Yashar/Or Chozer duality by grounding it in a halakhic institution (Shekalim) rather than in pure cosmological description. The move is characteristic of the Rashab's method: after extensive metaphysical elaboration, he re-anchors the abstract structure in a concrete Torah commandment, showing that the ontology was always already latent in normative Jewish practice. This prepares the ground for subsequent maamarim to examine how specific mitzvot — particularly those associated with the Mishkan — instantiate the divine light's full circuit.
