## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Mishpatim, Shabbat Shekalim 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### Opening Question (Kushya)

The maamar opens with three interlocking difficulties on Shemot 30:13: "זֶה יִתְּנוּ כָּל הָעֹבֵר עַל הַפְּקֻדִים מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל... עֶשְׂרִים גֵּרָה הַשֶּׁקֶל מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל תְּרוּמָה לַה'." First: why "זֶה יִתְּנוּ" (this they shall give) rather than "וְנָתְנוּ" (and they shall give)? Second: why command a "מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל" before revealing what a shekel is — no one yet knows half of what, so why not simply say "ten gera"? Third: once the verse defines the shekel as twenty gera, why repeat "מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל תְּרוּמָה לַה'" — has it not already been stated?

### The Conceptual Build

To answer, the Rashab draws on the hemshech's running distinction between כֵּלִים (vessels) and לְבוּשִׁים (garments). Vessels receive and unify with the light, shaping its revealed form; garments remain separate from the essence and conceal even as they transmit. The maamar then introduces a crucial refinement: אוֹתִיּוֹת עַצְמִיִּים — essential letters intrinsic to the light, like letters engraved into the body of a precious stone — versus letters separable and exchangeable like an impression in wax. The text states: "בְּנֻקְדַּת הַשְׂכָּלָה הָרֵי אוֹתִיּוֹת אֵלּוּ אֵין אֶפְשָׁר לְהַחֲלִיפָם" — at the flash-point of intellectual illumination the letters cannot be exchanged without diminishing the light, whereas in speech they can be freely substituted without loss.

This maps onto תּוֹרָה שֶׁבִּכְתָב versus תּוֹרָה שֶׁבְּעַל פֶּה. Written Torah's letters are intrinsic vessels — hence the legal stringencies of "חָסֵר וְיָתֵר," exact calligraphic form, and the prohibition against adding even a single letter. Oral Torah's letters are garments: "בְּתוֹשְׁבְּע"פ אֵינוֹ נוֹגֵעַ הָאוֹתִיּוֹת לְהְיוֹתָם בְּחִינַת לְבוּשִׁים בִּלְבַד" — one sage can articulate the same principle in entirely different words without diminishing the underlying light. A seeming paradox follows: if vessels reveal and garments conceal, why is Written Torah more opaque and Oral Torah more explicit? The answer: Written Torah carries intrinsically higher, more unified light — like a deep thinker who says little — while Oral Torah expands that same light into discursive form accessible below.

The same structure governs the four Divine Names ע"ב ס"ג מ"ה ב"ן. The letters of שֵׁם הוי' are essential vessels (כֵּלִים); the letters of other names are more external (לְבוּשִׁים). The four miluyim (spellings) reveal this: ש' ע"ב is written entirely with yudim because Chokhmah's quality — bitul, utter self-nullification — pervades all levels it contains. ש' ב"ן uses heys because Malkhut's contracted state means "הַגִּלּוּי הוּא כְּמוֹ הָעֶצֶם" — its revelation barely exceeds its external form. Descending to the worlds: Beriah's garments resemble inner vessels (the intellect's light shines openly in thought), Yetzirah's garments conceal like Asiyah's like coarse hide. Yet precisely in the densest concealment, "מַה יָּפוּ פְעָמַיִךְ בַּנְּעָלִים" — clarifying the נֶפֶשׁ הַבְּהֵמִית through the soul's embodied avodah generates the greatest יִתְרוֹן הָאוֹר.

### Resolution and Chiddush

The three kushyot now resolve. "עֶשְׂרִים גֵּרָה" are the ten powers of the divine soul (נֶפֶשׁ הָאֱלֹקִית) and ten powers of the animal soul (נֶפֶשׁ הַבְּהֵמִית). The first "מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל" — stated before the shekel's value is defined — refers to the soul as it stands in Atzilut, above number and measure: a gift from above whose magnitude "לֹא נוֹדַע מִסְפָּרוֹ." What the person is commanded to give is the other half: the ten powers of the animal soul refined through embodied service. "זֶה יִתְּנוּ" echoes "זֶה אֵלִי" at קְרִיעַת יַם סוּף — a moment of direct Atzmus-revelation — signaling that through biru ha-nefesh ha-behamit a commensurate light shines from above. The third phrase, "מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל תְּרוּמָה לַה'," names the gift returned: the revelation of the soul's root in Atzilut, bestowed precisely because the person gave the half that was theirs to give below.
