## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Naso 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Delivered for Parshat Naso — the longest single parasha in the Torah, containing the Priestly Blessing, the Sotah procedure, the Nazirite laws, and the lengthy account of the princes' dedications offerings — this maamar takes the parasha's central concern with "lifting up" (נשא) as its key to elaborate the hemshech's analysis of the ascending dynamic of Or Chozer and the physiology of divine light through the body (blood, heart, expansion and contraction).

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens on the word "נשא" itself — "lift up, raise." In the context of the parasha, it refers to taking a census by "lifting up the heads" of the Levitical families (the Gershonites, Kohathites, and Merarites), each assigned a specific carrying task for the Mishkan. The Rashab asks: what is the deeper meaning of "lifting up" in this context? How does the census and assignment of Mishkan-carrying roles connect to the Kabbalistic principle of Or Chozer — the ascent of divine light from below? And how does the Priestly Blessing ("יברכך ה' וישמרך... יאר ה' פניו אליך... ישא ה' פניו אליך") encode the structure of the divine Sefirot and their illumination of the world?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The Nazirite and blood-physiology analogy**: The Rashab develops an extended analogy centered on the circulation of blood — its expansion (hitkashrut) outward through the body and its contraction (histalkut) back toward the heart. When blood flows outward continuously without returning, it weakens. The return to the heart (histalkut) is what renews and strengthens the vitality of the entire circulatory system. This biological dynamic serves as the analogy for the Or Chozer: the divine light descends (hitpashut — expansion), reaches the farthest extremities of creation, and must then ascend (histalkut — contraction, withdrawal) in order to be renewed and to flow outward again with greater force.
- **Ratzo v'Shov as cosmic respiration**: The oscillation of blood is identified with the Kabbalistic principle of ratzo v'shov (running and returning) — the "heartbeat" of the cosmos. Just as life depends on this oscillation in the body, the maintenance of the worlds depends on the continuous alternation of Or Yashar (descent/expansion) and Or Chozer (ascent/contraction). The worlds are not static recipients of divine light but participants in a living rhythm.
- **The Levites as the circulatory vessels**: The assignment of specific carrying tasks to each Levitical family (Kohathites carry the Ark and holy vessels; Gershonites carry the curtains; Merarites carry the structural frames) corresponds in the Rashab's analysis to the differentiated roles of vessels (kelim) at different levels of the sefirot. The Ark (carried by Kohathites, the most holy) corresponds to the innermost vessels of Keter and Chokhmah; the curtains (Gershonites) to the intermediate vessels of the emotive sefirot; the structural frames (Merarites) to the outer vessels of the lowest Sefirot. The "carrying" is Or Chozer: the act of bearing the Mishkan upward on the journey corresponds to the soul's bearing of the divine light upward through its service.
- **The Priestly Blessing as sefirot-map**: The three verses of the Priestly Blessing correspond to three dimensions of divine illumination: "יברכך ה' וישמרך" (blessing and guarding) corresponds to the level of Keter — the divine will that blesses from above; "יאר ה' פניו אליך ויחנך" (illumination and grace) corresponds to Chokhmah and Binah — the divine wisdom shining its face directly; "ישא ה' פניו אליך וישם לך שלום" (lifting the divine countenance and giving peace) corresponds to the Sefirot of the midot (emotive attributes) and their harmonious integration in Yesod and Malkhut.
- **"Lifting the head" as elevation**: The specific phrase "שאו את ראש בני גרשון" (lift up the head of the sons of Gershon) is read not only as a census command but as a spiritual directive: the Levites, through their service, "lift up" the divine presence itself. Their carrying is an Or Chozer — the physical act of bearing the Mishkan pieces becomes a vehicle by which the divine light present in those objects ascends together with Israel's journey toward the Land.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the parasha's themes of carrying, blessing, and elevation all encode the single principle of Or Chozer: the human act of "lifting" (נשא) — whether lifting the heads of the Levites in census, lifting the Mishkan pieces on the journey, or lifting the priestly hands in blessing — is the structural Or Chozer dynamic. The physical and ritual acts of Naso are shown to be the cosmic respiration by which divine light ascends from the lowest material reality back toward its source, to be renewed and flow outward again.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Following the Shavuot maamarim that established the Torah-channel and the three-pillar structure, this maamar roots the Or Chozer dynamic in the body's own physiology (blood circulation) and in specific Torah ritual (Levitical service). This is a characteristic Rashab move: after constructing the abstract metaphysical system, he grounds it in the most concrete bodily and ritual details. The blood-circulation analogy becomes a recurring motif in the hemshech for explaining the ratzo v'shov dynamic.
