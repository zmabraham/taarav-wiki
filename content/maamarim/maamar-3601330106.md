## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayakhel-Pekudei 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Parshat Vayakhel-Pekudei is the culmination of the Mishkan-sequence in Shemot: all the donations gathered in Teruma are now fashioned, and the completed Mishkan is erected and accounted for (pekudei — the accounting). This maamar uses this double-parasha to develop the hemshech's account of the critical difference between tzivui (divine command, the Or's originating impulse) and asiyah (the actualized deed, the Or's terminal embodiment), arguing that the Mishkan's specific dignity — what makes it a dwelling for the Shekhinah — is that the human act of construction (asiyah, doing) transforms into a vessel for the Or in a way that the Or itself could not achieve by direct descent alone.

### The Opening Move

The parasha opens with Moshe assembling (vayakhel — gathering into a kahal, a community-whole) all the Israelites before announcing the Shabbat laws and the Mishkan's construction. The Rashab identifies the juxtaposition as the parasha's core question: why is Shabbat — the cessation from work — proclaimed immediately before the command to build the Mishkan? The answer cannot simply be "lest you think the Mishkan overrides Shabbat" (the halakhic explanation), because on a deeper level the juxtaposition must teach something about the nature of the Mishkan itself. What does the rest of Shabbat reveal about the act of Mishkan-construction?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Shabbat as the state of pure Or Yashar**: On Shabbat, all human constructive activity ceases. The world returns, as it were, to its pre-creation state — a world receiving Or Yashar without any human mediation or transformation. This is the state of the world vis-à-vis G-d's direct light, the mode of Sovev Kol Almin: the Or encompasses all equally, without the differentiations that human action introduces. Shabbat is therefore the paradigm of the world as pure Kli relative to divine light.
- **The Mishkan as the six-day work of the week**: The Mishkan is built on the six weekdays — the days of creative human action. This places it structurally in the domain of Or Chozer: it is the human collective (vayakhel — the assembled community) transforming raw materials through skilled labor into a vessel for divine presence. The Mishkan is Or Chozer's masterpiece: the maximum elevation of human material action into divine receptivity.
- **Vayakhel as the Or Chozer community-act**: The assembly (kahal) is itself significant. Or Chozer does not ascend from isolated individuals but from the collective — the full community of Israel. The Rashab reads vayakhel as a technical term: the unification of many partial upward-movements (individual Or Chozer contributions) into a single unified ascent. The Mishkan's construction required 603,550 people's donations and labor precisely because Or Chozer in its complete form is a collective phenomenon, not individual.
- **Pekudei as the accounting-structure (da'at)**: The second parasha, Pekudei, is the itemized accounting of every material used. The Rashab reads this accounting through the lens of Da'at (knowledge) — the faculty that unifies Chokhmah and Binah and brings their light into actual connection with the emotive faculties. Pekudei's counting and itemizing is the Da'at-act: it takes what was diffuse (all the donations) and makes it specific, measured, attributable. The dwelling of the Shekhinah in the completed Mishkan is not possible without this accounting — without the Da'at-integration that connects every material element to its source.
- **The Mishkan's erection as the completion of the circuit**: When Moshe finally erects (vayakem) the Mishkan on Rosh Chodesh Nissan — the first day of the first month of the second year — the divine cloud descends and fills the Mishkan (Shemot 40:34-35). This descent of the cloud is the Or Yashar re-entering the world through the vessel that Or Chozer's collective labor has prepared. The circuit is complete: Or Yashar commanded (tzivui) at Sinai, descended partially into matter through the Teruma donations, was shaped by skilled human hands (asiyah) through the six-days-mode, and now returns from below as an actualized Levush-structure — the completed Mishkan — that Or Yashar can now fill without shattering.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that the Mishkan represents the successful resolution of the problem posed by Ki Tisa (the shattering of the Tablets). The completion of the Mishkan demonstrates that when human asiyah (deed) is aligned with divine tzivui (command) through the full mediation of Kli and Levush structures, the Or Yashar can descend into matter without destruction. The kahal (community) is the Or Chozer formation that can sustain what no individual vessel could.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Vayakhel-Pekudei closes the Book of Shemot sequence and with it the hemshech's long meditation on the Mishkan as the paradigm of dira betachtonim. The subsequent maamarim (beginning with Vayikra) will shift to the service within the completed Mishkan — the korbanot (sacrifices) — which introduces the next stage of the hemshech's argument: how the ongoing service inside the completed structure continues to activate and sustain the Or's dwelling below.
