## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Shabbat Teshuva 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Shabbat Teshuva (the Shabbat between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur) 5670, develops the concept of Teshuvah as a metaphysical movement that corresponds precisely to the Or Chozer mechanism — not merely moral repentance but the soul's return to its source in the Yechida-dimension, which alone can access the Keter-level that Yom Kippur is to reveal.

### The Opening Move
The maamar addresses the week between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur as a specific structural moment in the divine calendar: after the coronation of Malchut (Rosh Hashanah), what must occur before the revelation of divine essence on Yom Kippur? The Rashab seizes on the quality of Shabbat Teshuva specifically — that Shabbat's unique quality of רצוא ושוב (yearning and return) intersects with the spiritual imperative of Teshuvah — to argue that the mechanism of genuine return is not an emotional process but a structural one involving the soul's deepest level.

### The Argumentative Arc
- **Ratzo v'Shov as the soul's oscillation.** The Rashab returns to the concept of רצוא ושוב from the opening of Ezekiel's vision (the Chayot rushing forward and returning). This oscillation is not incidental but describes the fundamental nature of every spiritual being: the impulse toward Ein Sof (ratzo) and the return into bounded existence (shov). Teshuvah is the ratzo-movement taken to its ultimate conclusion — not the normal oscillation of divine service but a thrust toward the absolute source.
- **Yechida and Keter.** The Rashab develops the correspondence between the Yechida (the highest soul-level, the point where the soul is one with the One) and Keter (the highest sefirah, the point where the sefirot are one with Ein Sof). Teshuvah, understood as accessing the Yechida, is therefore simultaneously accessing the level that corresponds to Keter in the divine structure. This is why Yom Kippur — which reveals Keter — is preceded by this period of Teshuvah: Teshuvah prepares the soul-vessel by activating its Yechida, which is the only vessel capable of receiving the Keter-illumination.
- **The Kav and Reshimu as the ground of Teshuvah.** After the Tzimtzum (the primordial contraction), what remains in the vacated space is the Reshimu (impression) — the trace of the infinite light. The Kav (line) that re-enters the vacated space is the directed shaft of Or Yashar descending from Ein Sof. The Rashab argues that Teshuvah operates at the level of the Reshimu: it is the soul's re-activation of the impression of the infinite that was always present within it, beneath all its finite constructions. True Teshuvah does not add something new; it uncovers what was always there.
- **Shabbat as the temporal dimension of Keter.** Within the week, Shabbat corresponds to Keter — it is the day of "ceasing" (שביתה) which, in Kabbalistic terms, means being at one's source rather than in active descent. Shabbat Teshuva is thus the temporal intersection of the soul's return-movement (Teshuvah) and the temporal Keter-dimension (Shabbat) — a doubled return to source that uniquely prepares for Yom Kippur.
- **Teshuvah's power to reach Keter directly.** The Rashab cites the principle that Teshuvah reaches the Throne of Glory (כסא הכבוד, which corresponds to Binah) but adds that the deepest form of Teshuvah — Teshuvah of the Yechida — reaches beyond even Binah to Keter itself. This is why Yom Kippur's forgiveness is absolute rather than graduated: it comes from the Keter-level, where there is no differentiation into degrees of sin or merit.

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that Shabbat Teshuva is the week's structural hinge: it is the moment when the soul's ratzo-movement (activated by Rosh Hashanah's coronation) reaches its apex, touching the Yechida-Keter correspondence, and thereby prepares the complete vessel for Yom Kippur's revelation of inner Keter. Teshuvah is thus not a mood or disposition but a precise metaphysical ascent to the soul's highest root.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar extends the Tishrei sub-series by introducing Teshuvah as the structural link between Malchut's coronation (Rosh Hashanah) and Keter's revelation (Yom Kippur). The hemshech has now established that the Tishrei season is a sequential progression through the levels of the divine structure: Malchut (Rosh Hashanah) → ascent through Teshuvah to Keter (Shabbat Teshuva / Yom Kippur) → re-entry into the world with that revelation (Sukkot). The Or Chozer mechanism is now being shown not merely as a theoretical structure but as the very shape of the Jewish spiritual year.
