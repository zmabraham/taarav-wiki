## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 2nd day of Shavuot 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar builds the analysis of two qualitatively distinct modes of rommemut (divine exaltedness/transcendence) as they bear on the structure of Tohu, and advances to a further cause of Tohu's internal fragmentation: the problem of shleimut — each sefirah of Tohu existed in a state of self-sufficient completeness that made it unable to receive from, or give to, any other sefirah. This maps onto the spiritual typology of Sodom and provides the foundation for understanding how Tikkun achieves true integration.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens on the first word of the Ten Commandments: "Anochi YKVK Elokekha asher hotzeitikha me'eretz Mitzrayim" (I am the Lord your God who took you out of Egypt). The opening question is pointed: why does the Torah identify God by the Exodus rather than by Creation? Creation — יש מאין, existence from absolute nothingness — is an incomparably greater demonstration of divine power than the miracles of Egypt (which were יש מיש, transformation of existing matter). Surely if one wants to make a claim about divine authority, "I who created heaven and earth from nothing" would be more compelling than "I who took you out of Egypt."

### The Argumentative Arc
- The maamar picks up from paragraph 236 (the close of the previous Shavuot maamar) where it was established that the sefirot of Tohu were in the mode of rommemut-as-metziyut (selfhood as positive experienced presence), causing them to perceive other sefirot as threatening realities that diminished their own standing.
- A new layer is introduced: there are two distinct aspects to Keter — pnimiyut (interiority, the aspect of essential hiddenness) and chitzoniyut (exteriority, the aspect of self-manifestation and dominion). The sefirot of Tohu corresponded to the chitzoniyut of the Keter-level (the "ten crowns" or "keter of each sefirah"), not its pnimiyut. In the mode of pnimiyut, the sefirot of the Akudim stage all dwelt together in unity because their existence was not in the mode of sensed metziyut. When they "came out" as the manifest sefirot of Tohu (Nekudim stage), they expressed only the external aspect of the Keter-crown — the mode of התנשאות (lordly self-assertion) rather than the mode of rommemut atzmit.
- The Rashab then introduces the second cause of Tohu's fragmentation: shleimut atzmi (intrinsic self-sufficiency). In Tikkun, each sefirah "knows" it is incomplete without the others — Chesed recognizes it needs Gevurah to be truly Chesed, and vice versa. This mutual need is not weakness but the structural logic of Tikkun: each sefirah's bitul toward the whole means it is oriented toward the divine completeness that resides in all opposites together. In Tohu, each sefirah was fully sufficient in itself (the mode of "שלי שלי ושלך שלך" — "mine is mine and yours is yours"). It had no felt need for the other and no impulse to give to the other.
- This is identified as the spiritual root of Sodom: the Midrash notes that Sodom's very geography made it self-sufficient (no need of rainfall from other lands, no need of trade). The people of Sodom were a social expression of Tohu's sefirot — radical self-enclosure, refusal of both giving and receiving. "Hashem in wisdom founded the earth" (Mishlei 3:19) — the wisdom of Tikkun is precisely the principle that each domain needs the other, that interdependence is the cosmic norm.
- The resolution to the Anochi question: "Anochi" points beyond the Name YKVK and beyond the sefirotic system entirely — to divine atzmiut (essential being), which is not in any mode of metziyut. From that level, both Creation and Exodus are equally expressions of the same infinite freedom. But the Torah specifically mentions the Exodus because yetziat Mitzrayim represents the soul's experiential liberation from the "Egypt" of its own contracted, self-enclosed existence — which is precisely the Tohu-mode. The Exodus is thus the experiential analog of the Tikkun-transformation: from self-enclosure (Sodom/Tohu/Mitzrayim) to openness and reception.

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that the double structure of Anochi YKVK Elokekha maps onto the dual nature of divine self-disclosure: Anochi (pointing to atzmiut beyond names) is revealed precisely through YKVK-Elokekha (the sefirotic system), and their unity is what the Exodus demonstrated to the Jewish soul — that the Infinite descends not despite finitude but through it. This is the experiential Tikkun of Tohu.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
The second night of Shavuot 5664 advances the hemshech by adding the concept of self-sufficient completeness (shleimut) as a second structural cause of Tohu alongside the previously developed cause of self-assertive rommemut. Together these two causes provide a complete account of why Tohu's sefirot shattered: they were each simultaneously too self-exalted to tolerate the other and too self-complete to need the other. Tikkun corrects both: bitul replaces self-assertion, and mutual interdependence replaces self-sufficiency. This duality will underlie the hemshech's subsequent treatment of how human avodah (divine service) mirrors and continues the cosmic Tikkun-process.
