## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayishlach 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the intricate relationship between chesed and emet as two distinct modes of divine generosity — chesed as superabundant, and emet as faithful-and-therefore-diminished — and uses Yaakov's prayer at the Yabok ford to demonstrate how the soul's recognition of its own smallness before divine kindness actually amplifies the Or Chozer it generates, constituting the highest form of petition.

### The Opening Move

The verse is Yaakov's prayer before meeting Eisav: "קטנתי מכל החסדים ומכל האמת" — "I have become small from all the kindnesses and from all the truth" (Bereishit 32:11). The Rashab identifies a sharp textual difficulty: it is understandable to say "I have become small from all the kindnesses" (Rashi: my merits have diminished because of the kindnesses already received), but what does it mean to say "I have become small from all the emet (truth)"? One can be humbled by kindness, but why would truth diminish one? Furthermore, the verse continues: "for with my staff I crossed this Jordan" — how is crossing with just a staff a reason to now say "save me"? The logical direction seems inverted.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Chesed and emet represent two distinct axes of divine giving**: chesed is abundant, free, and unconditioned — it flows without requiring a vessel of matching quality; emet is the mode of divine giving that is precisely calibrated to the recipient's reality, faithful to what was promised and to what is true about the receiver's capacity. They are not synonyms but orthogonal dimensions of how divinity relates to creation.
- **"Katanti mikol ha-chasadim" — chesed diminishes the recipient's independent claim**: when one has received abundant divine chesed, one's own merit (zechut) shrinks relative to the gift. This is straightforward: the more freely one has been given, the less one can claim by right.
- **"Mikol ha-emet" — the deeper paradox**: the Rashab's chiddush is that emet — divine faithfulness, the mode of Or Yashar that flows through the ordered seder hishtalshelut — also diminishes Yaakov, but in a different way. When God has been "true" to Yaakov (fulfilling every promise precisely), this reveals Yaakov's essential smallness before the very orderliness of divine providence. The emet of God's faithfulness throws into relief just how far Yaakov is from meriting what he has been given.
- **"Ki b'makli avarti" — the staff as the paradigm of Or Chozer**: Yaakov crossed the Jordan with only his staff — a simple, unadorned implement. In the hemshech's framework, the staff represents the concentrated, essential force of Or Chozer in its most stripped-down form: no accumulation of merit, no vessels, just the bare upward thrust of the soul. That he crossed with merely his staff and returned with two camps (shnei machanot) demonstrates that Or Chozer in its naked simplicity can generate a disproportionately powerful return of Or Yashar.
- **The inverted logic of "Hatzileni na" — save me because I am small, not despite it**: the Rashab resolves the puzzle by showing that Yaakov's request for salvation is grounded precisely in his smallness. This is not ordinary petition logic (I deserve help because of my merits) but the logic of Or Chozer: the very absence of independent merit, when fully recognized and articulated, creates the strongest upward pull on divine light. "Katanti" is itself the mechanism of salvation.
- **The maamar draws on the prior discussion of ratzoh eloki and ahava**: the source text references the distinction between the divine will (ratzon eloki) that is accessible to souls and the ahava and yirah that flow from it — connecting Yaakov's prayer to the ongoing hemshech analysis of how the soul's inner emotional states (ratzo and shov) generate Or Chozer of varying intensities.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the complete formula of Yaakov's prayer — katanti from chesed + katanti from emet + ki b'makli avarti + therefore Hatzileni — constitutes the template for how the neshama, when it truly recognizes its smallness before both the free gift (chesed) and the orderly gift (emet) of divinity, generates a uniquely powerful Or Chozer that is answered by the highest level of Or Yashar. The two camps (shnei machanot) that Yaakov returns with represent the dual influx of Or Yashar: one camp from the chesed-axis and one from the emet-axis.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar deepens the hemshech's psychology of Or Chozer by showing that its intensity is not proportional to the soul's accomplishments but inversely related to its self-assessment. The more genuinely the soul recognizes its smallness (katnut) before divine generosity, the more powerfully it generates Or Chozer — because that recognition is itself a form of bitul (self-nullification) that allows the Or Chozer to rise without obstruction. This teaching about katnut as a spiritual engine will resonate through the subsequent maamarim dealing with teshuvah, tefillah, and the soul's capacity to draw down Keter-level light.
