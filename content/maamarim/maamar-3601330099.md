## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Bo 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the concept of the Exodus moment — "bo el Paro" (come to Pharaoh) — as the paradigm for how Or Pnimi penetrates the deepest stronghold of the kelipot: not from without (which would be Or Makif) but by entering in ("bo" — come, not "lech" — go), because only from within the innermost chamber of the opposing force can the divine light achieve complete liberation.

### The Opening Move

God commands Moshe: "בא אל פרעה" — "Come to Pharaoh." The Rashab notes the striking choice of "bo" (come) rather than "lech" (go): God says "come to Pharaoh," which implies that God Himself is already within Pharaoh's domain and is calling Moshe to join Him there. This is a radical theological claim: the divine presence is present even in the deepest stronghold of impurity. The question becomes: what is the nature of the divine presence within Pharaoh, and why must the liberation be achieved by entering into that presence rather than confronting Pharaoh from without?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Pharaoh = the "tanin hagadol" (the great serpent) of the deepest kelipah**: the Zohar identifies Pharaoh with the cosmic serpent — the primordial force of self-referential power that denies the divine source. In the hemshech's framework, this corresponds to the kelipat nogah in its most extreme expression: not the transparent-shell of permitted things but the completely opaque shell of pure self-assertion ("li yeori, v'ani asitini" — "the river is mine and I made myself").
- **"Bo el Paro" — God is already present within Pharaoh's domain as the innermost spark**: the Rashab develops the teaching that the divine presence is not absent from the kelipot but present within them as the spark of divine life that sustains even the opposing force. This is the Or Pnimi that pervades all existence — even Pharaoh cannot exist without a divine life-force sustaining him. The command "come to Pharaoh" is thus the command to go to where the divine spark already is and liberate it from its captivity.
- **The final three plagues (locusts, darkness, death of firstborn) as the progressive stripping of Pharaoh's layers**: the plagues of Parshat Bo operate on successively deeper levels of the kelipah-structure. Locusts (arbeh) = the external layer of abundance that covers the inner truth; darkness (choshech) = the intermediate layer that prevents perception of the divine; death of firstborn = the innermost layer — the "firstborn" of the kelipah, the principle of self-referential priority that must be broken before liberation is possible.
- **The Pesach sacrifice and the "leaping over" (pesach = to skip) as Or Makif returning from above**: on the night of the Exodus, the divine strikes the firstborn of Egypt but "leaps over" (pasach) the houses of Israel. This skipping is Or Makif operating: the surrounding divine light hovers above the Israelite homes and protects them not by entering the houses but by surrounding them. This is the complementary movement to "bo" — first Or Pnimi enters the innermost domain of the kelipah (via Moshe entering Pharaoh's palace), and then Or Makif protects Israel from above.
- **Chametz and matzah as Or Pnimi vs. Or Makif in food**: the prohibition of chametz and the requirement of matzah at the Exodus encode the same principle. Chametz (leavened bread) represents the expansion of matter through time and fermentation — the principle of Or Yashar that has become so embedded in the natural order that it appears to operate independently (the bread "rises on its own," ki pita es haam). Matzah (unleavened bread) is the "bread of affliction" and also the "bread of faith" — the vessel that receives Or Pnimi directly, without the intermediate puffing-up of the natural order.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the liberation from Egypt is the historical enactment of the supreme Or Pnimi principle: true freedom comes not from fleeing the domain of the kelipah but from entering its innermost chamber ("bo"), finding the divine spark there, and liberating it. This is the deepest meaning of "bo el Paro" — God commands Moshe to enter, because the liberation must be achieved from the inside out, not the outside in.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Bo maamar completes the Va'era → Bo arc: Va'era analyzed the two names of God (El Shaddai as Or Makif, Havaya as Or Pnimi) as the framework of the Exodus; Bo shows how Or Pnimi operates at its most penetrating moment — entering the deepest domain of opposition to liberate the divine spark within. This analysis will feed directly into the Beshalach maamar, where the liberated people stand at the Sea of Reeds and the full power of Or Chozer is expressed in the Song of the Sea — the collective rising of all the Or Pnimi that was liberated from Egypt.
