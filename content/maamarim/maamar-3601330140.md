## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Bereishit 1905-06** in the year **עתר"ו** (1905–06), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

The specific question focuses on understanding how the language used points to a deeper Kabbalistic reality within the hemshech's ongoing analysis.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Bereishit 1905-06. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ בראשית, עתר"ו corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar — the first of the post-holiday Torah-portion sequence of 5666 — builds the hemshech's account of why the Torah (which is "a book of instruction" — sefer hahora'ah) opens with the creation narrative rather than with the first commandment (as Rashi famously raises, citing this as the question par excellence). The resolution introduces one of the hemshech's most far-reaching arguments: the ontological superiority of physical mitzva-performance (mitzvot ma'asiyot) over Torah-study and spiritual contemplation, grounded in the claim that the physical world's Yesh (being) carries divine power (koach ha'atzmus) more completely than even the highest spiritual lights.

### The Opening Move

The maamar's opening is the source text's exact citation from Rashi: "Bereishit bara Elohim et hashamayim v'et ha'aretz — In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" — and Rashi's question: "Aritzinus lo hayah tzorich l'hatchil et haTorah ela me'hachodesh hazeh lachem — It need not have begun the Torah except from 'This month shall be for you [the beginning of months]' (Exod. 12:2, the first commandment given to the Jewish people collectively). Why then did it open with Bereishit? Because of 'koach ma'asav higid l'amo — He declared the power of His works to His people' (Ps. 111:6)." Rashi's answer is about the Land of Israel: God told Israel who has the right to the Land. But the maamar probes beneath this surface: what is it about the creation narrative that is indispensable as a foundation for everything that follows — why is "koach ma'asav" the necessary preamble to all the mitzvot?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Torah as a book of instruction (hora'ah) — the apparent problem**: If Torah is fundamentally halachic (instructional — telling us what to do), then the creation narrative seems extraneous. An instruction-book typically begins with its first instruction, not with a cosmological preamble. The apparent solution (Rashi's "koach ma'asav" — declaring God's power to His people) explains why the narrative serves as justification for Israel's claim to the Land — but the maamar asks: is that the full depth of the answer? Why does "koach ma'asav" (the power of His creative acts) specifically require narration in a book of instructions?
- **The ontological priority of Yesh over Or**: The maamar develops what will be the hemshech's most counterintuitive metaphysical claim: that the Yesh hanivra (created physical being) carries within it a greater manifestation of divine atzmus (essence) than the spiritual lights (orot) that precede it in the chain of worlds. The argument proceeds as follows: the spiritual Orot (lights of Atzilut and above) are "Orot" — illuminations, disclosures, radiations of divine being — but they are not divine being itself. Even the highest Orot are revelations, not essence. The physical Yesh, by contrast, is created ex nihilo (yesh mi'ayin) — brought into existence from absolute nothingness — and this very act of creation from nothing requires a power that exceeds what any Or can channel: the power of atzmus itself, the divine self-existence that alone can generate genuine Yesh (being) from absolute non-being.
- **The proof from Yesh mi'Ayin**: The hemshech's formal argument (elaborated in the source text continuation and in related maamarim) is that Or (light, spiritual revelation) can only produce "Yesh mi'Yesh" — being from being, a kind of refinement or differentiation of what already exists in the spiritual domain. But the creation of physical matter from absolutely nothing requires Koach ha'atzmus — the power of divine self-existence — because only something that IS in an absolute, self-grounded way can produce something from absolute non-being. The physical world's brute existence (its density, its resistance, its sheer materiality) is therefore the most intense available manifestation of divine atzmus — not despite its opacity but because of it.
- **Mitzvot ma'asiyot (physical commandments) as higher than Torah-as-light**: This leads to the hemshech's bold practical conclusion: physical mitzva-performance in the material world draws from koach ha'atzmus in a way that Torah study (which is "Torah or — Torah is light" — an Or, a spiritual illumination) cannot. Torah study generates the highest forms of Or — even the Or atzmi (self-luminous light) that is Torah's distinctive quality. But physical mitzvot, because they engage the physical Yesh directly, generate Or Chozer that originates from the deepest level of divine atzmus — specifically, the atzmus as it underlies and generates physical Yesh. This is why Bereishit (the creation narrative) must precede the mitzvot: it establishes the ontological ground that makes physical mitzva-performance the highest form of avodah.
- **"Koach ma'asav higid l'amo" — the declaration of creative power as foundation**: "He declared the power of His works to His people" means: the Torah first shows Israel that God's creative power (koach ha'atzmus in its world-creating mode) is the ground of all existence — including the physical existence of the Land of Israel and of Israel itself. Only with this foundation established can the mitzva-instructions that follow be understood in their full depth: each physical mitzva is an act by which Israel engages koach ha'atzmus at the level of the physical Yesh, generating Or Chozer from the deepest available level.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that Bereishit is not merely a cosmological preamble but the essential metaphysical foundation for the entire Torah: it establishes that the physical world's Yesh carries koach ha'atzmus, and therefore that physical mitzva-performance is not a concession to human embodiment but the highest possible form of divine service. The creation narrative must come first because without it, the mitzvot could be misread as a program for the soul to escape the physical world (to ascend from Yesh to Ayin, from material to spiritual). With Bereishit as foundation, the mitzvot are properly understood as a program for the physical world to become dira betachtonim — a dwelling-place for the divine atzmus precisely within its Yesh-existence.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Bereishit maamar initiates the Torah-portion cycle of the 5666 sequence and makes explicit the principle that has been implicit throughout the High Holiday maamarim: the physical performance of mitzvot is the apex of divine service, not because spirituality is devalued but because the physical Yesh carries koach ha'atzmus in a way that spiritual lights cannot. This principle will be developed and deepened through the Noach, Lech Lecha, and Vayera maamarim, as the hemshech tracks the transition from the universal (Bereishit's creation of all reality) to the particular (Avraham's covenant as the specific human being who first discovered and actualized the koach ha'atzmus within the physical world's Yesh).
