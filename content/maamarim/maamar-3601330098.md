## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Va'era 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the concept of the divine "appearance" (vayeira — "He appeared") as the disclosure of the Or Makif level of Keter within the experience of Moshe and the patriarchs: what appeared to the Avot as "El Shaddai" (the divine name associated with tzimtzum and limitation) is now being disclosed to Moshe as the four-letter Name (Havaya) — a shift that encodes the difference between Or Makif that surrounds without penetrating the vessels and Or Pnimi that enters and transforms them from within.

### The Opening Move

God's opening declaration to Moshe is the central puzzle: "וארא אל אברהם אל יצחק ואל יעקב באל שדי ושמי הוי' לא נודעתי להם" — "I appeared to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as El Shaddai, but My Name Havaya I was not known to them." The Rashab presses on the apparent contradiction: God certainly spoke to the patriarchs and used the Name Havaya in those conversations — the Chumash records it explicitly. In what sense was the Name Havaya "not known" to them? This is not a factual gap but a structural distinction about modes of divine manifestation.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The Name El Shaddai corresponds to Or Makif — divine presence as surrounding, limiting, bounding**: "Shaddai" contains the root "she-dai" — "who said Enough!" to creation — representing the divine function of tzimtzum, of setting the boundary at which the infinite stops and the finite begins. The Avot experienced God primarily as the transcendent power that sets limits on creation — as Or Makif that surrounds but does not fully penetrate the vessels of the natural order.
- **The Name Havaya corresponds to Or Pnimi — divine presence as indwelling, animating, pervading**: the four-letter Name represents the aspect of divinity that is continuously present within creation at every moment, not hovering above it but woven into the fabric of existence as its inner life. The disclosure of Havaya is thus the shift from experiencing God as external (surrounding) to experiencing God as internal (indwelling).
- **The patriarchs' Or Makif experience vs. Moshe's Or Pnimi mission**: the Avot, as the "merkavah" (divine chariot) of Atzilut, were themselves in a state of total bitul (nullification) to the divine — they experienced God as the overwhelming surrounding presence (El Shaddai/Or Makif). But their very closeness to the divine meant that the disclosure of the Havaya-level (Or Pnimi) was not yet the task; they received the surrounding light but did not yet activate the indwelling.
- **Moshe's mission is the actualization of Or Pnimi within the national structure**: Moshe is the first shaliach (agent/emissary) whose task is not merely to embody divine holiness but to bring the Or Pnimi of Havaya into the national-collective body of Israel. The plagues are not merely acts of judgment but structured interventions that progressively strip away the kelipot that prevent Or Pnimi from penetrating Egypt/Mitzrayim's realm.
- **The disclosure of Havaya at the national level is the mechanism of geulah**: the hemshech has been building toward the insight that the galut (exile) is fundamentally the condition of Or Makif without Or Pnimi — the divine presence surrounds Israel even in Egypt but does not yet fully animate them from within as a free people. The geulah (redemption) is precisely the moment when Or Pnimi breaks through — when Havaya is "known" in its indwelling dimension, not merely its surrounding one.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that "Vayeira" — the divine appearing — is always first as El Shaddai (Or Makif), because the infinite cannot be known from within the finite without preparation. The disclosure of Havaya (Or Pnimi) requires the prior establishment of El Shaddai's surrounding presence, which creates the vessel. The Exodus will be the moment of Havaya's full disclosure — Or Pnimi entering the national vessel that Or Makif has prepared.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Va'era maamar advances the hemshech's analysis of Or Makif and Or Pnimi at the most concrete historical level: the shift from El Shaddai to Havaya is the biblical record of the shift from Or Makif to Or Pnimi at the national scale. This connects the abstract Kabbalistic framework to the lived experience of Jewish history and provides the hemshech with its most powerful example of how the two modes of divine light operate in historical time. The analysis will continue in the Bo maamar, which examines what occurs when Or Pnimi fully enters the domain of Egypt at the moment of the Exodus itself.
