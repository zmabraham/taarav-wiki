## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Eikev 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar advances the hemshech's analysis by using Parshat Eikev — whose dominant themes are the reward of Torah observance, the heel (*eikev*) representing lowliness and follow-through, and the blessing of the land — to develop the relationship between the lower rungs of the sefirotic chain (Netzach, Hod, Yesod) and the drawing of divine light into the material world. The construct being built is an account of how mitzvah performance in the lowest registers of physicality activates precisely the highest sources of Or Ein Sof.

### The Opening Move

The parasha's name *Eikev* (heel) presents an immediate paradox: what Kabbalistic significance can attach to something as base as a heel? The very lowliness of the term is the key. The maamar exploits the established teaching that the heel represents the aspect of the soul — and of divine service — that persists without felt inspiration, the *eikev* of perseverance that outlasts the *rosh* (head) of initial enthusiasm. The puzzle is why Torah would associate the highest rewards with this apparently least-elevated modality of service.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The chain of worlds and the lowest rung**: Building on the prior maamar's mapping of Torah's parashiyot onto the sefirotic structure, this discourse focuses on Netzach and Hod (*Bnei Yisrael* in the formula *vayedaber YHVH el Moshe daber el Bnei Yisrael*) as the specific sefirot that correspond to the everyday, habitual dimension of divine service — the "heels" of the sefirotic body.

- **Eikev as Or Chozer mechanism**: The very modality of service associated with *eikev* — consistent mitzvah fulfillment without peak experience — is precisely the vehicle of Or Chozer. Or Chozer operates not from the luminous heights of apprehension but from the tenacious constancy of the lowest level that refuses to disengage. This is why the Torah promises that *eikev tishme'un* (if you heed even the overlooked commandments) will produce the most expansive blessings.

- **Reward as cosmic not merely individual**: The blessing promised in the parasha — fertility, health, victory over enemies — is understood not as personal recompense but as the cosmic consequence of completing the sefirotic circuit. When Or Chozer from the lowest register of Asiyah ascends through the full chain, it draws Or Ein Sof back down in a form that transforms the material world itself.

- **The paradox resolved**: Precisely because the *eikev* of service bypasses the ego's investment in spiritual achievement, it reaches deeper than the head. The heel is unguarded; it touches the ground directly. In Kabbalistic terms, the lower the point of contact with physical reality, the higher the source that is engaged in the Or Chozer ascent — since the greater the distance traversed, the higher the origin from which the reflected light returns.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that the principle of *dira betachtonim* (divine dwelling below) is not achieved despite the lowly physical world but specifically through it. The *eikev* — the overlooked, habitual, unglamorous tail-end of religious life — is the very fulcrum that triggers an Or Chozer capable of reaching the Keter-level. This is the hemshech's claim in its most practically challenging form: the most ordinary religious acts carry cosmological weight.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Following the Va'etchanan maamar's grounding of Torah study as a cosmic act, this discourse extends the logic to mitzvah practice — specifically to the aspects of mitzvah practice that are least celebrated. The hemshech is building toward the comprehensive claim that all of Jewish life, from the elevated to the mundane, participates in the Or Yashar/Or Chozer dynamic. Eikev is the parasha that licenses attention to the bottom of the hierarchy, which the hemshech needs to validate its account of how the lowest reaches draw from the very highest sources.
