## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Shabbat Teshuva 1905-06** in the year **עתר"ו** (1905–06), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Delivered on the Shabbat between Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur — the "Shabbat of Return" (Shabbat Teshuva, so called because the Haftara begins "Shuvu Yisrael" — Return, O Israel) — this maamar builds the hemshech's account of teshuva (repentance/return) as the purest form of Or Chozer. Teshuva is not treated here as moral reparation (the halakhic category of chazara b'teshuva) but as a metaphysical event: the soul's ascent from its lowest point back toward its divine source in a movement that paradoxically enables a level of Or Chozer that could not be achieved by a soul that had never descended.

### The Opening Move

The Haftara's opening verse "Shuvu Yisrael ad Hashem Elokecha ki kashalta ba'avonecha — Return, O Israel, to the Lord your God, for you have stumbled in your sin" (Hosea 14:2) generates the inaugural paradox: why does the prophet say "ad Hashem" (up to God) rather than "el Hashem" (toward God)? The distinction is significant in Kabbalistic reading: "el" implies movement toward an external destination, while "ad" implies reaching a specific threshold — a point up to which but not beyond which the return movement extends. What is this threshold? And why does stumbling (kashalata) serve as the basis for the command to return — does the stumbling make the return necessary, or does it somehow make the return possible?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Teshuva as Or Chozer from the deepest level**: The hemshech's frame for teshuva is Or Chozer: just as Or Yashar descends to its maximum depth before Or Chozer begins its ascent, the soul's sin/stumbling represents the maximum depth of Or Yashar's descent into the realm of concealment (the "avon" is not merely a moral failure but a metaphysical condition of maximum divine hiddenness). The command "shuvu" is the command for Or Chozer to begin from precisely that depth — the depth of kashalata (stumbling) is the exact starting point from which Or Chozer can ascend with the greatest possible reach.
- **"Ad Hashem Elokecha" — the threshold of Atzmus**: The "ad" (up to) points to the specific ceiling that teshuva's Or Chozer can reach: it reaches ad (up to) the level of Hashem Elokecha — specifically, the conjunction of the divine Name Havayah (the name that points to transcendence beyond the chain of worlds) with "Elokecha" (your God — God as relationally present to you). This conjunction is precisely the level established in the preceding Rosh Hashana maamarim as the meeting-point of Or Yashar (Havayah's transcendence) and Or Chozer (Elokecha's immanence). Teshuva's Or Chozer reaches up to but not into Atzmus itself — it reaches the threshold (ad) where Or Yashar and Or Chozer meet in the Sefirah of Da'at.
- **Shabbat as the structural enabler of teshuva's highest reach**: The maamar develops a specific argument about why this particular form of teshuva-discourse occurs on Shabbat rather than on Yom Kippur itself. Shabbat's structure (shevita, cessation/rest) is the weekly analog of the cosmic "rest" that follows the completion of Or Yashar's creative descent. On Shabbat, the Or Yashar of the week's creative work ceases its downward movement, and the Or Chozer that was generated through that week's work ascends and is "received" by the upper levels. Shabbat Teshuva is therefore the moment when the Or Chozer of the entire year's avodah — including its stumbling and its repair — ascends maximally. Teshuva performed within the Shabbat framework achieves a level of Or Chozer that weekday teshuva cannot.
- **"Ba'avonecha" — sin as the vehicle of Or Chozer**: The Rashab develops the classic Kabbalistic insight (based on the verse "v'hachata'im yitamu min ha'aretz — let sinners cease from the earth" which is read as "let the sins cease, not the sinners") that the avon (sin/stumbling) is not merely an obstacle to Or Chozer but, paradoxically, its vehicle. The soul that has stumbled has descended to a depth of Or Yashar's concealment that a soul that never stumbled has not reached — and Or Chozer that rises from that greater depth reaches a correspondingly higher level. This is the hemshech's resolution of the Talmudic dictum (Berakhot 34b) that "in the place where ba'alei teshuva stand, even the completely righteous cannot stand": the ba'al teshuva generates Or Chozer from a depth the tzaddik has not visited, and Or Chozer from a deeper starting point reaches a higher endpoint.
- **The ten days between Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur as the Or Chozer arc**: The maamar positions Shabbat Teshuva as the midpoint of a ten-day Or Chozer arc: Rosh Hashana initiates the divine disclosure (Or Yashar descending to its maximum annual depth); the ten days of teshuva are the ascending movement of Or Chozer; Yom Kippur is the moment when Or Chozer reaches its annual apex — the level of Keter, specifically Pnimiyut Atik. The Shabbat between Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur is the moment of "menucha" (rest/reception) when the ascending Or Chozer pauses in the "upper Shabbat" of the divine structure — received by the upper sefirot before its final ascent to Yom Kippur's Keter-level.

### What It Gets To

The maamar reaches the conclusion that teshuva is not a linear repair-operation (fixing what was broken) but a spiral: the soul that returns (shuv) rises higher than it would have had it never descended, because Or Chozer from a lower depth reaches a higher height. "Ad Hashem Elokecha" defines the ceiling of this ascent: the soul in teshuva rises not merely to the level of pre-sin innocence but to the Da'at-level where Or Yashar and Or Chozer meet — the level at which the soul experiences divine unity (Havayah hu ha'Elokim) directly rather than mediated through intellectual understanding. Shabbat Teshuva's special power is that Shabbat's structure temporarily lifts the ceiling of Or Chozer, allowing the teshuva of this Shabbat to reach the level of Pnimiyut Atik that will be fully revealed on Yom Kippur.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Shabbat Teshuva occupies the structural center of the 5666 High Holiday sequence: it bridges the Rosh Hashana maamarim's establishment of Malchut/Atzmus and haktara (coronation) with the Sukkot maamarim' development of the four-species/inclusivity framework. The Or Chozer analysis of teshuva is also the hemshech's most sustained engagement with the question of moral failure and its metaphysical function — establishing that the soul's stumbling is not an accidental complication of the Or Yashar/Or Chozer system but a structural feature of it, one that the system is designed to convert into a higher ascent than would have been possible through unbroken righteousness.
