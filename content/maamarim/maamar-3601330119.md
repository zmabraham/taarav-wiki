## Context

This maamar was delivered during **2nd day of Shavuot, at the meal 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Delivered at the festive meal of the second day of Shavuot, this maamar constructs a Kabbalistic cosmology of the three pillars of the world (Torah, Avodah/service, and Gemilut Chasadim/acts of kindness) from Pirkei Avot, reading their scriptural sources through the lens of the hemshech's analysis of lights and vessels, primacy of vessels over lights in the divine plan, and the ontological precedence of Earth (the lower realm) over Heaven in divine intention.

### The Opening Move

The Mishna in Avot (1:2) states: "על שלשה דברים העולם עומד על התורה ועל העבודה ועל גמילות חסדים" — the world stands on Torah, on service/worship, and on acts of loving-kindness. The maamar cites the Midrashic prooftexts: "ואשים דברי בפיך" (I have placed my words in your mouth) for Torah; "ובצל ידי כסיתיך" (I have covered you with the shadow of my hand) for Gemilut Chasadim; "לנטוע שמים וליסוד ארץ" (to plant the heavens and to establish the earth) for service/worship (avodah), identified with the sacrifices (korbanot) and the image of "a ladder set upon the earth with its top reaching heaven." The question: what exactly is the meaning of these three pillars, their specific scriptural associations, and why the world requires all three?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Day and night as tzimtzum and kav**: The Rashab anchors the analysis in a principle established earlier in the hemshech: day and night correspond to the revelation of the kav (line of light) and the tzimtzum (contraction) respectively. The tzimtzum is prior to the revelation, but the revelation is the purpose; the tzimtzum is "secondary" yet necessary — it alone makes the structured divine light (which is the purpose) possible. This temporal paradox (tzimtzum precedes light in time, but light precedes tzimtzum in intention) becomes the master framework for understanding the three pillars.
- **Vessels precede lights in the divine plan**: Through an extended analogy of teacher and student, the Rashab establishes that the vessels (kelim) — the receptive capacity, the form of what will be received — are conceived first in the teacher's mind, even though in the actual act of transmission the light (the knowledge conveyed) flows first and the vessel (the student's comprehension-form) follows. Similarly, in the cosmic creative act, lights were actualized before vessels in the order of manifest reality, yet vessels were primary in divine intention (machshavah). This is the same principle as "sof ma'aseh oleh b'machshavah techilah" — the final purpose precedes all else in thought.
- **Earth precedes heaven in intention**: The Rashab develops the Midrashic dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel about whether Heaven or Earth was created first, resolving it by distinguishing two planes: in the order of actual creation (b'seder hishtalshelut), Heaven was created first and Earth derived from it; in the order of divine intention (b'machshavah), Earth was first, as it was the ultimate goal. This parallels the lights/vessels dynamic: Heaven = lights = first in actualization; Earth = vessels = first in intention.
- **The three pillars as three structural axes**: Torah corresponds to the divine speech ("I have placed my words in your mouth") — the light (or) descending from above, the Or Yashar axis. Gemilut Chasadim corresponds to the "shadow of my hand" covering — a protective, encompassing quality that corresponds to the makif (encompassing) dimension of divine light, the way divine care covers and sustains without necessarily being internalized. Avodah/service (specifically korbanot, the sacrifices, and the image of the ladder) is the axis of Or Chozer — human beings, from the lowest physical reality (earth, ארצה), reaching upward (וראשו מגיע השמימה) to the divine. The ladder is the Or Chozer itself.
- **Rshimo as vessel-root**: The Rashab connects the Rshimo (the residual impression left after the tzimtzum) to the principle that vessels preexist in potential even before lights are drawn into them. The Rshimo is the "vessel-nature" inherent in Ein Sof before creation — it is why ordered, bounded creation was possible at all. The three pillars of the world correspond to three aspects of this Rshimo-as-vessel-capacity: Torah (divine light accessible through bounded form), Avodah (human action as ascending light), and Gemilut Chasadim (the encompassing divine care that holds the entire structure).
- **The analogy of the overflow**: The Rashab uses the analogy of why a teacher's light, without a "bounded" quality within it (a natural organizational principle), cannot generate light for a student even when the teacher "withholds" their own light. Only a teacher who is internally organized (מסודר) can find, within their own contraction, the specific bounded light that corresponds to what the student can receive. This explains why the tzimtzum — far from being a mere "dimming" of the divine — was precisely what enabled structured, world-appropriate divine light to emerge.

### What It Gets To

The three pillars of the world are revealed as three modes of the lights-and-vessels dynamic: Torah is Or Yashar (light descending through divine speech into bounded form); Avodah/sacrifices/prayer is Or Chozer (human ascent from earth toward heaven); and Gemilut Chasadim is the makif (encompassing light) that sustains the whole from outside. The world "stands on" all three because all three structural dimensions of the divine-created relationship are necessary: descent, ascent, and encompassment. Removing any one collapses the ontological structure.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar is a pivotal synthesis maamar, the third in the Shavuot sequence. After the first night established the Names/tzimtzum framework and the second night established the Torah-channel, the festive meal maamar grounds the entire system in the practical categories of Jewish life. The three pillars of the world are shown to be nothing less than the three structural axes of the hemshech's cosmos — making the Kabbalistic system directly continuous with the ethical and practical teaching of Pirkei Avot. The detailed vessels-before-lights argument also advances the hemshech's ongoing treatment of how the divine plan (in which Earth/lower realms are primary) differs from the actual sequence of creation — a tension that will recur throughout the remaining years of the hemshech.
