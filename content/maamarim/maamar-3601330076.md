## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Nitzavim-Vayelech 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar constructs a differentiated ontology of divine Names vs. divine Lights (שמות ואורות), establishing that the category of שם (Name) represents hiddenness and separateness from the divine essence while אור (Light) represents revealed, essence-connected illumination — and then maps this distinction onto the structure of Malchut as the mode through which God governs and sustains creation.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens on the verse "אתם נצבים היום כולכם" — "You stand today, all of you, before the Lord your God" (Deut. 29:9). The Rashab notes that "today" (היום) in Kabbalistic tradition refers to Rosh Hashanah, the day of universal standing before God. Two paradoxes emerge: (1) At the giving of Torah (Sinai), ranks were sharply differentiated — Moses had his own domain, Aaron his, the elders theirs, the people theirs. But on Rosh Hashanah "all stand equally" (נצבים כולם בשוה). How can the highest and the lowest stand in identical relation to God? (2) If they all stand equally, why does the verse then immediately enumerate the graduated ranks — "your heads, your tribes, your elders, your officers, all the men of Israel"? What function do the distinctions serve once equality has been declared?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **The distinction between שם (Name) and אור (Light).** The Rashab opens a foundational analysis: Light (אור) is attached to its source — precisely because it is bound to the essence, it shines forth and reveals that essence. A Name (שם), by contrast, is separated from the essence; it does not reveal the essence but points to it from outside. The analogy from human experience: the vitality (חיות) of a person is a form of light — it clings to the essence and reveals that a being is alive. The name of a person is separate — it designates but does not disclose.
- **שם as the mode of Malchut's governance.** The verse "ארוממך אלקי המלך" (I will exalt You, my God the King) is read as pointing to Malchut — the sefirah through which God governs. But Malchut functions in creation not as a light flooding the world but as a Name: hidden, non-self-revealing, yet the designator of all reality. "מלכותך בכל משלה" — God's kingship rules everything — but as a כח נעלם (hidden power), not as perceptible light. Hence the Name structure: present everywhere yet nowhere manifest.
- **The paradox of equal standing.** Sinai's differentiation of ranks reflects the mode of אור — light gradates by proximity to its source; closeness brings greater radiance. Rosh Hashanah's universal equality reflects the mode of שם — a Name applies equally to all who bear it regardless of proximity. The king's Name rests upon all his subjects identically; rank does not alter name-relationship. This is why all souls "stand equally" on Rosh Hashanah: it is the day when the שם-dimension of divine kingship is most revealed.
- **Why ranks are still enumerated.** Even within the egalitarianism of שם, the enumerated ranks serve a real function: they specify how each level receives and transmits the Name-energy into its domain. The Name is one; its refraction through differentiated vessels creates the hierarchy of "heads, tribes, elders." Thus equality and hierarchy are simultaneously true — equality in the Name-relation, differentiation in the vessel-structure through which that relation operates.
- **The soul's relationship to the body as analogy.** The Rashab draws an analogy to how the human soul relates to the body through its name: the soul is essentially beyond the body, yet it "turns toward" those who call it by name (נפנה לקוראיו בשמו). The Name is the point of interface between the boundless essence and the bounded receiver. So too, Malchut as שם is the interface between Ein Sof and the created realm.
- **Inner and outer dimensions of Malchut.** Returning to the preceding maamar's framework: the outer dimension of Malchut is the Name that governs and sustains the worlds (חיצוניות); the inner dimension (פנימיות) is where Malchut is bound to its source in Z'eir Anpin and above — still functioning as a Name but one whose interiority is in direct contact with the light of Atzilut. The souls of Israel, on Rosh Hashanah, access the inner dimension through the equal standing that transcends differentiation.

### What It Gets To
The maamar arrives at the conclusion that the equality of all souls on Rosh Hashanah is not a flattening of spiritual distinction but a revelation of the שם-dimension of Malchut — which, being a Name rather than a light, relates to all equally as subjects of one King. The ranked enumeration that follows is not a contradiction but the articulation of how that universal relation differentiates through different vessel-structures. Malchut as שם is thus the operative concept linking divine transcendence to the created world without compromising either.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar, delivered in Elul just before Rosh Hashanah תרד"ע, pivots the hemshech from its extended analysis of Or and Kli to the dimension of שם — which becomes a new organizing concept. The distinction of Light vs. Name enriches the hemshech's account of how Ein Sof relates to created existence: not only through the gradated light of the sefirot (Or Yashar) but through the Name-structure of Malchut, which permits a non-gradated, universal presence. This prepares the ground for the Rosh Hashanah maamarim in the hemshech's second volume, which will develop the theme of divine kingship and the universal soul-standing in much greater depth.
