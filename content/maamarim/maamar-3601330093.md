## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayeshev 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the concept of the tzaddik as a structural necessity within the divine system — not merely an ethical ideal — by reading Yosef's story as the model for how the sefira of Yesod operates: channeling all the light from the upper sefirot into Malchut, while simultaneously withstanding the greatest pressures of the lower forces (the "pit" of galut) without being contaminated by them.

### The Opening Move

The parasha opens with Yaakov dwelling (vayeshev) in the land of his fathers — a moment of apparent rest and settlement — immediately followed by the upheaval of Yosef's story. The Rashab is drawn to the tension: why does the Torah immediately shatter Yaakov's rest? The Midrash notes that "Yaakov sought to dwell in peace, but the trouble of Yosef pounced on him." This prompts the Kabbalistic question: what is it about the archetype of Yesod (Yosef) that necessarily generates agitation in the system, and why must the tzaddik descend to the pit before ascending to his role?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Vayeshev corresponds to the Sefirah of Yesod in a state of potential**: Yaakov's desire to "dwell" (lashevet) is the resting-state of Yesod — the principle that gathers all the flows of the upper sefirot and holds them in concentrated form. But Yesod cannot remain in this gathering mode; its function is to channel, and channeling requires movement and descent.
- **Yosef's coat of many colors — the garment of Yesod that contains all colors**: the "ketonet passim" (coat of many colors or striped coat) represents Yesod's capacity to contain within itself the energies of all the other sefirot — each "color" a different sefira — without those energies canceling each other out. This is the unique characteristic of Yesod: integration without dissolution.
- **The pit (bor) without water — the structure of the klipot surrounding Yesod**: when Yosef is thrown into a pit that has "no water" (ein bo mayim), the hemshech reads this as the structure of the kelipot surrounding the channel of Yesod in the lower worlds. The absence of water (absence of Torah/divine flow) is the defining characteristic of the klipot — they are empty vessels that draw from Yesod's overflow without contributing to the upward flow.
- **The descent to Egypt as Or Yashar entering the deepest level of concealment**: Yosef's sale and descent to Egypt represents Or Yashar in its most concentrated, concealed form entering the lowest world (Mitzrayim/Egypt, numerically and conceptually corresponding to the constrictions of Asiyah). The Or Yashar does not cease flowing; it simply becomes maximally hidden, which is the precondition for the revelation that will follow (Yosef's rise to rulership — the ultimate dira b'tachtonim).
- **The tzaddik who "lives by his faith" sustains the world**: the hemshech connects the story of Yosef to the verse "v'tzaddik be'emunato yichyeh" — the tzaddik lives by his faithfulness/faith. In the Kabbalistic reading, the tzaddik's emunah (faith, also fidelity) is the mechanism by which Or Chozer continues to ascend even when Or Yashar is maximally hidden. Yosef in the pit continues generating Or Chozer through emunah — keeping faith with his dreams — which is precisely what sustains the channel of Yesod even in galut.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the Yosef narrative is the Torah's paradigmatic account of how Yesod functions in the lower worlds: descent into concealment is not a failure of the divine system but its most concentrated expression, because it is in maximal hiddenness that Or Chozer becomes most necessary and most potent. The tzaddik's role is not simply to be righteous but to maintain the active channel of Or Chozer through faith even in the pit.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Following the Vayishlach maamar's analysis of how smallness (katnut) generates Or Chozer, the Vayeshev maamar extends this teaching to the institutional level: the tzaddik (Yesod) embodies the same principle on a cosmic scale. The descent of Yosef into the pit is structurally equivalent to Yaakov's "katanti" — a voluntary smallness that generates disproportionate ascent. The hemshech is building the case that the entire seder hishtalshelut is organized around this paradox of intentional concealment generating maximal revelation, culminating in the dira b'tachtonim.
