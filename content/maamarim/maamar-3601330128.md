## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Va'etchanan 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Va'etchanan 1909-10. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ ואתחנן, העת"ר corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Parshat Va'etchanan contains the second version of the Decalogue, the Shema, and the command "שמור את יום השבת לקדשו" (guard the Sabbath day to sanctify it). This maamar constructs a comprehensive Kabbalistic theology of Shabbat as the "seventh" sefirah from below — identifying Shabbat with the pnimiyut of Binah — and elaborates the ascending structure of all seven worlds/sefirot on Shabbat, culminating in the soul of Shabbat as the time when all levels of existence ascend beyond their ordinary modes into their source. The analysis of Shabbat "guarding" vs. "remembering" (zachor vs. shamor) provides the entry point.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with the commandment "שמור את יום השבת לקדשו" (guard/preserve the Sabbath day to sanctify it), noting the apparent redundancy: if Shabbat needs to be guarded, this implies it flows on its own and one merely needs to preserve what is already there — yet "to sanctify it" implies that the human being must actively draw holiness into it. Furthermore, the Devarim version says "shamor" (guard) while the Shemot version of the Decalogue says "zachor" (remember/invoke). Why the difference in the two versions? The maamar also raises a puzzle about why the seventh sefirah is called Binah (understanding) rather than one of the lower sefirot, and how Binah can be "seventh" when it is third from the top.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The seven-sefirot count from Yesod upward**: The Rashab explains the seemingly paradoxical counting that makes Binah the "seventh": when counting from Yesod (the ninth sefirah in standard order, but the lowest significant masculine principle) upward, passing through the seven emotive sefirot, one arrives at Binah as the seventh. This is because Malkhut (the tenth sefirah) is not counted independently — it is considered "subordinate to Z'eir Anpin" (Malkhut is the "feminine" complement of the masculine Z'eir Anpin and enters the count with it). This yields: Yesod (1), Hod (2), Netzach (3), Tiferet (4), Gevurah (5), Chesed (6), Binah (7). Therefore "Shabbat is the seventh" and is associated with Binah.
- **Binah as the "inner Shabbat"**: Binah is the sefirah of understanding — the expanded, articulated comprehension of divine wisdom. Its quality of "Shabbat" (cessation, return) is intrinsic: Binah is the mode by which the "point" of Chokhmah (pure insight, undifferentiated) is expanded into full understanding — yet it is also the mode by which all things return to their source (teshuvah, repentance, is associated with Binah). Shabbat, in the Rashab's analysis, is not merely the cessation of work but the cessation of the entire mode of "external" existence — the return of all worlds to their root in Binah (understanding, the "great repentance").
- **The ascent of all worlds on Shabbat**: The Rashab elaborates the Kabbalistic principle (from Lurianic Kabbalah and the Zohar) that on Shabbat, all worlds ascend: Asiyah rises to the level of Yetzirah; Yetzirah rises to Beriah; Beriah rises to Atzilut; Atzilut rises to the level of Adam Kadmon. This creates a "vacancy" at each level — the lower world is momentarily elevated into its higher source. The divine light that fills the level vacated by the ascending world is the special "Shabbat addition" (neshamah yeteirah — the additional Shabbat soul) that every Jewish soul receives. Shabbat is therefore the weekly re-enactment of the primordial state before the worlds descended into their current positions.
- **Shamor vs. Zachor — guarding vs. invoking**: The Rashab distinguishes "zachor" (Shemot) and "shamor" (Devarim) as two aspects of Shabbat. "Zachor" (remember/invoke) corresponds to the Or Yashar — the active invocation, the drawing down of Shabbat's holiness through speech and action (Kiddush, the Shabbat songs, the Shabbat prayers — all "zachor" acts that draw the divine light into the Shabbat). "Shamor" (guard/preserve) corresponds to the Or Chozer — the protective, containing quality that ensures the Shabbat holiness is not dissipated or "leaked." On Shabbat, one must both draw holiness down (zachor) and guard/contain the holiness once drawn (shamor). These are the two directions of the divine light in their Shabbat mode.
- **The connection between Shabbat-ascent and the Shema**: The maamar connects Shabbat's ascending structure to the Shema's declaration "ה' אלקינו ה' אחד" — two Havayot (divine Names) bracketing Elokim (as analyzed in the Shavuot maamar for 117). On Shabbat, the upper Havayah (the divine beyond the system) and the lower Havayah (the divine through the system) become more immediately accessible — the tzimtzum-limitation of the Elokim-middle becomes more transparent. The Shema recited on Shabbat is therefore not merely the same declaration as during the week but carries an additional dimension corresponding to the elevated mode of all worlds on Shabbat.
- **Binah as "seventh" and the unification of Z'eir Anpin with Malkhut**: The Rashab elaborates that when Z'eir Anpin (the six emotive sefirot) and Malkhut are in their inner unification (yichud pnimi) — their deepest pairing — they are counted as one, as "Adam echad" (one being). When they are considered as two distinct entities, they are counted separately, and Binah is the eighth. When they are one, Binah is the seventh. Shabbat is the time of their inner unification — hence Shabbat is the "seventh" in the deepest sense: the time when the masculine-feminine polarity of the divine is unified, yielding a cosmic unity (echad) that corresponds to the Shema's "one."
- **The Manna on Shabbat — the deeper meaning**: The Rashab returns (building on the Devarim maamar) to why the Manna did not descend on Shabbat: on Shabbat, the worlds ascend to their source (Binah), and the pnimiyut of Binah is what "rests" — ceases from all descent. The Manna, being the pnimiyut of divine sustenance, does not descend because all pnimiyut ascends on Shabbat. What does come on Shabbat is the makif (encompassing) quality — the Shabbat holiness that surrounds all worlds from above during the ascent.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that Shabbat is the weekly cosmic ascent by which all levels of existence return to their source in Binah (the "womb" of all divine manifestation) and, through Binah, into the Keter and Ein Sof beyond. The human observance of Shabbat — both the "zachor" (invoking, drawing down) and "shamor" (guarding, containing) — participates in and enables this cosmic ascent. The Shabbat-soul's connection to Binah is not merely metaphorical but structural: the neshamah yeteirah (additional Shabbat soul) is a direct emanation from the pnimiyut of Binah, granted to the Jewish soul during the time when Binah's own pnimiyut is most accessible.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This is one of the most comprehensive Shabbat analyses in the hemshech. It synthesizes the two-Havayah/Elokim structure (from the Shavuot maamar), the pnimiyut/makif distinction (from the Devarim maamar), and the ascending-worlds principle into a unified Kabbalistic account of Shabbat. The hemshech's project of dira betachtonim is illuminated here in a new way: Shabbat is not a pause from the project but its weekly culmination — the time when the "lower dwelling" (tachtonim) ascends most fully into the divine source, previewing the ultimate state that the hemshech projects as the telos of all creation.
