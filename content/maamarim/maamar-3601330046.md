## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Va'etchanan 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar erects the architecture of Torah as the medium through which Or Ein Sof descends through the chain of Atzilut and reaches the lower worlds — establishing that each parasha is not merely a legal or narrative text but a particular channel of divine disclosure, structured by the logic of the sefirot from Chokhmah through Malkhut. In doing so it grounds the cosmic significance of Moshe's 515 prayers specifically in the meaning of *re'iyat ha'aretz* — the "seeing of the land" — as a Kabbalistic category.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with an intricate cluster of questions about Moshe's prayer in Va'etchanan: Why does Moshe ask to *see* the land rather than enter it? Why does he preface his request with "You have begun to show Your servant Your greatness" (*atah hachilota lehara'ot*)? Why does he use the humble register of *va'etchanan* rather than *va'etpallel*? And why 515 prayers — an exact number? The strangeness of these choices signals that the plain meaning is insufficient; a deeper structural correspondence must be operative.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Adam Kadmon as intermediary**: The maamar anchors in the established teaching that Adam Kadmon (A"K) is the *ממוצע* (intermediary) between Ein Sof and the worlds — not merely their beginning, but the all-encompassing archetype that includes all worlds in a pre-manifest state. This intermediary function operates on two axes: its inner dimension (penimiyut A"K) relates to Atik/the transcendent pole, while its outer dimension (chitzoniut A"K) relates to the worlds as their general root.

- **Time and A"K**: A"K exists in a unique relation to time: within it, the worlds are not yet manifest and so time is absent, yet A"K is not entirely beyond time the way Ein Sof is. It holds time in the mode of a single unified thought (*machshavah achat*), thus serving as the mediating point between the timeless absolute and the temporal multiplicity of the worlds.

- **Torah as Chokhmah de-A"K**: The Zohar's teaching "the Holy One looked into Torah and created the world" is parsed precisely: the Torah referred to is Chokhmah de-A"K, the garment (*levush*) of Ein Sof that is not merely a radiation but contains the *mahut* (essence) of Ein Sof itself — analogous to the *mashal* (parable) in which the essence of the *nimshal* (subject) is truly embedded rather than merely alluded to. This distinguishes Chokhmah de-A"K from a mere screen (tzimtzum-generated light) and makes it an expression of *or atzmi* (essential light).

- **Each parasha as a particular divine disclosure**: The formula "Vayedaber YHVH el Moshe" that opens each Torah portion is given a precise sefirotic mapping: YHVH refers to Chokhmah de-Atzilut (which encompasses the ten sefirot), "el Moshe" refers to Yesod Abba (the channel of transmission into the sefirot), "daber el Bnei Yisrael" to Netzach and Hod, and "lemor" to Malkhut, the source of the worlds of Beriah/Yetzirah/Asiyah. Each particular parasha thus represents the Or Ein Sof drawn into a specific configuration of the sefirot, culminating in Malkhut's extension into the lower worlds.

- **Moshe's prayer and re'iyat ha'aretz**: Against this backdrop, Moshe's desire to "see the land" is not geographical but ontological: *re'iyah* (seeing) corresponds to Chokhmah, the highest level of luminous apprehension within the chain. To see Eretz Yisrael is to draw the Or Ein Sof from the highest reaches of Chokhmah de-A"K into the level of Malkhut/Asiyah — the very telos of the hemshech. The humble register (*va'etchanan*, implying supplication from below) and the enormous number of prayers (515) are fitting because this is not a request achievable through any entitlement (*tevi'ah*) but only through an Or Chozer movement: the soul drawing from its deepest root a revelation that cannot arrive by simple descent.

### What It Gets To

The maamar arrives at the conclusion that Torah study constructs and repairs the worlds because each parasha is a specific divine overflow (*hashpa'ah*) channeled through the entire sefirotic structure from Chokhmah to Malkhut. Torah scholars are called *bonim* (builders) because they are literally assembling the divine light into the architecture of the worlds. Moshe's 515 prayers for re'iyat ha'aretz were 515 attempts to accomplish a cosmic-level Or Chozer: drawing the Or Ein Sof from its highest disclosure (Chokhmah de-A"K) into the lowest level (Malkhut/Asiyah). The denial of entry was thus not punishment but ontological limit — the full drawing of that light into Asiyah awaits the *dira betachtonim* of the future.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar executes a crucial double move in the hemshech: it links the abstract metaphysics of A"K-as-intermediary to the specific practice of Torah study, and it grounds Moshe's tefillot in the Or Chozer dynamic that has been the hemshech's central subject. It shows that the cosmic structure of Or Yashar/Or Chozer is not merely a metaphysical schema but is enacted in every encounter with Torah — giving the hemshech's framework an existential, liturgical, and halakhic dimension.
