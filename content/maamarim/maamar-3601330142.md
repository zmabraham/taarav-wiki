## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Lech Lecha 1905-06** in the year **עתר"ו** (1905–06), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

**Opening kushya**: The maamar opens with two intertwined questions about the verse ויאמר ה' אל אברם לך לך מארצך וממולדתך ומבית אביך: (1) what is the meaning of the doubling "לך לך" — why say it twice? And (2) what is the significance of the three-stage enumeration "מארצך וממולדתך ומבית אביך"? These are not treated as literary curiosities but as windows into a structural teaching. The Yalkut is cited: the five letters מנצפ"ך are all doubled forms, and all of them are an expression of "לשון גאולה" — the language of redemption. In particular, the letter כ"ך is the one through which Avraham was redeemed from Ur Kasdim. This launches the question: what is the metaphysical significance of doubling such that doubled forms specifically express redemption?

**The conceptual arc**: The maamar draws on a prior teaching about the verse "כי הוי' הוא האלקים בשמים ממעל ועל הארץ מתחת" — the unity of the divine Name Havayah (associated with infinite light) and the Name Elokim (associated with tzimtzum and concealment). This unity, the maamar explains, is achieved through mitzvot ma'asiyot: physical commandments draw down אור מקיף (surrounding light) and אור פנימי (inner light), and through this the yichud of Havayah and Elokim is effected — "עיקר ההמשכה הוא ע"י העבודה למטה במעשה המצות." The maamar then develops a two-tier structure. "ועל הארץ מתחת" corresponds to the revelation of אור פנימי — the Or Pnimi that enters and illuminates the worlds through concealment (אש שחורה — black fire, light that operates through hiddenness). "ובשמים ממעל" corresponds to something higher: the revelation of the אוא"ס הבל"ג — the Ein Sof without limit, the light that preceded the tzimtzum altogether, which is אש לבנה — white fire, light that is not constrained by measure at all.

**Key Chassidic concepts invoked**: אור פנימי and אור מקיף (Pnimi and Makif), אש שחורה ואש לבנה (black and white fire from the Zoharic tradition of Torah written on black fire on white fire), ממלא כל עלמין and סובב כל עלמין (the light that fills worlds vs. the light that encompasses them), tzimtzum as generating the Reshimo, and the distinction between a measured light and the genuinely unlimited.

**Resolution — the chiddush**: The doubling of "לך לך" corresponds precisely to this two-tier structure of divine revelation: one "לך" for the level of Havayah operative within the seder hishtalshelut (the Or Pnimi, the light that fills worlds), and a second "לך" for the level of Havayah that is above the chain of worlds entirely — the אוא"ס הבל"ג. Avraham's mission was to draw down "גילוי בחי' אוא"ס הבל"ג שיהי' גילוי אור בעולם," using the metaphor of a sealed flask of balsam oil: "טלטל עצמך ממקום למקום ושמך מתגדל" — move yourself from place to place and your name becomes great, i.e., through Avraham's movement (elevation from below to above, and then drawing down from above), the boundless divine light could be revealed. The three stages of departure — "מארצך" (Netzach-Hod-Yesod, habitual drives), "ממולדתך" (Chesed-Gevurah-Tiferet, emotional character), "ומבית אביך" (Chochmah-Binah-Daat, intellectual faculty) — are the soul's progressive self-divestiture that enables the ascent to the neshamah's root and from there draws down the unbounded light. The doubling encodes geulah because redemption, by its nature, requires both the Or Pnimi (illuminating from within the world's structure) and the Or Makif (the encircling unlimited light that transcends it).
