## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Shoftim 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar uses Parshat Shoftim's preoccupation with governance, justice, and the king — including the Torah's instructions for appointing judges, kings, and the entire apparatus of social order — to develop the hemshech's analysis of Malkhut (divine kingship) and its relationship to the Or Ein Sof. The construct being built is an account of how human systems of justice and authority are theosophic structures: the earthly king is a manifestation of divine Malkhut, and the Torah's laws of governance encode the sefirotic logic by which infinite light takes on bounded, relational form.

### The Opening Move

Parshat Shoftim opens with the command *shoftim v'shotrim titen lecha* — "judges and officers you shall place for yourself." The Rashab opens with the question of why the earthly institution of judgment (mishpat) should be so central to divine service, and what Kabbalistic reality it encodes. The oddity of the verse's phrasing — "for yourself" (*lecha*) rather than simply "in your gates" — invites the deeper reading: the appointment of judges is not merely a social utility but a disclosure of divine structure.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The king and Malkhut de-Atzilut**: The Torah's law of the king (*melech*) maps onto Malkhut de-Atzilut: just as the earthly king can exercise authority only in relation to subjects, Malkhut is the Sefirah of divine kingship that exists only insofar as there are created beings to whom divinity relates. The king has no crown without subjects — *ein melech belo am*.

- **Shoftim as the structure of Or Chozer**: The institution of judgment — in which the lower realm appeals to the higher for resolution, and the higher responds with binding authority — enacts the Or Chozer dynamic structurally. Created beings, through their legal and ethical disputes, appeal upward; divine wisdom (through its earthly instrument, the judge) descends as binding light.

- **The king's law and the hemshech's overall structure**: The Torah's restrictions on the king (no excessive horses, wives, or gold; the king must write a Sefer Torah and read it daily) establish that the *melech* remains a conduit rather than an autonomous source. His authority flows through him from the divine Malkhut; any arrogation of autonomy breaks the sefirotic circuit. This maps onto the hemshech's distinction between Or Yashar (descent of light through proper channels) and the disruption caused when a vessel claims its own sovereignty.

- **Justice as cosmic repair**: The establishment of just courts in every city is understood as a distributed deployment of divine wisdom (Chokhmah/Binah) into Malkhut's domain. Every act of honest judgment is an instance of Or Yashar reaching its lowest terminus and transforming the physical world — precisely the *dira betachtonim* dynamic.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the earthly institutions of governance — courts, judges, kings — are theosophic instruments: they are the mechanisms by which divine Malkhut, the lowest Sefirah, becomes effective in the world of Asiyah. The requirement that the king write his own Sefer Torah is particularly significant: it establishes that even sovereign authority must remain tethered to Torah — the Or Ein Sof's disclosure through Chokhmah de-A"K — as its ultimate source. Governance divorced from Torah is not Malkhut at all but a counterfeit sovereignty (sitra achra).

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar extends the hemshech's social and political range: it has already established the cosmic significance of individual Torah study (Va'etchanan) and personal mitzvah practice (Eikev); now it establishes that collective human institutions — law courts, monarchy — are also encoded in the sefirotic structure. The hemshech's claim about *dira betachtonim* is shown to encompass not just the private soul but the entire architecture of civilized life.
