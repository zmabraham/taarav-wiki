## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Balak 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ בלק, העת"ר corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar, anchored in Bilaam's prophecy from Parshat Balak, constructs a Kabbalistic analysis of the two modes of love (ahavah) — love that is contingent (dependent on a reason or benefit) versus love that is essential and unconditional — and uses this distinction to elaborate the hemshech's analysis of how the neshamah's deepest root (the Yechida) is connected to the divine essence (Atzmut Ein Sof) through a bond that transcends all transactional structure.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with Bilaam's prophecy: "מי מנה עפר יעקב ומספר את רובע ישראל" — "Who has counted the dust of Jacob and the number of Israel's fourth part?" The Rashab identifies three textual difficulties: (1) why is "Jacob" paired with "dust" (afar) while "Israel" is paired with "the fourth part" (rova)? (2) The grammar suggests there is a "number" to the "fourth of Israel" — yet the intention is that there is no number; why say "ומספר" if the meaning is innumerability? (3) The Midrash in Shir HaShirim Rabbah connects this verse to the ingathering of proselytes — "just as a sack of nuts can hold additional sesame seeds," so proselytes join Israel without diminishing it. What is the connection between the verse about Israel's innumerability and the absorption of converts?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Jacob and Israel as two soul-levels**: The name "Jacob" (יעקב) corresponds to the external, struggle-based dimension of the soul — the aspect that grapples with the material world, subdues the yetzer hara (evil inclination), and works through the categories of effort and earned achievement. The name "Israel" (ישראל) corresponds to the inner, essential dimension of the soul — the Yechida-level that is directly and essentially connected to the divine, independent of the soul's particular struggles and achievements. "Dust of Jacob" is the material-world footprint of the struggle-soul; "fourth of Israel" is the soul's innermost essential quality.
- **Afar (dust) — the paradox of lowness as greatness**: Dust (afar) is the lowest material substance, yet it is precisely dust that enables growth: "ויפח באפיו נשמת חיים" — the divine breath was blown into the dust-body. The soul of Jacob, in its very lowness and earthly engagement, contains within it the possibility of infinite growth — precisely because it is "afar" (earth-dust), which receives seed and produces endlessly. The "who has counted the dust of Jacob" expresses that this potential is infinite — the more Jacob's soul is involved in the material world in holy service, the more infinite its fruits become.
- **Rova (fourth) — the Yechida's transcendent innumerability**: The "rova" (fourth part) of Israel is understood Kabbalistically as the innermost, most essential quarter of the soul — the Yechida, which corresponds to the fourth level (atzmut, essence) of the divine. This is innumerable not because it is very large but because it transcends the categories of number entirely. It is above quantity. The Yechida is connected to the Atzmut Ein Sof directly — and Ein Sof is by definition beyond all number and measure.
- **Two modes of love — ahavah ha-t'luyah b'davar vs. ahavah she-einah t'luyah b'davar**: The Rashab pivots to the central distinction of the maamar: love that is dependent on a reason (t'luyah b'davar) versus love that is independent of any reason (einah t'luyah b'davar). Love based on reason — even elevated spiritual reason, such as "I love God because He is my life-source" — is ultimately self-referential: it loves God for what God provides. This is the love of "kol libo u-nefasho" (with all one's heart and soul) — genuine and powerful, but still structured around the experience of the self. When the self perceives the benefit, the love flows; when the benefit is obscured, the love can falter. This mode of love corresponds to the Jacob-dimension of the soul.
- **The love of "v'chol me'odecha" — beyond all reason**: "V'chol me'odecha" (with all your very-ness/beyond) represents the love of the Yechida-level: love not for any reason but from the essential bond of the soul with its divine source. This love is compared by the Rashab to a son's love for a father: not because the father provides benefit, not because the relationship is pleasant, but simply because the son is the son. The bond is ontological, not experiential. This love cannot be diminished by suffering, concealment, or absence of felt divine blessing — it is the Israel-soul's direct connection to divine Atzmut.
- **The converts (gerim) as spiritual "afar"**: The Midrash's connection of the verse to converts is now explained: proselytes who join Israel become, in a sense, "dust of Jacob" — they attach to the outer, struggle-based dimension of Israel's collective soul and through that attachment gain access to the inner, essential level. Just as dust can receive and amplify: the soul of the genuine ger (convert) can, through the Jacob-soul's material struggle, eventually reach the Israel-soul's essential connection to the divine.
- **The circumcision of the heart (milat ha-lev) — the two levels**: The maamar moves into an extended analysis of "ומלתם את ערלת לבבכם" (you shall circumcise the foreskin of your heart) versus "ומל ה' אלקיך את לבבך" (God shall circumcise your heart). The first is human effort — removing the "foreskin" of grossness that covers the heart's inner love. The second is divine intervention — removing even the experienced-love itself (which, as a felt sensation, can become an obstacle to the purely essential love beyond feeling). The fully rectified soul has its heart "circumcised" by God, entering the mode of love that is purely essential, entirely beyond sensation and self-experience.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the innumerability of Israel — "who has counted the dust of Jacob, and the number of Israel's fourth" — is a statement about two infinite dimensions of the Jewish soul: the infinite potential of the Jacob-soul engaged in material-world service, and the absolute beyond-number quality of the Israel-soul's essential connection to divine Atzmut. The fullest expression of divine service is when both are active: the Jacob-dimension labors in the material world, and the Israel-dimension is connected to the divine essence beyond all labor. The circumcision of the heart — both human effort and divine gift — is the process by which the gap between these two dimensions is progressively closed.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar is one of the hemshech's most psychologically rich treatments of the soul's inner structure. By distinguishing two modes of love and grounding them in two names/levels of the Jewish collective soul (Jacob/Israel), the Rashab advances the hemshech's analysis of how the Yechida (the highest soul-level) connects to Keter and Atzmut — the highest divine level. This prepares the ground for the hemshech's later analysis of how the soul, through the deepest forms of avodah, ultimately becomes a vessel not merely for the structured divine light (the sefirot) but for the divine Essence itself.
