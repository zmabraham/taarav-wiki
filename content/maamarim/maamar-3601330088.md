## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Chayei Sara 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the concept of Keter as the intermediary between Ein Sof and the structured world of Atzilut, exploring how divine will and pleasure (ratzon and ta'anug) descend through the channel of Chayei Sara — the life of Sara — as an archetype for the neshama's sustaining root above the seder hishtalshelut.

### The Opening Move

The Rashab opens from the double expression in the parasha: "ויהיו חיי שרה מאה שנה ועשרים שנה ושבע שנים" — the years of Sara's life are enumerated three times over. The Midrashic and kabbalistic tradition notes that all her years were "equally good" (כולן שוין לטובה), prompting the question: what is the inner meaning of this equality of years, and how does it map onto the structure of the sefirot? The puzzle is not merely textual but structural — how can years that span vastly different life experiences be "equal"?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The threefold enumeration corresponds to three levels of Keter**: the outer aspect of Keter (makif rishon), the inner aspect (pnimiyut ha-Keter), and Atik Yomin itself — the "concealed of concealed." Each grouping of years in the verse points to a distinct tier of the divine crown's structure.
- **"Equal in goodness" means rooted above the seder hishtalshelut**: what is truly equal is not experiential sameness but the quality of being continuously attached to a source that does not diminish through descent. The neshama, like Sara's years, draws from a reservoir (Keter) that is not subject to progressive diminishment as one descends through the worlds.
- **The hemshech has been building toward the distinction between Or Makif and Or Penimi**: this maamar applies that distinction to the life of the neshama itself — the Or Makif (surrounding light) is precisely what makes all moments of the soul's life "equal," since it does not enter the vessels but hovers above them as a constant.
- **Proof from the structure of Chaya and Yechida**: the two highest levels of the soul correspond to Keter's two aspects (Arich Anpin and Atik). Sara, as the paradigmatic Jewish matriarch, embodies Yechida — the dimension of soul that cleaves to the Yechida of the worlds, and this is why her years could not be diminished by external circumstances.
- **The conclusion pivots to avodah**: the practical implication is that every Jew possesses this constant root, but it must be accessed through the work of ratzo — the yearning that "runs" upward past the vessels into the encircling light.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the "equality of Sara's years" is a Torah-encoded teaching about the neshama's attachment to Keter-level Or Makif, which surrounds and sustains the entire chain of worlds without being diminished by it. This establishes that the soul's highest root (Yechida) is analogous to Keter's position: present at every level, unchanged by any of them.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

At this stage of Hemshech Taarav (year 8 of the discourse, 5670/1909-10), the Rashab is deepening the analysis of how Keter's inner and outer dimensions relate to one another and to the divine light that permeates the lower worlds. This maamar contributes the specific model of the neshama's "equal years" — a living illustration that Or Makif operates above the categories of more and less, advance and decline. It prepares the ground for subsequent maamarim examining how the Or Chozer (reflected light) rises through the entire chain and reaches this same Keter-level, completing the circuit that is the hemshech's central theme.
