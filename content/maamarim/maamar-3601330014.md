## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Devarim 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar, delivered on the Shabbat of Parshat Devarim (Shabbat Chazon, the Shabbat before Tisha B'Av), builds the account of Moses's unique prophetic mode — *aspaklaria ha-me'irah* (the luminous lens) — as distinct from all other prophecy, and uses this distinction to establish the structural relationship between Or Yashar in its most direct, undimmed form and the graduated Or Yashar that descends through the chain of worlds. Moses's prophetic mode is the analog of Chochmah (undimmed point-light) while all other prophecy is the analog of Binah and below (light spread into expanded vessels).

### The Opening Move

Parshat Devarim opens Moses's great valedictory address to Israel before his death: "These are the words that Moses spoke to all of Israel, on the far side of the Jordan..." (Devarim 1:1). The Talmudic tradition (Yevamot 49b) identifies Moses as the prophet who saw through a *luminous lens* (aspaklaria ha-me'irah), while all other prophets saw through a *non-luminous lens* (aspaklaria she-einah me'irah). The maamar's entry question: what is the structural basis of this distinction? Why does Moses's mode of divine communication have qualitatively more light than all other prophets? And what does it mean that Devarim — the book of *Moshe's own words* (more than any other book) — is considered Torah from Sinai (Torah min ha-shamayim)?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The aspaklaria distinction mapped onto Chochmah vs. Binah**: Moses's luminous-lens prophecy corresponds to Chochmah — the *nekudah* (point) of undimmed divine wisdom where the light is most concentrated and pure, before it spreads into the vessels of Binah's understanding. All other prophets operated through Binah and below — the light reached them through the graduated chain, somewhat diminished at each level. The "words that Moses spoke" in Devarim are therefore not merely human rhetoric but a Chochmah-level emanation channeled through a human mouth.

- **The paradox of Devarim**: Devarim is the book where Moses speaks most in his own voice — "these are the words that Moses spoke." Yet this is the book most identified with *Torah she-ba'al peh* (the Oral Torah's root), because it is precisely at the Chochmah level that Or Yashar and the divine will are most directly present. The "words" of Devarim are or-words — light-speech before it has been fully contracted into the graduated vessel-structure of explicit commandment. This makes them both the most "human-sounding" and the most divinely luminous simultaneously.

- **The Or Yashar descent from Moses to Israel**: Moses as the supreme channel of Or Yashar represents the critical juncture in the cosmic chain — the point where the Or Yashar that has descended from Ein Sof through Keter, through the Sefirot of Atzilut, through Beriah, Yetzirah, now arrives at the level of the human soul (neshamah) and is transmitted as Torah. Moses himself is the "kav" within the human domain — the line of transmission that does not yet lose the infinite quality of its source, because his aspaklaria is luminous.

- **The relationship to Or Chozer**: precisely because Moses is the Or Yashar conduit par excellence, his valedictory address — his final transmission before death — is also the moment of greatest Or Chozer potential. Death (and specifically Moses's death as the selfless final act of a life of transmission) is the analog of the greatest Or Chozer: the total return of the conduit's light back to its source. "On the far side of the Jordan" — Moses standing at the border he could not cross — is the image of Or Yashar at its terminal point, having descended as far as it can go, now turning toward Or Chozer.

- **Tisha B'Av (the Shabbat Chazon context)**: the maamar is delivered on Shabbat Chazon, which carries the prophetic vision of Isaiah — "the vision of Isaiah" — the most direct prophetic sight after Moses. The maamar likely engages this: Shabbat Chazon's "seeing" (chazon) hints at the latent Or Mekif light of Redemption that will ultimately restore the Beit HaMikdash, but this Or Mekif is currently surrounding-without-entering, precisely because the Or Pnimi channel (the Temple's continuous service) has been destroyed. The tension between Or Mekif's presence and Or Pnimi's absence is the theological charge of Shabbat Chazon.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes Moses's unique structural role in the cosmic chain as the Chochmah-level conduit of Or Yashar to humanity — the point where infinite divine light becomes the particular words of Torah without losing its infinite quality. The book of Devarim is therefore the most concentrated embodiment of Or Yashar at the human level. This conclusion opens the hemshech toward its subsequent analysis of how Or Yashar penetrates through the chain of worlds into the physical domain.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Having completed the summer parashiyot with their analysis of Sefirot-internal structure and graduated descent, the hemshech now pivots at Devarim to the figure of Moses as the human analog of the Or Yashar conduit. This shift — from cosmic structure to the prophetic human soul — is the first move toward the hemshech's eventual treatment of how the Nefesh HaElohit (divine soul) relates to the Or Yashar it carries within itself during divine service.
