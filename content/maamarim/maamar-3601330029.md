## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Toldot 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Toldot 1902-03. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ תולדות, תער"ג corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs the distinction between the *orot* (lights) and *kelim* (vessels) of the sefirot as a pair of mutually irreducible ontological categories — *ayin* (nothingness) and *yesh* (somethingness) — and maps this distinction onto the genealogical paradox of *"Eleh toldot Yitzchak ben Avraham, Avraham holid et Yitzchak"*. The redundancy in the verse (why mention both "son of Abraham" and "Abraham begot") is shown to map precisely onto the distinction between the sefirot-lights (which are *ayin*, radically self-effacing) and the sefirot-vessels (which are *yesh*, entities with their own form).

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with the exegesis attributed to the Rema (*R. Moshe Isserles*) on the word *ve-eleh* (with vav, meaning "and these") vs. *eleh* (without vav, "these"). Rabbinic tradition holds that *eleh* negates what came before, while *ve-eleh* (with vav) adds to it. The question: *Toldot Yitzchak* opens with *ve-eleh* — meaning it *adds to* the previous genealogy (Ishmael's). But what is being added? The apparent answer (Esav adds to Ishmael's negative legacy) seems backwards — it would be more honorable to say *eleh* (Yaakov negates/supersedes Ishmael). The deeper question is what the verse is structurally doing by mentioning both *Yitzchak ben Avraham* and *Avraham holid et Yitzchak*.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The sefirot as revealing the sefirot nistar (hidden sefirot)**: Building on the previous maamar's *sippur* analysis, the Rashab now develops the claim that the sefirot-orot reveal their own hidden roots — the *sefirot ha-genuzot* (the hidden sefirot that are the *machshavot nistarot*, hidden thoughts of Ein Sof). The visible sefirot (the ten sefirot of Atzilut that participate in the world) are *mekubalot* (received) from hidden primordial sources that do not themselves manifest in the world-structure.

- **Orot as ayin, kelim as yesh**: The fundamental distinction: the *orot* (lights within the sefirot) are categorized as *ayin* — they have no self-standing existence (*metziyut le-atzmo*), but exist only as *giluy ha-atzem* (revelation of the source). The *kelim* (vessels) are *yesh* — they have their own defined form, their own "face," their own ontological identity. This ayin/yesh distinction is not a value-judgment (yesh is not inferior) but an ontological one: ayin is the transparent, self-nullifying mode; yesh is the self-presenting mode.

- **Yaakov and Esav as or-and-kli**: The Rashab maps the two sons onto this distinction. Yaakov, *ish tam yoshev ohalim* (a wholesome man dwelling in tents), represents the *ayin*-mode of the orot: transparent, self-effacing, absorbed in Torah (which is itself associated with the *orot* of the sefirot). Esav, the hunter, man of the field, represents the *yesh*-mode of the kelim: self-presenting, form-making, tangible in the world. Both are generated by *Yitzchak ben Avraham* — both the ayin and yesh dimensions of the sefirot derive from the same source.

- **"Avraham holid et Yitzchak" — the second mention**: The verse's repetition of "Avraham begot Yitzchak" (after already saying "Yitzchak son of Abraham") is read as corresponding to the kli-dimension of the sefirot. *Ben Avraham* ("son of Abraham") captures the *or*-relationship: Yitzchak as the *giluy* (revelation) of Abraham, the way an or is the giluy of its source. *Avraham holid* ("Abraham generated") captures the *kli*-relationship: the generation of a *yesh* entity (a son) with its own separate existence. The verse thus encodes both or/ayin and kli/yesh in its seemingly redundant formulation.

- **Ve-eleh adding Esav to Ishmael**: The paradox resolves: the *ve-eleh* (adding Esav to Ishmael) is not ethically comparing them but ontologically stating that the *toldot* of Yitzchak include *both* the ayin-mode (Yaakov/or) and the yesh-mode (Esav/kli). A complete sefirot-system requires both the transparent ayin-lights and the self-presenting yesh-vessels. Esav corresponds to the kli-dimension that is added to the already-present or-dimension; this addition is what makes the system complete.

- **The heavens as proof of Ein Sof**: The maamar includes an extended analysis of how the heavens (*shamayim*) reveal the divine through their properties: their sustained existence without entropy, their continuous circular motion, their internally contradictory composition (fire and water in the Zohar's phrase *"she-mayim*"), their coordinated movement toward a single purpose despite having separate intelligences. All of these are proof of *koach ha-Ein Sof* — the Infinite Power — operating through them. The heavens are thus the macrocosmic model of the sefirot's *sippur/sapir* function: the cosmos tells (*mesaper*) of God by displaying what no finite power could sustain.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes the ayin/yesh ontological pair as the deepest description of the sefirot's internal structure. The orot are ayin — they are nothing in themselves, existing only as transparent revelations of their source. The kelim are yesh — they are genuine entities with form and identity. The toldot (generations/consequences) of a complete divine structure must include both — Yaakov (ayin/or) and Esav (yesh/kli) — because a world capable of receiving and revealing Or Ein Sof needs both the transparent receiving medium and the form-giving vessel.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Maamarim 27–28 developed the three meanings of *sefirah* and the kli/levush distinction. This maamar now provides the deepest conceptual grounding for the or/kli relationship by introducing the *ayin/yesh* pair. This pair will be indispensable for the hemshech's later analysis of how the Tzimtzum relates to the orot (which were never actually affected by it, since they are ayin) vs. the kelim (which were partially affected, since they are yesh). The ayin/yesh distinction is thus not merely an exegetical tool but a fundamental ontological axis of the entire hemshech.
