## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Chukat 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the account of *Ma'aleh u'Matah* — "above and below," the structure of gradation within the Sefirot — establishing that the hierarchy of the Sefirot is not a hierarchy of being or substance but a hierarchy of *light-quantity* (rov u'mi'ut ha-or), and that this graduated diminishment of light is precisely what enables the lower Sefirot and worlds to exist as distinct entities rather than being absorbed back into their sources.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens from the verse in Parshat Chukat (Numbers 19:1-2): "God spoke to Moses and Aaron saying: This is the statute of the Torah which God commanded, saying..." — and raises the double-attribution of command: the verse already states "God spoke" (introducing the speaker), so why does it also say "which God commanded" (אשר צוה ה')? Why not "which *I* command" (אשר אנכי מצוה)? And why does the Parah Adumah (Red Heifer) — whose ritual is performed entirely *outside* the sanctuary, in contrast to all other offerings performed *inside* — receive the designation "statute of the Torah" (חקת התורה), the most trans-rational legal category? These textual questions prime the maamar's structural analysis: outside/inside, above/below.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The foundation**: the preceding maamar established that the *sefirot ha-genuzot* (hidden Sefirot in Ein Sof) were brought into manifest existence through the Tzimtzum. The Tzimtzum's function is the individualization of each Sefirah — allowing Chochmah to be distinctly Chochmah, which necessarily involves a diminishment of the infinite light that was present in their fused pre-Tzimtzum state.

- **The analogy of letters forming a word** (from the previous maamar) is now extended: when a single letter is separated from a word (e.g., one letter of ברוך extracted alone), it loses the word's semantic insight (השכלה) even though it is the same letter — because the comprehensive power (כח כללי, general force) of the word depended on all letters together. Similarly, each Sefirah as it stands individually has less light than when all were in their fused pre-Tzimtzum state. The diminishment is a condition of individuation, not a flaw.

- **Ma'aleh u'Matah (above and below) is defined rigorously**: it is not a hierarchy of ontological dignity in the abstract but a hierarchy of *light-richness*. Chochmah is "above" Binah not because it is more divine in some absolute sense but because more light is present within it (rov ha-or); Binah is "below" because the light is somewhat contracted (mi'ut ha-or) relative to Chochmah. The emotional Sefirot (Middot) are further below because the light is further contracted. This is a *quantitative* gradient of divine luminosity, not a qualitative hierarchy of divine substance.

- **The necessity of diminishment for hierarchical structure**: if every Sefirah had equal light, there would be no gradation, no ma'aleh u'matah, and consequently no *hishtalshelut* (chain of descent), because the lower Sefirot need to be less brilliant than the upper ones in order for the transition from one to the next to be meaningful — and for them to eventually give rise to created worlds that stand as genuinely distinct from Atzilut. A Malkhut with the same light-richness as Keter could not serve as the font of Beriah.

- **The Parah Adumah** is read against this framework: its performance *outside* the sanctuary (chuts) corresponds to the Or Mekif dimension — the surrounding light that does not enter the inner sanctum (the Or Pnimi domain). The Red Heifer purifies those who are tamei (spiritually contaminated) by drawing on the encompassing light from above the hierarchical inner system. This is why it is a *chok* (statute beyond rational comprehension) — it works through the supra-rational Or Mekif dimension that transcends the graduated light-structure of the inner Sefirot.

- **The double-command structure** in the verse (*God spoke... which God commanded*) is resolved: the first attribution (*God spoke to Moses*) corresponds to the Or Pnimi transmission through Moses as the inner conduit; the second (*which God commanded*) introduces the Or Mekif dimension — the statute's authority derives from a level that transcends the graduated prophetic transmission and comes from Ein Sof's direct will (Keter/Ratzon), which is why it reads as if a separate command from above the chain.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that within the Sefirot, "above" and "below" mean specifically "more light" and "less light" — and that this graduated diminishment is not incidental but is the structural condition that makes the chain of worlds possible. Without mi'ut ha-or (diminishment of light) at each level, there is no ma'aleh u'matah, and without ma'aleh u'matah there is no hishtalshelut — no path from Ein Sof to Asiyah. The Or Mekif (Parah Adumah, chuts, the "also commanded") transcends this graduated structure.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar is the hemshech's first systematic account of what "above and below" means within the Sefirot — an essential clarification that the discourse requires before it can proceed to analyze the chain of worlds (hishtalshelut). It moves the discourse from the internal structure of Keter and the Lights-Vessels relationship to the *dynamics* of descent and gradation, setting up the analysis of Or Yashar (direct light descending) that becomes the hemshech's primary concern through its middle sections.
