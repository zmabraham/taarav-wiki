## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Shabbat Chol HaMoed Sukkot 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Shabbat Chol HaMoed Sukkot 5670, develops the principle of "אנת הוא אחד" (You are One) — the affirmation of divine unity found in the Zohar's Shabbat prayer — as the framework for understanding how Sukkot's festival of joy (שמחה) functions as the soul's deepest access to divine unity, and why the Sukkah specifically, rather than any other physical structure, is designated as the vehicle for this experience.

### The Opening Move
The special Shabbat of Chol HaMoed Sukkot occupies a unique structural position: it is simultaneously Shabbat (the temporal dimension of Keter) and within Sukkot (the festival of physical joy and ingathering). The Rashab opens with the Zohar's formula "אנת הוא אחד ולא בחושבן" — "You are One, but not in the numerical sense" — from the Shabbat Musaf prayer of Tefillat HaShabat. The question: what does it mean to say God is "One but not numerically"? All oneness we can grasp is numerical (the opposite of two); how do we affirm a unity that transcends this?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **"One but not numerical": the distinction between counted unity and essential unity.** The Rashab draws the distinction between a unity that exists by contrast (one as opposed to two) — which is always vulnerable to being superseded — and the unity of the Ein Sof, which is not "one" within a field of possible plurality but is the only reality such that plurality itself is merely a surface appearance. This distinction maps precisely onto the hemshech's earlier analysis of yichuda ila'ah (higher unity) vs. yichuda tata'ah (lower unity).
- **Or Ein Sof permeating all worlds.** On Shabbat Chol HaMoed Sukkot, the Or Ein Sof permeates the worlds more fully than on ordinary days — both because of Shabbat's Keter-dimension and because of Sukkot's specific quality of divine joy descending into material reality. The Rashab argues that this is precisely when the non-numerical unity of God becomes most accessible: the or that fills all worlds is not the same or that flows through the graduated sefirot, but the essential divine light that is present in all places equally and without mediation.
- **Simcha (joy) and bittul (nullification) as correlates.** The Rashab develops an important phenomenological point: true שמחה (joy) is not an emotional excitement that runs through the external faculties but a state that arises when the internal self drops its defenses and opens directly to its source. This is structurally identical to ביטול (self-nullification) — and indeed the Rashab argues that genuine simcha and genuine bittul are not opposites but the same movement described from two vantage points. Joy is what ביטול feels like from the inside.
- **The Sukkah as divine encompassment.** The Sukkah's structure is distinctive: unlike a house (which is built to contain and protect the self from the outside), the Sukkah is built to encompass — the self sits inside but is surrounded by something that is "other" — the Sukkah walls and schach (roof) that are explicitly temporary, permeable to sky and stars. This physical structure enacts the mystical state of "divine encompassment" (סובב כל עלמין) — the divine that surrounds and encompasses all worlds without becoming internalized. Sitting in the Sukkah is sitting within the divine encompassment.
- **Sukkot as אחדות and ריבוי simultaneously.** One of Sukkot's defining qualities is the seventy bull sacrifices offered in the Temple — representing the seventy nations, the maximal multiplicity. Yet simultaneously the holiday is about ingathering (אסיפה) — the harvest gathered into one. The Rashab reads this as the hemshech's structural duality in its most explicit form: a unity (אחדות) that does not suppress multiplicity (ריבוי) but contains it without contradiction — the non-numerical oneness of "אנת הוא אחד."

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that the Sukkah's physical encompassment is the bodily enactment of the non-numerical divine unity: by sitting within the Sukkah, the Jew is placed within the dimension of Or Ein Sof that surrounds all worlds — not the Or that flows through the sefirot and becomes differentiated, but the encompassing Or that is equally present everywhere. Shabbat Chol HaMoed Sukkot is thus the climactic moment of the Tishrei season: the Or Ein Sof, accessed via the Yechida on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, is now physically present in the wooden walls and leafy roof of a temporary hut.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar delivers one of the hemshech's most developed treatments of the distinction between Or HaSovev (encompassing light) and Or HaMemale (permeating light) in the context of lived religious experience. The Tishrei arc is completing: ascent to Keter (Rosh Hashanah / Yom Kippur) → transition through Moshe's Torah-transmission (Haazinu) → descent into embodied physical joy (Sukkot) → culmination in the encompassing divine unity (Shabbat Chol HaMoed) → and then Shemini Atzeret / Simchat Torah, which will conclude the festival season with a new understanding of the relationship between Torah and the divine.
