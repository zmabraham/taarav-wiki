## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Beha'alotcha 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Parshat Beha'alotcha centers on Aaron's kindling of the Menorah ("When you raise up the lights..."), the Pesach Sheni (second Passover for those who were impure), the silver trumpets, and the journeys of the Israelites in the wilderness. This maamar uses the Menorah-kindling — specifically the command to raise each flame until it burns on its own ("ad sheyadelok ha-shalhevet me'eilehah") — as the lens for developing the hemshech's concept of how Or Chozer operates within the Sefirot themselves, not only between the human and divine realms.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens on the phrase "בהעלותך את הנרות" — when you lift up the lights. The linguistic choice of "lifting up" (ha'ala'ah) rather than simply "lighting" (hadlakah) is the textual crux. The Talmudic teaching is that Aaron was to light the Menorah until the flame "ascended on its own" — the human action initiates but then steps back, allowing the flame its own independence. What is the deeper meaning of this autonomy? How can a flame "light itself"? And why is the priest's role to raise, not merely to ignite? Additionally, the maamar takes up the Menorah's seven-branched structure and what each branch represents in the sefirot-architecture.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The flame's self-arising**: The Rashab distinguishes two phases in the Menorah-kindling: the initial lighting (the human kohen applies fire to the wick) and the subsequent "self-burning" (the flame burns independently, fed by its own oil). In Kabbalistic terms, the initial lighting corresponds to Or Yashar — divine light that descends and initiates. The sustained burning corresponds to Or Chozer — the lamp's own upward-burning, fed by the oil of the vessel, which now sustains itself. The kohen's task of "lifting up" is to ensure the transition from the first phase to the second: to bring the divine light to the point where it ignites the vessel's own light, which then ascends independently.
- **The seven lamps as seven sefirot**: The seven lamps of the Menorah correspond to the seven lower sefirot (Chesed through Malkhut). Each lamp has its own wick and oil but all are fed from the same central shaft (the middle lamp corresponding to Da'at/Yesod, the axis). The Rashab analyzes how the Menorah's geometry — all lamps facing the center — indicates that Or Chozer from all seven sefirot is directed toward the central axis, which then conducts it upward toward the Keter.
- **Aaron's unique qualification**: Aaron as High Priest is identified with the sefirah of Chesed and with the aspect of pnimiyut (interiority/love) in divine service. His role in lighting the Menorah is therefore not incidental: Chesed is the sefirah through which Or Yashar primarily descends and the one most aligned with the "outward flow" of divine beneficence. For Or Chozer to ascend properly, it must be initiated by the love-quality of Chesed — by service that originates in ahavah (love) rather than merely obligation.
- **"When you raise up" as a general principle of avodah**: The Rashab reads "be-ha'alotcha" (when you raise up) as a broader principle of all divine service: the human being's role is not to "produce" the divine light but to "raise up" what is already present latently within the world and within the soul. The oil was already in the lamp; the kohen's task was to ignite it. Similarly, the divine light (the Yechida-level of the neshamah, the Or Ein Sof within every created thing) is always already present — the task of avodah is to raise it from latency to actuality.
- **Pesach Sheni and the second chance**: The maamar connects the Menorah-narrative to the Pesach Sheni episode (those who were impure or distant offered a second Passover sacrifice a month later). The principle of Pesach Sheni is that there is always a "second chance" for Or Chozer — no one is permanently excluded from the upward ascent. The very fact that the Torah provides this alternative demonstrates that Or Chozer is not dependent on perfect initial conditions but on the ongoing will to ascend. This reinforces the hemshech's theme that the circuit between divine and human is self-correcting.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that "be-ha'alotcha" — the act of lifting — is the fundamental metaphor for all Or Chozer: the human being, through avodah rooted in love (Aaron's quality), ignites latent divine light within the world so that it ascends on its own. Once properly kindled, the soul's Or Chozer is self-sustaining, fed by the "oil" of the soul's own substance. This is the full realization of the hemshech's thesis: the lower realm doesn't merely receive Or Yashar passively — it generates its own ascending light that completes the divine circuit.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar brings the hemshech's analysis of Or Chozer to a new level of refinement: Or Chozer is not merely a "reflection" of Or Yashar (as if it were secondary) but has its own autonomous burning, fed by the vessel's own oil. This is a crucial distinction — it means the lower realms are not merely passive recipients and reflectors of divine light but active generators of an ascending light that has its own sustaining power. This sets up the later sections of the hemshech that will deal with the soul's independent spiritual capacity and the concept of "inner service" (avodah pnimit).
