## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Toldot 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs the distinction between two modes of divine service that mirror the two sons of Yitzchak — the avodah of Yaakov (representing the inner, essential divine light, Or Pnimi) and the avodah of Eisav (representing an externally-oriented striving that fails to penetrate to the core) — and uses this to differentiate between two structural levels within the soul's engagement with Keter.

### The Opening Move

The parasha opens the Jacob-Esau dynamic with the famous verse "ויתרוצצו הבנים בקרבה" — the children struggled within her womb — and then the divine declaration "ורב יעבד צעיר" (the elder shall serve the younger). The Rashab seizes on a textual puzzle: in what sense does Eisav, who appears to have the vigor and primacy of the firstborn, "serve" Yaakov, and how does this inversion encode a teaching about the order of spiritual worlds and the correct path of avodah?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The firstborn/younger inversion mirrors Or Yashar vs. Or Chozer**: Eisav, as the firstborn, corresponds to Or Yashar — the first, direct emanation that carries raw intensity but lacks the refinement of return. Yaakov, the younger, corresponds to Or Chozer — the light that ascends from below, which paradoxically achieves more because it reaches back toward the source.
- **"Two nations in your womb" — the structural duality within Keter itself**: The hemshech has been analyzing how Keter contains two aspects: Arich Anpin (the revealed, patient dimension of the divine will) and Atik Yomin (the utterly concealed, primordial delight). These two aspects struggle within the divine order, and Yitzchak — who embodies Gevura and the contraction of light — is the vessel in which this struggle occurs.
- **Yitzchak's love for Eisav points to the trapping/gevura dynamic**: The Rashab reads Yitzchak's love for Eisav ("כי ציד בפיו") as pointing to the way that gevura-energy (the principle of contraction and boundary) naturally "loves" the external, bounded form — a key reason why the seder hishtalshelut produces worlds that appear separate from their source.
- **Rivka's understanding of the deeper truth corresponds to Bina**: Rivka, corresponding to Bina (the supernal mother), perceives that the true carrier of the divine intention is Yaakov — the inner principle — and engineers the blessing accordingly. This parallels how Bina, the womb of all the sefirot, ensures that Or Chozer ultimately reaches the higher levels.
- **The blessings of Yitzchak as a model of how Or Makif descends into the world**: The elaborate blessings that Yitzchak bestows represent the mechanism by which Keter's hidden content becomes actualized in the lower worlds — not through direct descent (which would overwhelm the vessels) but through the mediating structure of the patriarch's blessing.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the seeming reversal — younger serves as the spiritual master over the elder — is not an anomaly but the very structure of the divine order. Or Chozer (Yaakov's mode) achieves an ascent that Or Yashar (Eisav's mode) cannot, because the reflective movement reaches into the source while the direct flow merely descends. This is the deeper meaning of "ורב יעבד צעיר": the "elder" (primary, direct) light ultimately serves the purpose of the "younger" (reflective, ascending) light.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar is a crucial bridge in the Taarav's analysis of Or Yashar and Or Chozer. By grounding the two modes of divine flow in the archetypal figures of the Torah, the Rashab moves the discourse from abstract Kabbalistic mapping to narrative theology. The Jacob-Esau framework will reappear throughout subsequent maamarim as a touchstone for understanding why Or Chozer — the light generated by human avodah — holds a higher spiritual position than Or Yashar, the automatic flow of divine grace from above.
