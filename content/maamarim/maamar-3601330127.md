## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Devarim 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Parshat Devarim opens Sefer Devarim (Deuteronomy), which in the Kabbalistic tradition is called "Mishneh Torah" — the "second Torah" or "repeated Torah." This maamar constructs a Kabbalistic account of what "Mishneh Torah" actually means: the relationship between the first four Books of the Torah (in which God speaks in the third person, "God spoke to Moshe") and the fifth Book (in which Moshe speaks in the first person, "I pleaded to God"). The maamar uses the distinction between Mishneh Torah's mode of speech and the earlier Books to elaborate the hemshech's analysis of the relationship between Keter (the highest Sefirah, corresponding to God's direct speech) and Malkhut (the lowest Sefirah, corresponding to the emanated, structured divine word that can be received below).

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with the verse "אלה הדברים אשר דבר משה אל כל ישראל" (These are the words that Moses spoke to all Israel) and raises multiple textual-structural difficulties: (1) The four earlier Books of the Torah consistently use third-person narration ("God spoke to Moses saying...") — as if a third party is reporting the divine speech. Devarim switches to first person from Moses ("I pleaded to God," "I commanded you") — as if Moses himself is the author speaking. What is the significance of this shift? (2) Why is Devarim called "Mishneh Torah" (repetition/second Torah) — it repeats many laws already given in the earlier Books? What is gained by the repetition? (3) The very first words "אלה הדברים" use "dvarim" (words/matters) rather than "Torah" (teaching) — why?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The Man (the מן, manna) and its non-descent on Shabbat as the entry point**: The maamar enters through a surprising path — explaining why the manna did not fall on Shabbat. The manna was "pnimiyut" (inner dimension) food — spiritual nourishment from the level of Atzilut that descended in a physical garment. On Shabbat, the inner dimensions ascend; the pnimiyut of all worlds rises upward. Because the manna was from pnimiyut, it could not descend on Shabbat — the very nature of Shabbat as an ascending day prevented the descent of pnimiyut-nourishment. What does descend on Shabbat is the makif (encompassing) quality — not the pnimiyut (inner) light.
- **The two modes of Torah-speech**: This distinction (pnimiyut vs. makif on Shabbat) is the key to Mishneh Torah. The four earlier Books represent "or pnimiyut" (inner light) — Torah as received from the divine pnimiyut, transmitted through the channel of the kav from Ein Sof downward through all the worlds, ultimately "dressed" in the third-person narrative of hishtalshelut. Devarim represents "or makif" (encompassing light) — Torah as encompassing rather than penetrating, spoken from the level of the divine makif that envelops all worlds from outside rather than flowing through them.
- **Moses as the vessel for makif-Torah**: Moses speaking in first person in Devarim is not a departure from divine authority but its highest expression: Moshe attained the level of da'at elyon (supernal knowledge) and bittul such that his own speech became transparent to the divine speech. In the earlier Books, the "God spoke to Moses" formula indicates that the divine light flowed through Moses as a channel (tzinor); in Devarim, the divine light encompassed Moses so totally that Moses' own speech became the outer expression of the encompassing divine will. This is the Or Makif becoming Or Pnimiyut through the vehicle of the highest human vessel.
- **Mishneh Torah as translation between levels**: The "repetition" of Devarim is not redundant but constitutes a translation of the Torah from one mode of existence to another. The earlier Books are Torah as it exists in the mode of hishtalshelut — the pnimiyut descending through the chain of worlds. Devarim is Torah as it exists in the mode of atzmut — the encompassing divine essence expressing itself through the vessel of Moshe. The laws that appear in both are the "same" laws only on the surface; at a deeper level, the Mishneh Torah version of each law contains an additional dimension corresponding to the makif-level of the divine will.
- **"Eleh ha-Dvarim" — words that become Torah**: The use of "dvarim" (words/matters) rather than "Torah" in the opening is intentional. "Dvarim" points to the raw, unmediated speech-act — the word before it becomes structured teaching. This corresponds to the Or Makif level: the encompassing light is "unstructured" from the perspective of the structured worlds below, arriving as pure word before becoming articulated system. When Moses spoke "eleh ha-dvarim" — these are the raw words — he was accessing the pre-structural level of Torah.
- **The Keter-connection of Mishneh Torah**: The Rashab connects Mishneh Torah to Keter specifically: just as Keter is the "boundary" between the infinite Ein Sof and the structured world of Sefirot — simultaneously inside and outside the system — Devarim is the "boundary" of the Torah, simultaneously inside the system (it repeats and applies the laws) and outside it (it speaks from the encompassing, makif level). The Sefer Devarim is thus the Torah's own Keter.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that Mishneh Torah represents a qualitatively different level of Torah: not the inner, pnimiyut light flowing through the chain of worlds (as in the first four Books) but the encompassing, makif light that surrounds and contains all worlds speaking directly. This "second Torah" is the Torah as it exists at the level of Keter — encompassing, above all internal differentiation, expressing pure divine will. The shift to Moses' first-person speech signals not human authorship but the highest possible level of human-divine transparency, where the vessel becomes fully aligned with the encompassing divine will.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The hemshech is now entering the Book of Devarim, and this maamar reframes the entire approach: the summer-fall cycle of maamarim from Devarim onward will draw on the makif dimension of Torah — not the structured inner light of the earlier Books but the encompassing divine will that speaks directly. This corresponds to the hemshech's own progression: after years of building the inner structure of the Kabbalistic system (the pnimiyut approach), the hemshech now shifts to exploring how the encompassing divine light (Keter-level) relates to that structure — a shift from "how does divine light flow through the system" to "how does the divine essence that transcends and encompasses the system express itself."
