## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Shelach 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: The spies sent to scout the Land returned with conflicting reports — ten with a discouraging report and two (Caleb and Joshua) with an encouraging one. The Talmud discusses how Shammai and Hillel disputed matters of Jewish law with Shammai ruling strictly and Hillel leniently. Is there a connection between the spies and these great Torah sages?

**Central Concept**: The relationship between Chochmah (Wisdom) and Gevurah/Chesed: Shammai's approach from Chochmah/Gevurah (constriction, strictness) versus Hillel's from Chesed/understanding (expansion, leniency). How divine service rooted in Gevurah leads to stricter interpretations while service rooted in Chesed leads to more lenient ones — yet both are expressions of the same divine truth from different sefirot-perspectives.

**Resolution**: The difference between Shammai and Hillel reflects the difference between two authentic modes of apprehending divine truth: Shammai from the faculty of Gevurah/restriction produces halakhic strictness; Hillel from the faculty of Chesed/expansion produces leniency. Both are 'words of the living God' (דברי אלהים חיים) because they reflect different Sefirot-level refractions of the same divine light.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the account of how two authentic and opposing halakhic positions can both be "words of the living God" (דברי אלקים חיים) by grounding the Shammai-Hillel dispute in the structure of Lights-in-Vessels: each sage apprehended the same divine truth through the "coloring" of his sefirot-vessel (Gevurah or Chesed), and the Light itself — the divine wisdom received from their shared teacher — was the same in both cases, only differentiated at the vessel-level.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens from Parshat Shelach's narrative of the spies and pivots immediately to the Shammai-Hillel halakhic disputes. The question it raises is structural: if Shammai ruled from Gevurah (strictness) and Hillel from Chesed (leniency), yet both received the same tradition from their teacher, what explains the divergence? And more pressingly: since both positions cannot be simultaneously correct on any given halakhic question, how can both be "words of the living God"? This is not merely a question about rabbinic hermeneutics — it is a question about the relationship between a single divine truth and its multiple refractions through different sefirot-vessels.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The spies' dual report** (encouraging vs. discouraging) is read as the narrative instantiation of the same phenomenon: Caleb and Joshua apprehended the Land through a vessel of Chesed (expansive possibility), while the ten apprehended it through a vessel of Gevurah (constricting assessment). The land was the same; the vessels of apprehension differed.

- **The analogy of light passing through colored glass** is implicit in the source text's account: water placed in a colored vessel takes the vessel's hue. But the maamar is more precise than this simple analogy — the water (light) is genuinely transformed in the vessel's apprehension (not merely appearing different from outside), since the halakhic conclusion Hillel draws really is a ruling of Chesed and Shammai's really is a ruling of Gevurah.

- **The Light/Vessel distinction is the crucial mechanism**: the Lights (the divine wisdom transmitted by the teacher, the *svarah* — logical argument — as it existed in its source) contain Chesed and Gevurah only in a fused, pre-differentiated state. The Vessels (the individual sage's mode of apprehension) cause the differentiation. This is why the same argument (svarah) can produce two halakhic conclusions: not because either sage erred, but because the Light's inherent ambiguity (containing both tendencies in fusion) becomes actualized differently through each vessel.

- **The case of Shammai ruling leniently and Hillel strictly** (which occasionally occurs in the Talmud) is deployed to validate this model: if Shammai were simply "the Gevurah sage," he could never rule leniently; but the theory of Lights-switching-Vessels (אחליפו דוכתייהו — "they exchanged their places," the Talmudic idiom) explains this: sometimes the light of Chesed passes through Shammai's Gevurah vessel, or vice versa. This is only possible because the Light is not identical with its usual Vessel.

- **The four parshiyot of Tefillin** are introduced as a further proof: the four compartments are associated with Chochmah, Binah, Chesed, and Gevurah — yet Chesed and Gevurah are normally Middot (emotions), not Mohin (intellect). The explanation is that when Chesed and Gevurah are operating as *Mohin* (intellectual principles rather than emotional drives), it is precisely when their Lights (still in their pre-differentiated state) have not yet become fully actualized into their Vessels. The Light of Chesed in the Mohin-state is not the emotive act of kindness but the intellectual *tendency toward* expansion — and at that level, it can occasionally exchange with the Gevurah-tendency.

- **The three levels of or-mayim-raki'a** (light-water-firmament) from Kabbalah are cited to map the progression of a teaching from the teacher's mind through transmission to the student's crystallized understanding — at the "water" level the teaching is still fluid and undifferentiated; it is only at the "firmament" (raki'a, the student's crystallized grasp) that the Chesed/Gevurah split fully actualizes.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that the halakhic plurality of authentic opposing rulings is not a defect of rabbinic transmission but a structural feature of how divine truth (a Light containing both Chesed and Gevurah in latent form) interacts with differentiated human vessels of apprehension. The phrase "both are words of the living God" is now given metaphysical grounding: the living God's speech contains both sides of every truth in fusion; it is the vessels of the receiving sages that actualize one side or the other without falsifying either.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Building directly on maamar 7's account of how Tzimtzum differentiates the Lights into particular Sefirot, this maamar applies the same Lights-Vessels logic to human cognition and halakhic diversity. It is the hemshech's first move into the applied dimension — showing that the cosmic Light-Vessel structure is not merely a metaphysical abstraction but is mirrored in the lived reality of Torah transmission and disagreement.
