## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Balak 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### Opening Kushya

The maamar opens with a textual difficulty in the verse from Bilam's prophecy: "מי מנה עפר יעקב ומספר את רובע ישראל" (Numbers 23:10). Two problems are posed. First, "מי מנה" implies that Yaakov's "dust" is beyond counting — yet "ומספר את רובע ישראל" seems to say that Yisrael's fourth does have a number. Second, since Yisrael is a higher rung than Yaakov (as is well established), why is the innumerable quality attributed to the lower name (Yaakov) and a numberable quality to the higher (Yisrael)?

### The Conceptual Architecture

To resolve this, the maamar recapitulates and extends the hemshech's account of three levels of the divine lights (אורות):

1. **The lights as rooted in Ein Sof before tzimtzum** — the ten hidden sefirot (ע"ס הגנוזות במאצילן), which are in a state of absolute unity: "אנת הוא חד ולא בחושבן" — You are One but not in the sense of counting. Here the lights are indistinguishable as ten; they are utterly beyond any number.

2. **The lights as they emerge through tzimtzum, before clothing in vessels** — individual sefirot become distinguishable in principle, yet remain in a state of peshitut (simplicity), described by the phrase "ע"ס בלי מ"ה" and "חכים ולא בחכמה ידיעא." This is diminishment (מיעוט) not annihilation (סילוק): the infinity of the source still pervades these lights.

3. **The lights as clothed within vessels (כלים)** — only here do the lights take on definite character as Chokhmah, Chesed, Gevurah, and so on. The maamar explains this via the principle that "בסוף ההמשכה מתגלה בחי' ההגבלה שבשרשן" — at the endpoint of the drawing-down, the limitedness latent in the root of the lights finally becomes manifest.

These three levels map onto the geometric triad of **נקודה–קו–שטח** (point–line–surface): the hidden root is a point, the emerging lights a line of simple undifferentiated potential, and the lights-in-vessels a surface that acquires genuine dimensionality and differentiation.

### Key Chassidic Concepts

- **ע"ס הגנוזות** (the ten hidden sefirot) — the root of lights in Ein Sof, corresponding to Atik of Adam Kadmon
- **צמצום** (tzimtzum) as מיעוט (diminishment) rather than סילוק (removal), preserving continuity with the source
- **בלי מה** (without definite character) — the lights as a line, transcendent of qualitative differentiation
- **אריך ועתיק** — the first level corresponds to Atik, the second to Arikh in broad terms
- **אנת הוא דקשיר לון ומייחד לון** — the union of lights-in-vessels with Ein Sof is maintained only through a superior encompassing light

### Resolution and Chiddush

The maamar resolves the verse's paradox as follows. "עפר יעקב" refers to ba'alei teshuvah, whose teshuvah draws from the very first level — the hidden sefirot utterly beyond number, "מי מנה" meaning genuinely innumerable. "רובע ישראל" refers to tzaddikim who fulfill Torah and mitzvot: the 613 commandments are "תרי"ג המשכות מאוא"ס ב"ה," and mitzvot, being in time and place, come with number — yet this is a numbering that itself opens onto the infinite, "בבחי' מספר אשר לא יספר." The chiddush is that Yaakov (teshuvah) draws from a level higher than Yisrael (Torah-fulfillment) precisely because teshuvah reaches behind the tzimtzum to the utterly undivided root, whereas Torah and mitzvot draw in a structured, quantified way through the light-in-vessels. Both are valid modes of divine drawing-down; they operate from different strata of the three-level structure the maamar has built.
