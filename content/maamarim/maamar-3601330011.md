## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Balak 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the account of how Bilam's prophetic access — genuine divine contact achieved from *outside* the Jewish covenantal structure — maps onto the Or Chozer (reflected light) dynamic: the same divine light that descends from above through the sefirot-hierarchy (Or Yashar) can also be accessed from below through the path of Or Chozer, but when accessed outside the covenantal vessels, it inverts and distorts. The maamar uses Bilam's blessing-curse paradox to establish the structural conditions under which Or Chozer operates validly versus when it becomes a counterfeit.

### The Opening Move

Parshat Balak centers on Bilam, the non-Jewish prophet who possessed genuine prophetic capacity yet was engaged to curse Israel. The maamar's entry question addresses the paradox that Bilam's curses turned to blessings — the Talmudic statement that one can discern God's character (midas ha-rachamim) in the very fact that the curses were transformed. How does authentic prophetic contact (Bilam genuinely received divine communication) coexist with the corrupt intention to curse? The structural question is: what is the relationship between the conduit of divine light and the vessel/intention of the one who receives it?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The Or Yashar / Or Chozer framework** established in preceding maamarim is applied to prophecy: Or Yashar descends through the chain from Keter to Malkhut through the graduated sefirot-vessels; Or Chozer ascends from below to above, returning the light upward through the vessel of the prophet's or worshipper's consciousness. Bilam represents a case of Or Chozer access — reaching upward toward the divine — but through vessels that are not calibrated by Torah's covenantal structure.

- **The distinction between the Light and the vessel of access**: the divine light contacted through prophecy is objective — it is the same Ein Sof-light regardless of who accesses it. What differs is the vessel through which it is contacted and the direction in which the contacted light flows. Through Israel's covenantal vessels, Or Chozer ascends and is re-absorbed harmoniously into its source, sustaining divine habitation below. Through Bilam's vessels, the same light is bent toward destructive ends — the "curse" direction — because his vessels are configured around self-interest and external power.

- **Why curses became blessings**: the maamar argues that at the level of divine light itself — the Or Yashar / Or Chozer interface — there is no intrinsic negativity; the inversion that produces curse exists only at the vessel-level. When God intervened to transform Bilam's words, what occurred was a restoration: the Or Chozer access that Bilam had was redirected to flow through the proper (covenantal) vessel-channel, and at that point, what the light expressed was naturally blessing, not curse. The light itself never changed; the vessel-direction did.

- **The donkey episode** (Numbers 22) functions in this analysis as the maamar's illustration of vessel-inversion: the donkey, an animal possessing no prophetic faculty, nonetheless perceived the Angel of God that Bilam — the great prophet — could not initially see. This is interpreted through the Or Chozer framework: the donkey, precisely because it had no self-interested prophetic apparatus (no vessel configured for personal aggrandizement), could register the divine presence in its raw simplicity, while Bilam's own prophetic vessel — tuned to power and self-interest — blocked what a creature of simple animal perception could access.

- **The relationship to Or Mekif** (surrounding light): Bilam's access is ultimately from an Or Mekif orientation, the encompassing light that does not discriminate by covenantal membership. But Or Mekif not channeled through the inner vessels of Torah will not produce structured divine habitation — it remains as raw power, undifferentiated, and thus susceptible to inversion. The maamar establishes that valid Or Chozer ascent requires the Or Pnimi infrastructure of Torah and mitzvot to give it direction.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that Or Chozer — the soul's upward reach toward the divine — is structurally real and genuinely accesses the divine light, but that its validity and directionality depend on the covenantal vessel-structure through which it operates. Bilam proves the point negatively: his Or Chozer access was genuine (hence the blessings embedded in his words) but his vessels were inverted (hence his intention to curse), and the resolution of the tension was possible only by divine intervention that restored the vessel-direction to its proper orientation. This is the hemshech's first direct treatment of Or Chozer as a *hazardous* dynamic — one that requires proper vessel-calibration.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Following the Korach maamar's account of how disputes "not for Heaven's sake" are structurally deficient (lacking both authentic sefirot-faces), and the Chukat maamar's account of graduated light-diminishment, this maamar now addresses Or Chozer as the upward movement within the cosmic structure. It introduces the theme of vessel-calibration as the critical variable for whether Or Chozer returns light validly or distorts it — a theme that will become central to the hemshech's account of divine service (avodah) in later maamarim.
