## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Noach 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Moving into the regular weekly parsha cycle after the festival season, this maamar uses Parshat Noach to construct the theory of *Tohu* and *Tikkun* as two cosmic eras — and specifically to analyze how the *shevirat ha-kelim* (breaking of the vessels of Tohu) relates to the Or Mekif / Or Pnimi dynamic. The narrative of the Flood (*mabbul*) becomes a template for understanding how the withdrawal of Or Pnimi (the "flood" that destroys the old Tohu-structure) actually makes possible the emergence of a superior Tikkun-structure in which Or Mekif can invest itself more fully.

### The Opening Move

The parasha opens with the characterization of Noach as *"ish tzaddik tamim hayah be-dorotav"* — "a righteous, complete man in his generations." The traditional debate (Rashi vs. Nachmanides) about whether *be-dorotav* is praise or qualified praise — great only relative to a corrupt generation, not by absolute standards — opens the maamar to a deeper question: what is the relationship between *tzaddik* (righteousness) and *tamim* (completeness/integrity) as two distinct spiritual qualities? And why does the Torah specify both?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Tzaddik and tamim as Or Pnimi and Or Mekif modes**: The Rashab develops *tzaddik* as the quality corresponding to Or Pnimi — righteousness expressed through the inner-world structure of mitzvot, middot, and Torah learning. *Tamim* (completeness, integrity) corresponds to Or Mekif — a wholeness that is not achieved through accumulation of inner qualities but that exists essentially, above the hishtalshelut of individual virtues. A tzaddik can be "great in his generation" without being tamim; tamim is an Or Mekif quality that transcends the generational context.

- **Noach's Ark as Or Mekif vessel**: The Ark (*teivah*) is analyzed as the structural analogue of the Or Mekif: it encompasses the remnants of the pre-Flood world (seed for a new creation) without investing itself within the world's structures. The Flood *is* the withdrawal of Or Pnimi from the corrupt Tohu-world of Noach's generation; the Ark *is* the Or Mekif that preserves the essential seeds of creation through the period of Or Pnimi's absence.

- **Mabbul as the withdrawal that enables renewal**: The hemshech develops a counter-intuitive reading: the Flood is not primarily punishment but the mechanism of cosmic renewal. The Or Pnimi of the Tohu-world had been distorted by the generation's sins; its withdrawal (the Flood) was necessary to allow a fresh emergence of Or Pnimi in a Tikkun-mode. This parallels the hemshech's earlier argument that the shevirat ha-kelim of Tohu was itself a necessary stage in the development of a world capable of containing Or Mekif.

- **The rainbow (*keshet*) as Or Chozer sign**: The rainbow that God places as a covenant sign after the Flood is interpreted through the Or Chozer framework: it is visible light *reflected* and *refracted* — the Or Chozer of the surviving world's divine service ascending and refracting into the full spectrum of colors. The covenant is thus not merely a promise but a statement about the structure of the post-Flood world: Or Chozer will always be possible, no matter how low Or Pnimi descends.

- **Seven Noachide laws as Or Pnimi minimum**: The seven Noachide commandments given after the Flood correspond to the seven lower sefirot in their minimum necessary state — the Or Pnimi infrastructure that must remain intact even in a non-Israelite world to allow the continued existence of the creation. This minimum suffices to keep the world's vessel-structure intact until Israel receives the 613 commandments that fully actualize all the sefirot.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that the Noach narrative is the Torah's account of a cosmological transition: from the Tohu-world (characterized by intense Or Pnimi/Or Mekif in the sefirot, but without adequate vessel-structure, thus destined to break) to the Tikkun-world (in which Or Pnimi operates at lower intensity but within a sustainable vessel-structure). Noach is the bridge-figure — the *tzaddik-tamim* who embodies both or modes — and the Ark is the Or Mekif container that makes the transition possible.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

After the festival season's analysis of Or Mekif in the context of sacred time (Rosh Hashana, Sukkot, Shemini Atzeret), this maamar extends the Or Mekif / Or Pnimi analysis into cosmic history — the narrative of Tohu and Tikkun. This is the hemshech's first systematic engagement with the Tohu/Tikkun distinction, which will become increasingly central as the discourse develops. The Noach framework provides the hemshech with a historical-cosmological axis to complement its ontological and experiential axes.
