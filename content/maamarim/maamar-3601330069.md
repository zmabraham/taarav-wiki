## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Balak 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar constructs a systematic phenomenology of the two faculties of sight (re'iyah) and hearing (shmi'ah), mapping them onto the kabbalistic categories of Chokhmah and Binah respectively, and then extends this to establish why visual perception grasps the essence (mahut) of its object while auditory perception grasps only the existence (metziyut). This becomes the basis for understanding Balak's fear of Israel as fundamentally different in kind from his knowledge of the Egyptian Exodus — and reveals why the righteous "see" their way upward while the wicked "see" their way downward.

### The Opening Move
The narrative opens: "Balak son of Tzippor saw all that Israel had done to the Amorites, and Moab was in dread" (Numbers 22:2-3). The Kelei Yakar raises a pointed question: Balak certainly knew about the vastly greater miracles of the Exodus — the ten plagues, the sea splitting — which dwarfed Israel's military victory over the Amorites. Why was it specifically the latter that terrified him, rather than the former? And why does the text say "he saw" rather than "he heard"? Additionally, the Midrash (on the verse "Haman saw") draws a typological contrast: the seeing of the wicked descends them into Gehinnom, while the seeing of the righteous elevates them to supreme heights.

### The Argumentative Arc
- The Rashab builds from the previous maamar (Chukat 5664) on the pnimiyut/chitzoniyut of the kli and the machshavah/dibbur distinction. Now he moves to the parallel faculty-pair: re'iyah (sight) and shmi'ah (hearing), which are rooted in Chokhmah and Binah respectively.
- Chokhmah is the faculty of immediate, point-like apprehension (נקודה) — the instant "flash" of understanding that grasps the thing itself in its essence, before it has been spread out into discursive understanding. Binah is the faculty of discursive expansion (Rechavot HaNahar, "the breadths of the river") — the systematic unfolding of the point-insight into a comprehensible structure. Sight corresponds to Chokhmah: it grasps the physical substance (guf) of the object directly, as a whole, in a moment. Hearing corresponds to Binah: it registers the presence of the other (the sound's existence, its metziyut) but not its essence.
- Proof-text: Moses came down from Sinai and heard from God that Israel had made the Golden Calf — he knew this fully. Yet he descended with the Tablets in hand. When he saw the Calf and the dancing, he shattered the Tablets. This is not because he "learned more" — he knew it all already. Seeing versus hearing is not a quantitative difference (more information) but a qualitative one: seeing puts you in direct contact with the mahut (essence) of what you see, in a way that transforms the knower.
- The inversion for wicked versus righteous: the righteous person's seeing is rooted in the Chokhmah that is oriented toward Ein Sof (the "inner point" of Chokhmah that draws from above). When such a person sees, their faculty of sight is linked to its divine root, and the act of seeing elevates them. The wicked person's seeing is Chokhmah-faculty severed from its root, operating in the mode of self-referential apprehension. When such a person sees, the act of seeing draws their soul downward, deepening their entrenchment in self.
- Balak "saw" rather than "heard" Israel's deeds — meaning his faculty of direct, essential apprehension registered something qualitatively different from the miracles of Egypt. Egypt's miracles were heard (great events, reported, comprehended intellectually) but not seen in this essential sense. Israel's victory over the Amorites was a direct disclosure of the divine power acting through Israel's own agency — it was "seeable" in the essential sense: the divine mahut was revealed in the physical act. This was more threatening to Balak because it was not a miracle from above (an event that happened to Israel) but a revelation through Israel (the divine manifesting through their action).

### What It Gets To
The maamar establishes that perception is not passive reception but active ontological engagement: what we see shapes us, and how we see determines whether our seeing is an ascent or descent. This is not merely psychological but kabbalistic: re'iyah and shmi'ah are genuine conduits to Chokhmah and Binah respectively, and the soul's direction (toward or away from its divine root) determines whether these conduits carry light upward or draw darkness downward. Balak's "seeing" was the wicked-mode of Chokhmah, which registers but cannot contain the divine disclosure — so it produces dread and ultimately the project of cursing.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
Parshat Balak 5664 introduces the re'iyah/shmi'ah distinction as a new analytical pair that complements the machshavah/dibbur pair established in Chukat. Together these two pairs — both of which turn on the Chokhmah/Binah axis — give the hemshech a rich vocabulary for analyzing how the soul's faculties engage with divine light at different levels of depth and immediacy. The maamar also advances the hemshech's account of how Or Chozer operates at the human level: the righteous person's act of seeing is an act of Or Chozer (the divine light ascending through their perception), while the wicked person's seeing is a descent that inverts the Or Chozer dynamic.
