## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Shemini Atzeret 1905-06** in the year **עתר"ו** (1905–06), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

The Shemini Atzeret maamar is the theological apex of the 5666 High Holiday cycle. It builds the hemshech's account of the distinction between Chitzoniut Atik (the external aspect of Atik Yomin — the "Ancient of Days," the Partzuf of divine will and pleasure) and Pnimiyut Atik (its innermost aspect) — and shows that the singular intimacy of Shemini Atzeret, the "eighth day" on which Israel alone brings its reduced offering (one bull, one ram), corresponds to the revelation of Pnimiyut Atik specifically to Israel. The maamar also advances the hemshech's analysis of the inner Keter-structure by distinguishing between two modes of "nothingness" (Ayin) in the Keter-level.

### The Opening Move

The verse "B'yom hashemini atzeret tihye lachem — On the eighth day there shall be a solemn assembly for you" (Num. 29:35) is opened through the Midrash Rabbah's famous parable: a king made a seven-day feast and invited all the citizens of the country. On the eighth day, the king said to his beloved friend: "Now that we have fulfilled our obligation to the citizens of the country — come, let the two of us make do with whatever we find (yom nivra'el, a small intimate gathering), just me and you." Similarly: in the seven days of Sukkot, Israel offered 70 bulls for the 70 guardian-angels of the 70 nations; on Shemini Atzeret, God says to Israel: "Come, let us just be together — one bull, one ram." The opening question is: what is the nature of this singular intimacy? Why, after the elaborately structured seven-day offering, does the most intimate divine-Israel encounter require the most minimal offering?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The seventy bulls as Or Yashar distributed through the 70 nations**: The seven-day Sukkot offering of 70 bulls represents Or Yashar flowing through the 70 saro shel ma'alah (supernal ministers/angels) that govern the 70 nations. This is Or Yashar in its "public" mode — the divine light that sustains the entire created order, distributed through the governance structures that mediate between Ein Sof and the diversity of human civilizations. The number 70 = 7 × 10 = the seven lower sefirot each containing ten, the complete structure of Or Yashar's distribution through hishtalshelut.
- **The 70 as Chitzoniut Atik; the singular as Pnimiyut Atik**: The Rashab's key structural move, developed extensively in the source text's continuation (via the analysis of the two forms of Keter/Ayin): the 70-fold distribution of divine light corresponds to Chitzoniut Atik (the external aspect of Atik), the aspect of divine will and pleasure that is oriented toward the creation and its governance. Chitzoniut Atik is the source of the individual pleasures (ta'anugim pratiyim) that motivate each sefirah's specific activity — it is the divine pleasure "dressed in" (mitlabesh b') the framework of Chokhmah (the Zohar's "Keter as Ayin that is clothed in Chokhmah"). Pnimiyut Atik, by contrast, is the divine pleasure that is entirely self-referential — not oriented toward any created object or divine function, but simply the delight of Ein Sof in itself. It is this level that is revealed in the intimate "just me and you" of Shemini Atzeret.
- **The minimal offering as maximal proximity**: The reduction from 70 bulls to 1 bull is not an impoverishment of the divine service but its intensification: the 70-bull structure is the elaborate mediation required when Or Yashar distributes itself through the 70 sarin (ministers). The single bull and ram of Shemini Atzeret require no such mediation — the offering goes directly from Israel to God without passing through the mediating structures of the 70 nations' governance. This is the formal structure of Or Chozer at its most direct: a single ascending movement without intermediate stages, from the human offering (material reality) to Pnimiyut Atik (the innermost divine self-delight).
- **The two forms of Keter-Ayin in the source text**: The maamar (via the continuation sections) develops the analysis of the Keter-Ayin. The Keter contains two aspects: (1) the aspect that is the "lowest level of Atzmus" (bechina tachtonah sheba'atzmus) — this is the Keter as the last glimmer of divine self-sufficiency before the world of emanation begins, corresponding to Chitzoniut Atik; and (2) the aspect that is the "highest root of emanation" (rosh u'mikor l'natzalim) — this is the Keter as the first principle of the divine self-disclosure that generates the chain of worlds, corresponding to the Keter's orientation toward creation. Between these two, Pnimiyut Atik is neither: it is entirely above and prior to both. The maamar further develops: even the "inner aspect of Ein Sof within Pnimiyut Atik" (the A"s she'betoch pnimiyut Atik — the infinite that is concealed within and through the three supernal "heads" of Keter) may be the truest form of Ayin ha'amiti.
- **The "external" and "inner" Kav (line of divine self-disclosure)**: Following the source text's extensive analysis, the maamar distinguishes between Chitzoniut haKav (the outer dimension of the divine line that extends post-tzimtzum through the worlds) and Pnimiyut haKav (the inner dimension). Chitzoniut haKav is the light as it differentiates into the ten sefirot and their various configurations (nikkudot, then keilim). Pnimiyut haKav is the light as it remains undifferentiated — "ein bo hitkhalekut, b'chol makom hu b'shaveh" (there is no division in it, in every place it is equally present) — and does not enter into concealment through the keilim (vessels). Shemini Atzeret is the festival of Pnimiyut haKav: the undivided, equally-present divine light that does not need the elaborate structure of the 70-nation/70-sefirot mediation.
- **The root of the Kav in Malchut of Ein Sof**: The source text reveals an important structural claim: "the root of the Kav's drawing is from the Malchut of Ein Sof" — meaning the contraction (tzimtzum) that made space for the Kav was itself an expression of Malchut (divine governance/royalty at the Atzmus level), specifically the moment when Ein Sof "measured within itself" (shiyyer b'atzmo) the ten sefirot and then contracted to allow a void (chalal) into which the Kav could descend. This grounds the Shemini Atzeret intimacy: Israel's relationship with Pnimiyut Atik is not a relationship with something external to the generative structure of creation but with the innermost principle that initiated creation — the Malchut of Ein Sof's willingness to make space for the other.

### What It Gets To

The maamar's conclusion is that Shemini Atzeret is the revelatory endpoint of the entire autumn cycle: Rosh Hashana reveals the Malchut-dimension (divine kingship as relational mode), Yom Kippur reveals the Or Chozer reaching Keter, Sukkot distributes the elevated Or Yashar through all creation's levels (70 nations), and Shemini Atzeret bypasses all distribution entirely to reveal Pnimiyut Atik — the inner divine self-delight that was the original impetus for all creation. The minimal offering of the eighth day is not poverty but purity: the unmediated meeting of Israel's Or Chozer with the source from which Or Yashar originally emerged. The festival's name "Atzeret" (from atzar — to hold back, to gather in) refers to God "holding back" Israel from departing at the end of the Sukkot season, for one more day of this intimate, unmediated encounter.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This is one of the hemshech's most analytically dense maamarim, advancing multiple dimensions of the project simultaneously: (1) it completes the High Holiday theological cycle begun with Rosh Hashana's two-Da'at framework; (2) it introduces the Chitzoniut/Pnimiyut Atik distinction that will be a master-concept throughout subsequent volumes; (3) it provides the most explicit available treatment of the two dimensions of the Kav (line), which is foundational to the hemshech's entire account of the post-tzimtzum divine self-disclosure; (4) it establishes the Israel-as-unique-partner-of-Pnimiyut-Atik claim that will underpin later maamarim on the soul's essential divine nature (shoresh haneshama). The Bereishit maamar that follows will carry this pnimiyut-analysis directly into the question of why the Torah begins with the creation narrative rather than with the first commandment.
