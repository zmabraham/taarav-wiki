## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayera 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs the two-fold meaning of the word *Sefirah* — (1) from *mispar* (number/limit) and (2) from *sippur* (narration/revelation) — as two distinct ontological functions of the sefirot, corresponding precisely to *or* and *kli* (light and vessel). Using Abraham's hospitality to the three angels (Genesis 18) as its exegetical anchor, the maamar establishes that the sefirot's primary function is not to *limit* the divine but to *reveal* it — and that this revelatory function itself depends on the paradoxical relationship between light's natural upward tendency and the vessel that holds it downward.

### The Opening Move

The verse reads: *"He was standing over them under the tree, and they ate"* (Gen. 18:8). The Talmud (Kiddushin 32b) derives from this verse, and from an incident with Rabban Gamliel's students, that *standing and serving* is a distinct act from *feeding* — two separate dimensions of hospitality. The Rashab asks: what is the kabalistic meaning of Abraham's two-fold service — both feeding (*ma'akhil u-mashkeh*) and *standing over them* (*omed aleihem*)? And why does the verse specify *"under the tree"* a second time, when it had already been mentioned earlier?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Sefirah as mispar (number/limit)**: The first meaning of sefirah is counting and measuring (*mispar*). This refers to the function of the *kelim* (vessels) — they are the structures that give the divine light a definite form, a specific "count," a bounded identity. The primary locus of the *mispar* function is Malchut, the lowest sefirah, which constitutes the *most* bounded expression of divine light. Above Malchut, the sefirot have a *mispar asher lo yisaper* (a number that cannot be counted) — they are in the domain of the bounded, but their measure is so vast as to be practically unbounded.

- **Sefirah as sippur (narration/revelation)**: The source text cites the *Bahir* and *Pardes Rimmonim* (Reshit Chokhmah) for the second meaning: *"The heavens declare (mesaperim) the glory of God"* (Ps. 19:2). The sefirot are *me-saperot* — they tell, they narrate, they *reveal* — the hidden divine thoughts. As speech reveals the thoughts hidden in the mind, the sefirot reveal the *machshavot nistarot* (hidden thoughts of Ein Sof) to the lower worlds. This is the function of the *orot* (lights) within the sefirot, not the kelim.

- **The natural tendency of light upward**: The maamar's central argument is that *light by nature ascends* — it seeks to return to its source. The source text explicitly develops the analogy: higher soul-faculties (intellect/sekhel) are in a mode of *self-elevation and hiddenness* (hitna'alut), while lower faculties (emotions/midot) are in a mode of *revelation and descent*. The fire analogy from the Tanya (ch. 19) is invoked: fire naturally seeks to ascend to its elemental source, and is held downward only when something catches it (the wick and oil). Similarly, divine light naturally seeks to ascend and be absorbed in its source — only the *kli* (vessel) catches it and holds it in downward manifestation.

- **Kelim as the vehicle of downward revelation**: This is the maamar's chiddush in the Or Mekif / Or Pnimi analysis: the reason the sefirot-kelim are needed is precisely *because* light wants to rise. Without the kelim, all Or Pnimi would immediately return to Ein Sof (Or Mekif mode). The kelim *hold* the light in downward manifestation, enabling the world's continued existence and the divine service that draws on structured Or Pnimi.

- **Abraham's two-fold hospitality**: The *feeding* (*ma'akhil*) corresponds to the *sippur* function — the light itself flowing to the recipients (the angels/guests), revealing divine generosity. The *standing over them* (*omed aleihem*) corresponds to the *mispar* function — maintaining the structural, bounded container (the tree, the shade, the physical space) that makes the feeding possible. The tree mentioned a second time specifies the specific *kli*-structure (the bounded natural space) within which the light of hospitality was held.

- **Tohu and Tikkun through the sefirah-lens**: The maamar connects this analysis to the Tohu/Tikkun distinction introduced in maamar 26: the world of Tohu had powerful *orot* (lights in the sippur mode) but inadequate *kelim* (mispar function), causing the shevirah. Tikkun is the world in which the kelim are properly scaled to hold the lights — the *mispar* function is adequately developed to contain the *sippur* function's revelatory power.

### What It Gets To

The maamar has established a precise two-function theory of the sefirot: (a) the *kli*-dimension (*mispar*) bounds and holds the light in downward manifestation; (b) the *or*-dimension (*sippur*) reveals the hidden divine through that bounded form. Both functions are necessary — *sippur* without *mispar* produces the Tohu-shevirah; *mispar* without *sippur* produces dead formalism with no divine content. The healthy sefirot integrate both: the kli holds the light, and the light reveals through the kli.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Maamar 26 introduced the Tohu/Tikkun framework. This maamar now gives it its deepest analytical grounding: the sefirot's two-function theory explains *why* Tohu broke (inadequate *mispar* relative to the *sippur* intensity) and *why* Tikkun is stable (adequate balance of both functions). This theory — light's natural upward tendency held in productive downward manifestation by the vessel — is one of the hemshech's most foundational structural principles, and this maamar is its first fully developed articulation in the discourse.
