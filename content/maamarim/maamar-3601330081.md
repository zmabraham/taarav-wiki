## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 2nd day of Sukkot 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on the night of the second day of Sukkot 5670, constructs a Kabbalistic ontology of the Four Species (Arba Minim) by mapping their selection from the entire plant kingdom onto the distinction between inner and outer Malchut, and then articulating how the Four Species together constitute a unique instrument for unifying these two dimensions within a single embodied act.

### The Opening Move
The Torah commands: "You shall take for yourselves on the first day the fruit of a beautiful tree (esrog), palm branches (lulav), myrtle boughs, and willows of the brook" (Lev. 23:40). The Rashab's opening question is both botanical and metaphysical: out of the entire plant kingdom, why were precisely these four species selected for the mitzvah? What quality do they share, or what complementary qualities do they embody, that makes their combination the vehicle for divine will (רצון העליון)?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **Connecting to the Shoshanah analysis.** The Rashab explicitly picks up from a prior maamar on "As a rose among thorns, so is my beloved among the daughters" (Song of Songs 2:2) — "who is the rose? That is the Community of Israel (כנסת ישראל)." The rose (שושנה) is identified there as the inner dimension of Malchut (פנימיות המל'), while the rose's outer petals and color are the outer dimension (חיצוניות המל'). The maamar reopens this framework.
- **Outer Malchut: the colors of governance.** Citing the Zohar's Hakdamah, the Rashab establishes that the rose's red and white colors (חיוור וסומק — white/Chesed and red/Gevurah) correspond to the outer face of Malchut — the face that governs Beriyah, Yetzirah, and Asiyah through measurable expressions of chesed and gevurah. These are "colors" (גוונין) in the technical sense developed in Pardes Rimonim: they are the visible manifestations of sefirotic qualities as they reach into the world of action. Outer Malchut is always visible, differentiated, and expressible.
- **Inner Malchut (Knesset Yisrael): the hidden unity.** The inner face of Malchut — which the Zohar calls Knesset Yisrael — does not manifest as red or white, chesed or gevurah. Its chesed and gevurah are present but hidden (בהעלם), not yet expressed as differentiated qualities. The Rashab's striking analogy from human emotional life: when a judge (שופט) renders a verdict, they may act harshly — but a righteous judge who genuinely cares for all parties experiences both mercy and rigor simultaneously, without the distortion of emotion pulling either way. This is the inner emotional life: din and rachamim coexist without mutual cancellation.
- **The Four Species as a unity of all four.** The four plant-types embody four modes of divine expression: the esrog (fruit and fragrance — taste and smell, Torah and deeds), the lulav (fruit but no fragrance — Torah without deeds), myrtle (fragrance but no fruit — deeds without Torah), willow (neither — simple people with neither). Holding all four together — binding them in unity, waving them in all six directions — performs the unification of all soul-types and all modes of divine expression into a single gesture. The four become one.
- **The inner/outer Malchut unification.** The esrog is held separately (it has both taste and fragrance — it is complete in itself) while the lulav, myrtle, and willow are bound together. The separate esrog corresponds to pnimiyut Malchut; the bound three correspond to the three outer expressions of Malchut in the three lower worlds (Beriyah, Yetzirah, Asiyah). Holding esrog and bound-lulav together in one act unites inner and outer Malchut — the same unification sought through the prayers of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, now expressed in physical object and bodily gesture.
- **Why these species specifically: the unity of multiplicity and oneness.** The Four Species, unlike most mitzvah objects, are not manufactured or transformed — they are taken as they are from nature. This is the Rashab's deeper point: the divine will descends into natural reality without altering it, selecting from within nature's existing multiplicity precisely those combinations that, when assembled, manifest the hidden unity beneath all differentiation. This is כוח הפועל בנפעל — the power of the Maker in the made — now visible in plant matter.

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that the Four Species selection is not arbitrary but reflects a precise Kabbalistic economy: each species embodies a specific mode of inner/outer Malchut expression, and their assembly performs the unification of all these modes. The act of waving the Arba Minim is the bodily enactment of the theoretical unity between inner and outer Malchut developed throughout the preceding Tishrei maamarim.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar initiates the Sukkot sub-section of Part 2. Where Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur were entirely inward and ascending (coronation of Malchut, ascent to Keter through Teshuvah), Sukkot begins the outward movement: bringing the revelation down into physical objects — first the Four Species (embodied in plant matter), then the Sukkah walls (to be analyzed in the next maamar). The hemshech is demonstrating that the complete divine service is always the full arc: ascent (Or Chozer) followed by descent (Or Yashar newly luminous from having reached Keter).
