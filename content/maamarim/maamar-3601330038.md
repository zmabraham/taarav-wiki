## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Beha'alotcha 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Beha'alotcha 1902-03. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ בהעלותך, תער"ג corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar develops a sustained analysis of ישסו"ת (Yisrael Saba and Tevunah — the lower Abba and Ima, corresponding to the lower partzufim of Chochmah and Binah) as the "emotions of the intellect" (מדות דחו"ב), distinguishing between inner and outer dimensions of Binah, and using the Menorah's seven lamps and their relationship to "the face of the Menorah" (פני המנורה) to locate Aharon's priestly service at the level of pnimiyut haChochmah — the inward face of Wisdom.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens with a precise textual double question: (1) What is "the face of the Menorah" (פני המנורה) that the six lamps are to illuminate toward? Rashi explains it as the central (seventh) lamp — but then the text should say "six lamps," not "seven." (2) A midrash brought by Rashi states that Aaron's despondency at the dedication of the Mishkan (when the princes brought offerings but the Levites did not) was consoled by God: "Your service is greater than theirs, for you kindle and tend the lamps." The maamar demands: in what sense is the kindling of the Menorah greater than the elaborate offerings of the princes? These questions open the maamar's central investigation into what the Menorah specifically represents in the Kabbalistic framework.

### The Argumentative Arc
- ישסו"ת are the lower aspects of Abba (Chochmah) and Ima (Binah) — specifically their נ"ה"י (Netzach, Hod, Yesod), which function as "pipes" transmitting the intellectual light into the realm of the emotions (מדות/Midot). This is distinct from the emotions-within-intellect (מדות שבשכל, the intellectual passions of pure cognition) — ישסו"ת are the leaning of the intellect toward manifestation in an other.
- The maamar cites the Etz Chaim (Shaar 14, ch. 4): ישסו"ת correspond to the letter ה of the Divine Name (שם הוי'), because they are the "Binah-level" — and Binah is categorized as a "mere illumination" (הארה לבד) relative to the essential Atzmut, because all comprehension involves some distance from the source. Or Yashar from Atzilut reaches Beriyah only as Binah-level illumination, not as pure essence.
- The distinction between inner and outer Binah is introduced: outer Binah (חיצוניות בינה) is the Binah that functions as the "source of hishtalshelut" — the root of the chain of worlds, accessible through structured contemplation. Inner Binah (פנימיות בינה) is the Binah of "comprehension of essence" (השגת המהות) — a paradoxical mode in which the intellect grasps not merely the existence of a thing but its inner reality.
- The analogy deployed is startlingly phenomenological: when a person struggles with an intellectual problem and suddenly "gets it" (a flash of insight, עס ווערט עם רעכט), in the moment of the flash the person has the "nochah" (settled rightness) of the idea without yet being able to articulate it in discursive form. This flash is the level of pnimiyut haChochmah — a knowing that is above propositional structure. The subsequent inability to explain the idea in full detail does not mean the initial flash was illusory; it means the initial grasp was at the level of pnimiyut, which has not yet descended into the level where discursive reasoning can work on it.
- The Menorah's seven lamps all illuminate "toward the face of the Menorah" — they all point toward the central lamp, which represents pnimiyut haChochmah (the inner face of Wisdom). The six outer lamps = the six emotional Sefirot (Chesed through Yesod) in their intellectual root; they illuminate toward the central lamp = they are drawn toward and derive their light from pnimiyut haChochmah. This is why the text says "seven" not "six" — the central lamp is not merely a point of reference but the source from which the six derive; all seven are united in their orientation toward pnimiyut.
- Aaron's service surpasses the princes' offerings precisely because the kindling of the Menorah draws from pnimiyut haChochmah — the highest level accessible through priestly service. The offerings of the princes drew from "outer Binah," from the structured emotion-in-intellect level. Aaron drew from the inner face of Wisdom itself — which is why his action is irreplaceable: only he, as Kohen Gadol embodying the Chesed-of-Chochmah (ח"ס), could kindle this inner light in the souls of Israel.
- Aaron's name (אהרן) contains the letters of "ראה" (to see) — indicating that his mode of service was the mode of seeing/direct perception (ראייה), corresponding to the Chochmah-level of direct intuitive grasp, as opposed to Abraham's love-from-comprehension (which was outer-Binah level, mediated by discursive understanding).

### What It Gets To
The maamar establishes a hierarchy of how divine light reaches the emotional Sefirot: (1) through outer Binah — the structured understanding that gives birth to emotional response; (2) through inner Binah/pnimiyut haChochmah — the direct intuitive grasp that bypasses discursive mediation and generates a love (אהבת אהרן) of qualitatively higher intensity. The Menorah's seven-lamps structure is the architectural model of this: all emotional light derives ultimately from the "inner face" of the central wisdom-lamp.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar significantly advances the hemshech by developing the inner/outer distinction within Binah itself — a distinction that will become foundational for understanding how Torah (which "emerges from Chochmah") can generate different qualities of Or Chozer depending on whether one engages it at the outer or inner level. The analysis of ישסו"ת as "emotions of the intellect" provides the precise anatomical vocabulary for understanding how Or Yashar descends from the intellect-level into the emotion-level — a transition that the hemshech will repeatedly revisit in its analysis of the divine-human relationship.
