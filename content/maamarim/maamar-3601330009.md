## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Korach 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs the account of the ten *hidden* Sefirot (*sefirot ha-genuzot*) as they exist within Ein Sof before the Tzimtzum — establishing that they are simultaneously *ten* and *simple* (without contradiction), because their ten-ness derives from the purpose of future revelation (a divine intention/measurement, שיעור) rather than from any actual differentiation, and their simplicity derives from their total fusion and cleaving to the divine essence. It uses this to explain why a dispute "for Heaven's sake" is sustainable while one not for Heaven's sake is not.

### The Opening Move

The Mishnah in Avot 5:17 opens the discourse: "Every dispute that is for the sake of Heaven will ultimately be sustained; and one that is not for the sake of Heaven will not be sustained. Which is a dispute for Heaven's sake? Such as that of Hillel and Shammai. And which is not for Heaven's sake? Such as the dispute of Korach and his assembly." The apparent puzzle: why does the text enumerate *both sides* of the Hillel-Shammai dispute (both Hillel and Shammai) but only *one side* of the Korach dispute (Korach and his assembly, not Moses)? And what is the virtue in a dispute's *being sustained* — why is perpetuating a disagreement a sign of its validity?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The Hillel-Shammai dispute is sustained because both sides reflect genuine divine truth** (as established in the previous maamar on Shelach): the Lights of divine wisdom contain both Chesed-tendency and Gevurah-tendency in fusion; each sage actualizes one aspect through his vessel, and since both aspects are rooted in the divine Light, both positions are eternally valid. Two authentic sefirot-faces can coexist indefinitely without one annihilating the other.

- **The ten hidden Sefirot in Ein Sof** are now analyzed in detail. Before the Tzimtzum there were indeed ten Sefirot latent within Ein Sof — these are the *sefirot ha-genuzot* referenced in Kabbalah. The challenge: how can there be "ten" within the absolutely simple Ein Sof? Wouldn't ten-ness imply differentiation, and differentiation imply limitation?

- **Resolution through the distinction between two types of ten-ness**: (a) Ten-ness that comes from actual differentiation in manifestation — the post-Tzimtzum Sefirot of Atzilut, where each is a distinct *meziut* (presence) with its own character; (b) Ten-ness that is pre-differentiational — the *sefirot ha-genuzot* in Ein Sof, where all ten exist in complete fusion (התערבות והתאחדות) with no individual Sefirah standing out. Their "ten-ness" is a function of the *divine intention/measurement* (שיעור) that Ein Sof embedded within itself regarding future revelation — the blueprint, not the building.

- **They are simultaneously simple because of their total cleaving (devekut) to the divine essence**: the argument is that anything that cleaves completely to its source takes on the character of its source. The ten hidden Sefirot are in a state of perfect cleaving (דביקות) to Ein Sof, and since Ein Sof is utterly simple, they too are simple — their ten-ness is there, but latent, not yet individuated, and so it does not constitute limitation.

- **The Adam Kadmon analogy**: the name "Adam Kadmon" (primordial human) seems self-contradictory — *adam* implies something new/created, *kadmon* implies primordial/uncreated. The resolution: the primordial thought of Adam (the blueprint of all existence) arose within Ein Sof as the first "measurement" — it was *Adam* (human-form, structured) yet existed within *Kadmon* (the utterly primary); it bore both qualities without contradiction because it was a latent intention within an unlimited source.

- **Korach's dispute** is resolved structurally: Korach represents a dispute that emerges from self-interested motivation, where the two sides are actually one corrupted side (Korach and his assembly) against the divine truth embodied in Moses. There is no second legitimate sefirah-face — only the illusion of duality created by personal ambition. This is why only one side is named: the other "side" (Moses) was not in dispute at all — he simply embodied truth. A dispute not for Heaven's sake lacks the two-authentic-faces structure; it has only one real side and one false one, so it cannot be sustained as a genuine duality.

### What It Gets To

By the end, the maamar has established the doctrine of the *sefirot ha-genuzot*: ten Sefirot latent within Ein Sof before Tzimtzum, simultaneously ten (by divine intention) and simple (by total fusion and devekut). This is the hemshech's account of what the Tzimtzum works *upon* — not nothing, but these hidden Sefirot that await particularization. It has also given the Hillel-Shammai dispute a metaphysical basis in the two-faced character of divine wisdom's Light, while diagnosing Korach's dispute as a structural failure — lacking the dual-authentic-face form.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar completes the foundational cosmological account that the Shavuot trilogy and the Shelach maamar began: the hemshech now has a full picture from pre-Tzimtzum (sefirot ha-genuzot in Ein Sof) through Tzimtzum (particularization of Lights into distinct Sefirot) to the differentiated Lights-and-Vessels of Atzilut. Korach and Hillel-Shammai are the rabbinic mirror of this cosmic structure.
