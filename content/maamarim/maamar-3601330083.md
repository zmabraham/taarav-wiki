## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Shemini Atzeret 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Shemini Atzeret 5670, constructs the Kabbalistic identity of Shemini Atzeret as a wholly independent festival — not merely the conclusion of Sukkot but a festival with its own unique divine service — by working through the mechanics of how the divine "voice" (קול) condenses from its primordially simple, undivided state into the articulated letters of speech (אותיות הדבור) through successive compressions, and why this process is the key to understanding Simchat Torah.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens with the verse "On the eighth day there shall be a cessation (עצרת) for you" (Num. 29:35), and immediately raises the question: Shemini Atzeret functions as an independent festival for all purposes of festival law (פז"ר קש"ב), yet in Parshat Re'eh, when the three pilgrimage festivals are listed, Sukkot is described as "seven days you shall celebrate" — with no separate mention of Shemini Atzeret. Two related questions: (1) Why is the "cessation" of Sukkot on the eighth day while Passover's cessation (Shavuot) is on the fiftieth? (2) What is the connection between Shemini Atzeret and Simchat Torah (the celebration of completing the Torah) — why do we celebrate the Torah specifically at this moment?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **Recovering the framework of אלקי המלך.** The Rashab returns explicitly to the hemshech's organizing concept of "אלקי המלך" — the divine power (כח אלקי) that functions as כח הפועל בנפעל (the power of the maker within the made). He clarifies a crucial point: this divine power is described as "hidden" (כח נעלם) not because it is separate from the world it animates but because it remains "attached to its source" (דבוק במקורו) while simultaneously causing creative change (דביקות בבחי' התחדשות). The paradox: it is the most intimate thing in the world (the power that makes each thing what it is) yet remains entirely hidden within the made thing.
- **The Voice (קול) as the paradigm.** The Rashab launches an extended analysis of the human voice as the paradigm for understanding how an essentially simple, undivided reality (Ein Sof) gives rise to a highly articulated, differentiated reality (the created world). The voice (קול) emanates from the heart's breath (הבל הלב) as a primordially simple sound — not yet articulated into letters, not yet "loud" or "soft" or any particular pitch. In its essential state, it is undivided (פשוט) and contains no differentiation. Yet from this simple voice, all the articulated letters of speech emerge through the five places of articulation (חמש מוצאות הפה).
- **Tzimtzum of the voice: the inner voice that cannot be heard.** The Rashab develops a crucial distinction within the voice itself: the "inner voice" (קלא דלא אשתמע — the voice that is not heard) is the sound that arises from the deepest level of the heart — from the level of inner emotion (פנימיות המדות) that has not yet separated into chesed or gevurah. This voice is so interior that it cannot be articulated at all; trying to articulate it requires a tzimtzum — the inner voice must "contract," and only its outer dimension (חיצוניות הקול) can emerge and become differentiated into letters.
- **The five places of articulation as the "five gevurot."** When the simple voice is channeled through the five places of articulation (lips, teeth, palate, tongue, throat — corresponding to the five letters מנצפ"ך), it is divided into the building-blocks of speech. The Rashab identifies this with the five Gevurot (powers of restriction/judgment) in the Kabbalistic structure of Malchut — the very powers that enable the undivided Ein Sof to become differentiated into specific beings and specific Torah laws. Speech is the human analog of Malchut's creative articulation of reality.
- **Shemini Atzeret as the eighth: the point beyond Shabbat.** Seven corresponds to the seven days of creation (and their sefirot from Chesed to Malchut); the eighth is beyond the natural cycle — it is the point that transcends the seven but does not leave the world. Shemini Atzeret is the "eighth" because it is the day when the divine source (beyond the seven sefirot) "stays behind" (עצרת = detention, holding back) — lingering with Israel after the seven days of Sukkot, revealing its presence in a more intimate mode than the structured festival service. It corresponds to the inner voice (קלא דלא אשתמע) that is beyond all articulation.
- **Simchat Torah: celebrating the voice before letters.** The Rashab concludes with the connection to Torah: Torah as it exists at the level of Shemini Atzeret is Torah in its pre-articulated state — not yet divided into specific commandments and narratives, but Torah as the undivided divine wisdom. Simchat Torah celebrates not any particular teaching but the very existence of divine speech as such — the simple voice that underlies all speech. Dancing with the Torah (in circles — no beginning, no end) embodies the return to this pre-articulated unity.

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that Shemini Atzeret's independence from Sukkot corresponds to the dimension of the divine that is beyond the seven sefirot — the point where the "voice" has not yet become "speech," where the divine unity has not yet articulated into specific forms. Simchat Torah celebrates this pre-articulate Torah — and through dancing with it, the Jew touches the divine voice before all differentiation.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar is the capstone of the Tishrei section of Part 2. The entire arc from Rosh Hashanah through Shemini Atzeret has traced a complete cycle: Rosh Hashanah (coronation of Malchut from the outside) → Yom Kippur (ascent to Keter) → Sukkot (descent into physical joy, encompassing Or Ein Sof) → Shemini Atzeret (the point beyond the cycle, the pre-articulate divine unity). Now the hemshech will transition to the regular weekly parshiot, applying the same structural insights to the narrative of Bereishit — the story of creation itself.
