## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Noach 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Noach 1909-10. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ נח, העת"ר corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Parshat Noach 5670, constructs a detailed psychology of prayer by distinguishing between Chochmah and Binah as modes of knowing — specifically the difference between "seeing" (ראיה, associated with Chochmah) and "hearing" (שמיעה, associated with Binah) — and maps this onto the difference between the Shema prayer (seated, Binah-mode) and the Amidah (standing, Chochmah-mode), using Noach's unique form of "walking with God" as the paradigm for the higher mode.

### The Opening Move
The Torah opens Parshat Noach with a dense series of descriptions: "These are the generations of Noach; Noach was a righteous man, perfect in his generations; with God, Noach walked." Four questions the Rashab raises: (1) Why does the verse begin "these are the generations of Noach" when it proceeds to describe Noach himself rather than his descendants? (2) Why is the name "Noach" repeated twice? (3) What is the significance of "in his generations" — why does this qualification appear? (4) Why add "with God Noach walked" after already establishing he was "righteous and perfect"? And the Zohar adds: Noach was destined from the day of creation to be in the Ark — what is the inner meaning of Noach and the Ark being constituted for each other?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **Standing vs. Sitting in prayer: Chochmah vs. Binah.** The Rashab returns to a theme from the maamar on Parshat Lech Lecha (in an earlier section) regarding a Zoharic teaching: "Whenever a person enters those supernal days, they must stand on their legs, thighs and body together, like a male standing in strength." The three differences between standing and sitting are analyzed: (a) In sitting, the legs are lowered while the body is raised; in standing, all parts occupy the same level. (b) In sitting, the head is lowered; in standing, the head is raised. (c) In sitting, the feet are off the ground; in standing, the feet are grounded. These differences map onto the difference between Binah-consciousness (the seated, meditative cognition of Shema/Shacharit) and Chochmah-consciousness (the standing, direct-seeing cognition of the Amidah).
- **Binah as hearing/comprehension: the mode of Shema.** Binah corresponds to שמיעה (hearing) because it is a receptive, analytical mode — it receives information from outside itself, processes it into understanding, and builds a conceptual model. This is the mode of ההתבוננות (contemplative meditation) in Shema — one meditates on the proposition "God is One," building an inner model of divine unity through sustained reflection. This generates emotional love and awe as the meditative work produces its fruit.
- **Chochmah as seeing/knowing: the mode of the Amidah.** Chochmah corresponds to ראיה (seeing) because it is a direct, immediate knowing — not received from outside and processed, but arising from within as the grasping of essence. The paradigmatic Chochmah-experience is: a sage "sees the yet-to-come" (רואה את הנולד) — he sees, from within the present moment, the causal unfolding of reality. He does not need to reason his way to this; he perceives it directly. In divine service, the Amidah (standing prayer) corresponds to this: the soul stands not as a receiver of divine information but as a participant in the divine life — touching Chochmah's direct apprehension.
- **Why Chochmah's extension is limited.** The Rashab articulates a structurally important principle: Binah's extension (התפשטות) travels through the worlds in a graded, sequential manner — it can be transmitted, taught, passed from teacher to student. Chochmah, being non-derivative (it is the flash of insight itself, not the elaborated understanding), cannot extend by itself. It is transmitted only through the vessel of Binah. This is why Chochmah's direct illumination is necessarily limited in scope while Binah's elaborated understanding can travel indefinitely.
- **Noach as the paradigm of Chochmah-standing.** The phrase "את האלקים התהלך נח" — "with God, Noach walked" — is read by the Rashab as describing a mode of divine service that is continuously in the Chochmah-standing mode: not the periodic flashes of insight during meditation but an ongoing, continuous walking together with the divine. This is the mode of the צדיק (righteous person) who has internalized divine awareness to the point that it is not a state he enters but his continuous condition. "Walking" (התהלכות) rather than "standing" (עמידה) implies movement — not static insight but a dynamic, ongoing presence.
- **The Ark as the vessel of Binah.** If Noach corresponds to the Chochmah-mode of direct knowing, then the Ark (תיבה) corresponds to the Binah-mode — the elaborated vessel that contains and transmits. The Zohar's reading that Noach was destined from creation to be "in the Ark" expresses the fundamental principle: direct knowing (Chochmah/Noach) must always be housed within the elaborated vessel (Binah/Ark) in order to be preserved and transmitted. The Tzaddik who lives in pure Chochmah-awareness still needs the "Ark" of Torah-structure and community to pass that awareness forward.

### What It Gets To
The maamar arrives at a sophisticated account of the complementarity of Chochmah and Binah in divine service: neither is complete without the other. The "seeing" of Chochmah needs the "hearing" of Binah to become transmissible; the "hearing" of Binah needs the "seeing" of Chochmah to reach the source rather than merely circulate information. Noach's "walking with God" is the model for a divine service that has integrated both — his continuous movement in God's presence is Chochmah-knowing housed within the Binah-Ark of structured Torah life.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar deepens the hemshech's psychology of divine service by introducing the Chochmah/Binah distinction as a key to understanding the difference between ordinary contemplative prayer and the rarer mode of continuous divine awareness. Coming after the Bereishit maamar's analysis of creation's two-stage structure (potential/formal then actual/articulated), this maamar shows that the soul's modes of knowing (seeing vs. hearing) correspond exactly to these cosmic stages. The hemshech is building toward an integrated account of how the soul's highest knowing-mode (Chochmah-seeing) enables it to participate directly in the ongoing act of creation — the soul not merely as spectator of the divine life but as co-walker.
