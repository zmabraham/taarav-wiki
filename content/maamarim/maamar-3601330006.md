## Context

This maamar was delivered during **2nd day of Shavuot 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: The powers of the soul are 'inner' (pnimi) — they manifest specifically in organs suited to them. The intellect manifests in the brain, not the hand. Yet a person's will governs all limbs equally. What is the nature of will that transcends the inner/specific-organ relationship that governs all other soul-powers?

**Central Concept**: The distinction between Keter (will/Ratzon) and the other Sefirot: the inner soul-powers (intellect, emotions) are each tied to specific organs/vessels, while Keter/will is unbounded and encompasses all without limitation. This explains why Keter is called a 'crown' — it rests atop and encompasses the entire head rather than being localized within it.

**Resolution**: Will (Keter) transcends the inner/outer (pnimi/mekif) distinction that applies to lower powers. While intellect and emotions enter and are limited by their respective organs, will operates from 'outside' all of them — governing without being contained. This is why Keter is uniquely capable of acting as the intermediary (ממוצע, mamtze'et) between Ein Sof and the Sefirot.

## Deep Dive

### Opening Question

The maamar opens on the second day of Shavuot with a question drawn from the verse at Sinai: **"ויחן שם ישראל נגד ההר"** — and Rashi glosses it **"כאיש אחד בלב אחד"** (as one man with one heart). The kushya the maamar poses is: what was the necessity at Matan Torah specifically of Israel being unified as one? Why was this the indispensable precondition for receiving the Torah?

### Conceptual Architecture

The maamar answers by building a careful account of the Kelim (vessels) of Atzilut and why their mode of existence differs fundamentally from that of the Orot (lights).

**1. The twofold structure of Atzilut.** Atzilut serves as the intermediary (ממוצע) between Or Ein Sof and created worlds. As such it contains two dimensions: Orot (lights) — which are in a mode of boundlessness (בחי' בלי מה) — and Kelim (vessels) — which are in a mode of bounded existence (בחי' גבול). The Kelim are where the seven Divine Names (שמות שאינן נמחקים) reside: the Name "אל" in the vessel of Chesed, "אלקים" in the vessel of Gevurah, etc. But these Names animate the life-force of the vessels themselves (החיות של הכלי), not the lights clothed within them.

**2. Origin of the Kelim: distance and concealment.** The source-text states the key principle: **"רשימו היא בחי' כח נעלם"** — the Reshimu (the residual impression left after the Tzimtzum) is a concealed power, and everything that flows from it carries the character of concealment. The Kelim draw their inner vitality from the Reshimu, and therefore they subsist in a mode of hiddenness rather than revealed closeness. This is why the Zohar says **"כד אנת תסתלק... אשתארו כגופא בלא נשמתא"** — when the light withdraws, the Kelim remain like a body without a soul, because their own life-force is already in the mode of concealment.

**3. Formation of the Kelim: the collision of Or Yashar and Or Chozer.** The maamar invokes the Etz Chaim: the Kelim are formed when Or Yashar (direct light) and Or Chozer (reflected light) strike one another, and letters "fall" from that collision. This falling is itself a mode of distance (בחי' ריחוק). The analogy given is two scholars in sharp debate: from the clash of contradictory positions, a new and lesser insight is born — not an unfolding of the original understanding, but something produced by negation. **"ולהיות שבאה ע"י סתירת וביטול שכלו... ה"ה סברא קטנה בערך עצם ההשגה"** — because it arose through the refutation and nullification of the previous position, it is a diminished insight relative to the original. So too the Kelim: their formation is through a "falling," and thus they are inherently at a remove from their source.

**4. Paradox: the Kelim are nonetheless actual Elokut.** Despite their mode of distance, the Kelim of Atzilut are genuinely Elokut — divine. The maamar uses the image of **"ניצוץ הנבדל מן האבוקה"** (a spark separated from the torch): the spark is literally fire from the torch itself, yet it is separate and does not illuminate like the flame of the torch. So the Kelim are Elokut that has emerged in a mode of separation.

**5. The superiority of the Kelim's bitul.** The maamar then introduces a surprising inversion: the very distance that characterizes the Kelim gives them a capacity for bitul (self-nullification) that surpasses even the bitul of the Orot. The Reshimu — root of the Kelim — was untouched by the Tzimtzum itself; the Kav (the line of light, root of the Orot) is already the light that withdrew and was then re-extended. Therefore, the Kelim's root is in a sense deeper.

### Resolution and Chiddush

This is the chiddush: **"ויחן שם ישראל... כאיש אחד"** describes not merely social unity but the bitul ha-Kelim — the nullification of the vessels. Human souls in their essence (bechinas ha-Orot) are always unified; their diversity arises from their Kelim, from the differentiated faculties and dispositions each soul inhabits. At Sinai the people achieved the bitul of their Kelim — the division dissolved — and this was the necessary preparation for Torah, which is the **"המשכת גילוי עצמות א"ס ב"ה"**. Only the bitul of the Kelim creates the vessel through which Ein Sof's very essence can be drawn down. Unity at Sinai was not incidental; it was structurally required.
