## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 2nd day of Sukkot 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: The festival of Sukkot (Night of 2nd day of Sukkot 1902-03) involves dwelling in temporary booths. How does this relate to the Hemshech's theme of divine habitation (dira betachtonim)? Why is the temporary, fragile structure of the Sukkah specifically the vehicle for this experience rather than the permanent Temple structure?

**Central Concept**: The Sukkah as a model for Or Mekif (surrounding light) — the divine presence that encompasses without fully entering. The Rashab uses the four species (ארבעת המינים) to explore how different types of divine service (prayer, Torah, action, character) each represent a different 'species' of Or Chozer, all of which are unified in the Sukkah-space.

**Resolution**: The Sukkah's temporary, encompassing structure specifically evokes the Or Mekif mode of divine presence. By dwelling temporarily in this Or-Mekif space, the Jew's soul accesses its Chaya-level (the soul-level corresponding to surrounding light) — producing a quality of divine contact that permanent structures, associated with Or Pnimi, cannot provide.
