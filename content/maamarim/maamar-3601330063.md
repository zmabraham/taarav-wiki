## Context

This maamar was delivered during **2nd day of Shavuot 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar builds the analysis of two modes of divine giving — השפעה מצד הביטול (giving from a place of self-nullification) and השפעה מצד הגדולה (giving from a place of greatness and abundance) — and deploys this distinction to solve why the sefirot of Tohu, despite their enormous power, failed to give to one another. The maamar pivots from ontology (why Tohu shattered) to the logic of giving, establishing that true cosmic generativity requires the right inner orientation.

### The Opening Move
The verse from Ecclesiastes 2:13 — "I saw that wisdom has an advantage over foolishness, as light has an advantage over darkness" — opens the maamar with a seemingly trivial observation. King Solomon, the wisest of all men, employs his wisdom to announce that wisdom is better than foolishness? This is self-evident to anyone. What novel truth is Solomon communicating? The Rashab uses this apparent triviality as a koan that unlocks the inner logic of how one mode of understanding generates, and another mode merely registers without generating.

### The Argumentative Arc
- The previous maamar established that Tohu's sefirot could not give to one another because each was self-complete (shleimut atzmi). But there is an objection: even granting that Tohu's sefirot lacked the mode of giving that arises from bitul (where the giver sees the recipient as worthy precisely because the giver has no inflated self-image), they should have possessed the other mode of giving — giving from sheer abundance and greatness. A very powerful and wealthy entity gives generously precisely because its greatness means giving costs it nothing. Why didn't Tohu's sefirot give from this second mode?
- The Rashab develops the two modes of giving: (a) chesed d'Ze'ir Anpin — giving rooted in bitul, where the giver descends to the recipient's level, sees the recipient's worth, and gives with genuine engagement; (b) rov chesed (abundant chesed, as in Arich Anpin) — giving rooted in the very excess of the giver's greatness, where giving is as natural as sunlight radiating without cost or deliberation. In human terms: the man of great wealth gives to all equally, to great and small alike, not because he considers each person's merit but simply because his goodness overflows.
- For the higher mode (giving from greatness) to operate, the giver must truly be in a mode where giving involves no loss, no diminution, no "spending" of self. This is only possible when the giver is in the mode of or (light) rather than shefa (a flow of substance). Or radiates without the source being diminished; shefa depletes.
- The sefirot of Tohu, despite their enormous amplitude of light (תוקף האור), were in the mode of metziyut (positive existential presence). Because they were in the mode of metziyut, their giving would have constituted a transfer of substance — a real flow from their abundance to the other — which would have implied both a real other (a recipient with metziyut of its own) and a real diminution. Their mode of self-experience made genuine or-type giving impossible.
- The wisdom-advantage of Ecclesiastes is now decoded: wisdom does not merely "know more" than foolishness — it generates, it illuminates, it propagates itself without being depleted. This is the advantage of or over darkness: darkness is a state; light is a process. The advantage of wisdom over foolishness is not a static comparison but a structural one — wisdom, when properly rooted in bitul, gives itself away and thereby grows.

### What It Gets To
The maamar arrives at a systematic account of the two modes of chesed and their cosmic roots: chesed from bitul (Chesed of Ze'ir Anpin, named for the nullification-mode of Shem מ"ה) and chesed from greatness (rov chesed of Arich, rooted in the boundless or of Ein Sof). For the sefirot of Tikkun to both give and receive, they must operate in the first mode. The second mode — giving from pure greatness without diminution — is properly characteristic only of the level above Atzilut, of Or Ein Sof itself, which is in the mode of pure or and never shefa.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
The second day of Shavuot 5664 advances the hemshech's systematic analysis of Tohu and Tikkun into the domain of giving and receiving — which is ultimately the domain of creation itself. The hemshech has been moving from ontology (what the sefirot are) to dynamics (how they interact). This maamar introduces the or/shefa distinction, which becomes a major conceptual axis in the subsequent sections: the question of how divine light moves through the chain of worlds — whether as sunlight (or, without diminution) or as water flowing downhill (shefa, with depletion of source) — is the hemshech's central question about the mechanism of tzimtzum and Tikkun. Solomon's "wisdom advantage" is thus a preview of the fundamental insight: the Torah, as or rather than shefa, can permeate all levels without being diminished, which is why it can serve as the medium of divine-human encounter.
