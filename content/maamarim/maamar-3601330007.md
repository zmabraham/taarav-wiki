## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Naso and Beha'alotcha 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: The verse 'Raise the head of the sons of Gershon, also they, by their families' — why does it say 'also they' (גם הם, gam hem)? What does this addition mean? All the Levitical families were counted and given tasks — why single out the Gershonites with this 'also'?

**Central Concept**: The distinction between Chochmah (Wisdom) and the nature of the divine 'nothing' (Ayin, אין). The Zohar's teaching that 'wisdom emerges from nothing' (והחכמה מאין תמצא) is explored: this 'nothing' refers not to absolute non-existence but to the divine level of Keter, which is 'nothing' relative to lower levels because it exceeds all categories and names. The relationship between Keter (the 'nothing') and Chochmah (the 'something' that emerges from it).

**Resolution**: The 'also they' (gam hem) of the Gershonites represents the quality of elevation that comes precisely from those who serve in a secondary, supporting role — analogous to how Chochmah, which seems to 'emerge from nothing,' is actually more elevated than what appears to be primary. The Rashab uses this to develop the paradox that the highest spiritual levels appear to be 'nothing' from below, yet are the very source of all 'something.'

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the critical distinction between the *Lights* and *Vessels* of Atzilut as two genuinely different metaphysical modes within a single world — one partaking of Ein Sof's boundlessness (gilu'i ha-ha'alem, a revealing of what was latent), the other constituting a true ontological novelty standing in absolute incommensurability (ein erech) to its source. It does so by resolving the standing tension in the Zohar: *Ve-ha-Chochmah me-Ayin timatzei* — "Wisdom emerges from Ayin (Keter/Nothing)."

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens from the doubling in Numbers 4:22 — "Raise the head of the sons of Gershon, *also they* (גם הם), by their families." Why "also they"? The sons of Kehath had already been counted in the previous parasha; the Gershonites are counted here with this appended "gam hem." The question is exegetically subtle: what status-claim does *gam hem* make for the Gershonites that required such an addition? The puzzle is resolved through the hemshech's ongoing framework: the Gershonites carried the *coverings* of the Mishkan (the outer curtains), while the Kehathites carried the *sacred vessels* themselves. Coverings correspond to the Or Mekif; vessels to the Or Pnimi. The "also they" signals that the outer/surrounding function is not secondary but has its own elevation, one that in some sense exceeds the inner.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The preceding maamarim established that Atzilut has two aspects**: gilu'i ha-ha'alem (revealing what was latent) in its Lights, and ein erech (incommensurability) in its Vessels. The Lights derive from the Kav (the post-Tzimtzum conduit) which in turn extends from pre-Tzimtzum Ein Sof — so the Lights maintain a quality of Ein Sof within Atzilut. The Vessels, however, stand in ein erech to Ein Sof, just as finite creation stands in ein erech to Atzilut.

- **The paradox of the Lights**: if the Kav extends from pre-Tzimtzum Ein Sof, the Lights should be truly infinite — how then can they be the Or Pnimi of specific, differentiated Sefirot (Chochmah is one thing, Chesed another)? The maamar confronts this from the source text's chapter 12: even the Lights of the ten Sefirot are not absolutely simple in the way pre-Tzimtzum Ein Sof is simple, because they are *ten* rather than one. The Sefer Yetzirah's "ten and not nine" (עשר ולא תשע) indicates that even in the Lights there is structured differentiation — they have ten-ness.

- **The Sefirot of Atik Yomin** are deployed as the proof case: Atik has ten Sefirot yet no Vessels (as the Etz Chayyim states, אין אלהים עמדי at the level of Atik — Elohim/Vessels are absent there). This shows that ten-ness belongs to the Lights themselves even without Vessels. Therefore the ten-ness of the Lights within Atzilut proper is also Light-ten-ness, not merely an artifact of their Vessels.

- **The function of Tzimtzum** is then analyzed: in the pre-Tzimtzum state, all ten hidden Sefirot (ha-sefirot ha-genuzot) were in a state of complete inter-fusion and unity — no individual Sefirah stood out with its own presence. The Tzimtzum's function was not to create the Lights (they pre-existed) but to *particularize* them: to allow Chochmah to stand forth distinctly as Chochmah rather than remaining fused with all others. This standing-forth involves a diminishment of the Light (מיעוט האור) and simultaneously gives each Light its distinct character, enabling it to clothe itself in a specific Vessel.

- **The analogy of the word/letters**: the source text deploys an extended analogy from the Tanya's Iggeret HaKodesh ch. 12 — when letters combine into a word (e.g., ברוך, *barukh*), each letter carries its own vital force; but the word as a whole carries a *higher* unifying power (כח כללי, a comprehensive force) that transcends the individual letters and binds them into a semantic unit. This combining-force (כח המצרף) comes not from the *meaning* (the intellect clothed in the word) but from a higher faculty — the faculty of Chochmah, specifically Abba — which determines *that these particular letters* and not others should carry this meaning. The intellect alone does not necessitate a particular letter-combination.

- **The Gershonites / *gam hem*** resolution: the coverings (Gershonite task) correspond to the Or Mekif and to the sefirot ha-genuzot's pre-Tzimtzum inter-fusion, which is a *higher* level than the differentiated Or Pnimi that the Kehathites' vessels carry. "Also they" — the Gershonites — are elevated in the same way that the pre-Tzimtzum hidden state is elevated above the post-Tzimtzum differentiated state, even though from below the differentiated seems more present.

### What It Gets To

The maamar arrives at a three-tier picture: (1) The ten hidden Sefirot in Ein Sof before Tzimtzum — pure unity/fusion without distinct presence; (2) the Lights of Atzilut after Tzimtzum — distinct yet still partaking of Ein Sof's quality (gilu'i ha-ha'alem); (3) the Vessels of Atzilut — standing in absolute incommensurability (ein erech) to their source, constituting genuine novelty. And it adds the Tzimtzum's role as particularizer rather than creator of the Lights. This is the first maamar to analyze what the Tzimtzum *does* to the Lights in structural detail.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Shavuot trilogy established Keter's structure and Atzilut's dual mode; this maamar (first of the weekly-parasha series) builds the hemshech's account of how the Lights came to be differentiated — the mechanism of Tzimtzum as particularization. It also introduces the word/letter analogy from Iggeret HaKodesh, which the hemshech will return to repeatedly when analyzing how higher-level faculty determines lower-level expression.
