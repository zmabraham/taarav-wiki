## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Lech Lecha 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Parshat Lech Lecha 5670, constructs an account of Avraham's command "Lech lecha" (Go for yourself) as the paradigm of the soul's journey from attachment to its natural environment into the open expanse of divine service — specifically mapping this onto the Amidah prayer as the "ladder set on earth with its head reaching heaven," which draws down the revelation of divine light into Malchut.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens with the verse: "God said to Avram: Go for yourself from your land, from your birthplace, and from your father's house, to the land that I will show you" (Gen. 12:1). Three textual difficulties: (1) The word "לך" appears superfluous — "לך לך" should simply be "לך" (go); what does the doubling add? (2) Why does God specify where Avram is going from (land, birthplace, father's house) rather than simply telling him where to go? (3) The destination is kept vague — "to the land that I will show you" — why not specify it? And in the subsequent promises, what is the greatness of making Avram's name great (ואגדלה שמך) for someone who already described himself as "dust and ashes"?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **"Lech lecha": going toward the self.** The Rashab reads "לך לך" not as emphatic doubling but as directed reflexivity — go toward yourself. The leaving of land, birthplace, and father's house is not merely geographical displacement but an inward journey: stripping away three levels of external identity (national, cultural, familial) to reach the bare essence of the soul beneath all its acquired coverings. This is the movement of Teshuvah and of the Amidah.
- **The "ladder" of the Amidah.** The Rashab returns to his analysis of the Amidah as "a ladder planted on earth with its head reaching heaven" (Jacob's ladder image). The Amidah begins in Asiyah (the standing body, grounded) and rises through the sefirot until it reaches "שמי השמים" — the heaven above the heavens — the level of Keter above Atzilut itself. "ארוממך" (I will exalt You) names this movement: through the prayer-ladder, Malchut is raised back toward its source in Keter.
- **The second direction: drawing down.** Having established the ascent (ארוממך from below), the Rashab develops the corresponding descent. The full spiritual movement is two-directional: the soul ascends through prayer to Keter, and the revelation of Ein Sof then descends into Malchut. This is expressed in "ועמדו רגליו ביום ההוא" (Zechariah 14:4) — the divine "standing" at the lowest point, the presence of Ein Sof in the world below.
- **"To the land I will show you": Malchut as the unrevealed destination.** The vagueness of the destination is not indefiniteness but a pointing toward something only accessible through the journey itself. "The Land" (הארץ) is Malchut — and Malchut's inner face can only be reached, not pre-specified. The instruction is: begin, and the destination will be disclosed as you travel. This is the structure of genuine divine service.
- **Even the highest angels cannot see: the necessity of the inward journey.** The Rashab develops that even the Chayot HaKodesh (who carry the divine Throne in Beriyah) do not see the Glory directly — "ki lo yir'ani ha'adam va'chai" — because they perceive only the outer radiance of Malchut's vessels. The light of Atzilut terminates at Malchut d'Atzilut. Only through the inward journey of "lech lecha" — the Amidah's stripping of identity — can a soul access what the highest angels cannot see: the inner face of Malchut connected to Keter.
- **"Making Avram's name great": elevation of the שם-dimension.** Returning to the שם/אור distinction from maamar 76: Avram's "name" is the soul-dimension that interfaces with the world as a governing Name. Making his name great means connecting this שם-dimension back to its divine source. The self-description "dust and ashes" is not a contradiction: precisely one who has nullified the ego (dust and ashes) can have the true שם elevated to its divine height. Total nullification enables maximal Name-elevation.

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that "lech lecha" is both instruction to Avraham and a structural description of the Amidah: go away from all external identities, enter the inner self, and from that point begin the ladder-ascent toward Keter. The destination ("the land I will show you") is the inner face of Malchut revealed only through the journey itself. Avraham's greatness is the model: total self-nullification combined with the elevation of the Name-dimension to divine heights.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar develops the hemshech's treatment of the Amidah as the archetypal Or Chozer vehicle, now grounded in the Torah's narrative. In the Tishrei maamarim, the mechanism was analyzed theoretically (what Rosh Hashanah / Yom Kippur achieve). In the Bereishit-Noach-Lech Lecha sequence, the same mechanism is grounded biographically: Avraham's journey is the first historical instance of a human soul actualizing the full ladder-ascent. The hemshech integrates its Kabbalistic architecture with the Torah's narrative of human spiritual development — showing that the Patriarchs' lives are not merely historical episodes but the original actualization of the cosmic structures the hemshech has been building.
