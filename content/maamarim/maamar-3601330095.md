## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayigash 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the principle of "vigash" — the act of drawing near in confrontation — as the paradigm for how the soul at the level of Malchut breaks through the barrier between worlds and achieves direct contact with the higher Partzuf of Ze'ir Anpin, translating the narrative of Yehuda's approach to Yosef into a precise model of how Or Chozer must be bold, confrontational, and complete in order to draw down the highest revelation.

### The Opening Move

The parasha opens: "ויגש אליו יהודה" — "And Yehuda drew near to him." Rashi emphasizes the multiplicity of meanings in "vigash": it can mean drawing near for battle, for appeasement, or for prayer. The Rashab probes the unusual force of this word: Yehuda does not merely "come" or "speak" — he thrusts himself forward. What is the inner significance of this confrontational mode of approach, and why is Yehuda specifically the one to make this approach rather than any of the other brothers?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Yehuda represents the sefira of Malchut among the brothers**: in the kabbalistic mapping of the twelve tribes, Yehuda corresponds to Malchut — the lowest sefira, the one whose function is reception, kingship, and speech. The tribe of Yehuda gives Israel its king (David) and its royal lineage precisely because Malchut is the sefirah of governance and the point at which all higher energies are concentrated into actualized rule.
- **Yosef represents Yesod — the channel that must be activated by Malchut's approach**: Yosef is consistently identified with Yesod in the hemshech's framework. The confrontation between Yehuda and Yosef is therefore the meeting of Malchut and Yesod — two sefirot that must be in active relationship for the divine system to function. Yesod channels the light downward into Malchut, but for that channel to open, Malchut must actively draw near (vigash).
- **The "vigash" as Or Chozer's assertive dimension**: Or Chozer is not merely a passive upwelling; at its most potent, it has an assertive quality — it presses upward with force. Yehuda's bold approach to Yosef is the Torah's image of Or Chozer in its confrontational mode, where the lower level (Malchut) does not wait for the light to descend but reaches up and demands the connection. This is precisely the dynamic of teshuvah (return) at its most powerful.
- **The stakes: Binyamin = the capacity for Or Pnimi of the highest order**: the entire confrontation is about Binyamin, whom Yosef is holding as hostage. Binyamin represents, in the hemshech's schema, Or Pnimi of the level of Keter — the most hidden, most essential inner light. Yehuda's demand that Binyamin be released is therefore Malchut's demand that the innermost divine light be released into actualization in the lower worlds. The vigash is not merely about one brother but about the entire question of whether pnimiyut ha-Keter can enter the domain of the lower worlds.
- **Yehuda's guarantee ("anochi e'ervenu") as the structure of Or Chozer's full commitment**: Yehuda had made himself a guarantor (arev) for Binyamin — he staked his own existence on Binyamin's safe return. This total commitment is the structure of Or Chozer when it reaches Keter-level: the ascending light puts itself entirely at stake, offering itself as the vessel of the highest light. This is the avodah of total bitul (nullification) — not passive disappearance but an active staking of one's entire being in service of the higher connection.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that "vigash" — drawing near with total, confrontational commitment — is the necessary mode of Malchut's engagement with Yesod in order to unlock the channel of Or Yashar flowing from Keter-level light. The story of Yehuda and Yosef is the Torah's account of how the bold avodah of the Jewish people (Malchut) presses its claim on the divine channel (Yesod) to release the most concealed light (Keter/Binyamin) into the world.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Vayigash maamar advances the hemshech's analysis of how Or Chozer achieves its most powerful operation: not by gentle rising but by bold, committed "drawing near." Following the Vayeshev maamar's exploration of the tzaddik-as-Yesod in concealment, this maamar shows the complementary side — how Malchut (the neshama at the level of the lower worlds) actively demands the connection, creating the dynamic tension that makes the full circuit of Or Yashar/Or Chozer possible. The vigash will be revisited in later maamarim as the model for Knesset Yisrael's relationship to the divine.
