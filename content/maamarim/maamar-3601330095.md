## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayigash 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### Opening Kushya

The maamar opens with a double textual difficulty cited from the Or HaChayyim (או"ח): the verse states "ויגש אליו יהודה ויאמר בי אדוני" — but Yehuda and the brothers had already entered Yosef's house and spoken with him. So what fresh "drawing near" does the verse describe? And why does Yehuda open with "בי אדוני" — words of petition — when the actual conciliatory language ("ואל יחר אפך") comes only afterward? The maamar also asks why Yehuda argues that he himself should remain as slave in place of Binyamin — what advantage does Yehuda see in himself over Binyamin?

### Conceptual Architecture

The Rashab grounds the answer in a sustained analysis of the structure of Atzilut built across the preceding chapters (paragraphs שפו–שצ) of the hemshech. The key move is distinguishing the upper nine sefirot from Malchut. The nine sefirot — including the middot such as Chesed and Chokhmah — are described as being in a state of "עלי' ודבקות" (ascent and cleaving) in their source in Ein Sof. They exhibit bitul (nullification) in two modes: the Orot (lights) in "ביטול בעצם" because they perceive their source directly; the Kelim (vessels) in "ביטול בהעדר תפיסת מקום" (nullification through absence of self-assertion). Crucially, even the middot as they appear in Atzilut remain in a state of peshitut (simplicity, undifferentiated expansiveness) — like a stirring of love in the heart that has not yet taken particular form. The principle is: "אידי דטריד למבלע לא פליט" — absorbed in receiving from above, the sefirot do not radiate downward.

Malchut is categorically different. It is "עלמא דאתגלי'" — the world of revelation — and functions as descent and speech rather than ascent and silence. The verse "רוח הבהמה יורדת למטה" (Kohelet 3:21) marks Malchut as the sefira whose essential nature is downward flow and manifestation. The maamar traces how the Malchut within each sefira is what converts that sefira's peshitut into a particular, finite expression that lower worlds can receive.

### Key Concepts

- **אדם דאצילות — Adameh l'Elyon**: the entire structure of the ten sefirot as "similar to the supernal," rooted in their bitul toward Ein Sof
- **ביטול האורות / ביטול הכלים**: two grades of nullification — self-dissolution in the lights, absence of self-assertion in the vessels
- **פשיטות המדות**: the quality of undifferentiated expansiveness in the sefirot of Atzilut, analogous to love felt in the heart before it takes any particular shape
- **מל' — כח ההתגלות**: Malchut as the power of disclosure, structured as speech (דבור עליון), downward-oriented and particularizing

### Resolution and Chiddush

The chiddush crystallizes around "אנכי שלומי אמוני ישראל" — Malchut completes (משלמת) Israel above (Ze'ir Anpin / Yosef), because only through Malchut does the highest intention of Atzilut reach actual expression in Beriah-Yetzirah-Asiyah. Critically, Malchut's root is higher than all nine sefirot: "שרשה למעלה מבחי' החכ' היינו מבחי' הכתר" — its source is in Keter/Anochi, which is why it alone has the power to draw down and reveal. The verse "מי שם פה לאדם... הלא אנכי ה'" is invoked to show that speech (Malchut) flows specifically from Anochi, the level above Chokhmah.

This resolves the narrative questions: "ויגש אליו יהודה" — Yehuda (Malchut) performing haala'at man (elevation from below), the inner dimension of tefillah; "בי אדוני" — "through me specifically" the hamshachot (drawn-down divine revelations) of the twelve middle blessings of Shmoneh Esrei are effected. Yehuda's argument for substituting himself for Binyamin is thus not about personal virtue but about metaphysical function: the completion of the divine intention requires Malchut's engagement, not merely Yesod's (Binyamin's = tzaddik tachton). The vigash of Yehuda to Yosef enacts semichat ge'ulah l'tefillah — the joining of Yesod (ge'ulah/Yosef) with Malchut (tefillah/Yehuda) — but in the direction opposite to what the Zohar's usual formulation implies: here it is Malchut that approaches Yesod, because the downward flow itself requires the ascending initiative of Malchut to unlock it.
