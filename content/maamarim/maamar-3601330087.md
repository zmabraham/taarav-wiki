## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayera 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Parshat Vayera 5670, uses the narrative of God's appearance to Avraham at the entrance of his tent in the heat of the day ("בחום היום") as the lens through which the Rashab develops the distinction between the mode of divine disclosure during illness and recuperation — specifically, what it means for the Shechina (divine Presence) to appear at the "entrance" (פתח) rather than from within, and how this maps onto the distinction between Or Pnimi (inner permeating light) and Or HaSovev (encompassing light).

### The Opening Move
The parasha opens: "God appeared to him (Avraham) in the plains of Mamre while he sat at the entrance of the tent in the heat of the day" (Gen. 18:1). Three questions the Rashab raises: (1) Why does God appear specifically "in the heat of the day" (בחום היום) — why not at a more comfortable time? (2) Why does Avraham sit specifically at the entrance (פתח) of the tent — why not inside? (3) After this divine appearance, the narrative immediately shifts to three men approaching — are these the same as the divine appearance, or different? And what is the inner meaning of Avraham running to greet guests in the midst of a divine vision?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **The setting: Avraham's recuperation after circumcision.** The Rashab follows the Talmudic tradition that this scene occurred on the third day after Avraham's circumcision (the most painful day of recuperation). God appeared specifically then — to perform bikur cholim (visiting the sick). The question is what mode of divine presence is activated specifically during illness and why.
- **The "entrance" as the threshold between inner and outer.** "Peta'ch" (פתח, entrance) in Kabbalistic analysis is the threshold — the point where the inner space (inside the tent, Or Pnimi, the internalized divine light) meets the outer space (outside the tent, Or HaSovev, the encompassing divine light). Avraham sitting at this threshold is not incidental but describes his spiritual state: in recuperation, the soul partially withdraws from its full engagement with the body and hovers at the threshold between the inner life and the encompassing divine.
- **The "heat of the day" as the Or HaSovev.** The Rashab reads "חום היום" (the heat of the day) as pointing to the solar quality of the encompassing divine light — not the differentiated light of the sefirot that enters the world in structured form (Or Pnimi) but the undifferentiated, all-encompassing divine heat that surrounds reality without being absorbed into it. On ordinary days, the full force of this Or HaSovev would be unbearable to a person of Avraham's sensitivity; but in the state of post-circumcision recuperation, the ego-dimension is weakened, the boundary between self and world is thinner, and the Or HaSovev becomes more accessible.
- **The circumcision as removal of the ערלה (foreskin/blockage).** Circumcision (מילה) removes the ערלה — literally the foreskin, which in Kabbalistic terms represents the outermost shell (klipa) that prevents the divine light from making direct contact with the innermost point of the human soul. After circumcision, the Yesod (channel of divine flow) is uncovered — and Avraham, three days later, is at the moment when this uncovering is most physiologically and spiritually acute. The divine appearance at this moment is not despite the pain but because of the spiritual opening created by the pain.
- **The three guests: Or Yashar in its three modes.** The three "men" (men/angels) who appear to Avraham correspond, in the Rashab's analysis, to the three Patriarchal qualities that Avraham embodies in differentiated form — or, in the hemshech's framework, to the three modes of Or Yashar descending through the three worlds of Beriyah-Yetzirah-Asiyah. Avraham's running to greet them (in the middle of a divine appearance) is not an interruption of the divine encounter but its completion: the Or HaSovev (the encompassing divine light revealed at the tent's entrance) must be received and channeled into specific acts of chesed (hospitality) — Or Pnimi at work. The full spiritual movement is: Or HaSovev appears → is received by the soul at the threshold → is translated into Or Pnimi through the specific acts of chesed in the world.
- **"He who has eyes to see, let him see": the Rashab's epistemological point.** The Rashab notes that Binah is the mode of "hearing" (שמיעה) but Chochmah is the mode of "seeing" (ראיה) — and Avraham, as the Chochmah-tzaddik (cf. maamar 85 on Noach's Chochmah-walking), receives the divine appearance as a direct seeing (ראיה) rather than mediated hearing. This is why the text says "וירא אליו ה'" — "God appeared to him" — using the root ראה (seeing): the mode of divine revelation here is the Chochmah-seeing mode, not the Binah-hearing mode.

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that Avraham's spiritual state after circumcision — at the threshold, in the heat of the day, in pain — constitutes the ideal condition for receiving the Or HaSovev: ego-boundaries are thin, the ערלה (blockage) has been removed, and the soul hovers at the exact junction between inner permeation and outer encompassment. The divine "appearance" to Avraham is the model for how the Or HaSovev can become accessible to a human soul — not through ascetic withdrawal from the world but through the vulnerability of illness and the radical openness that comes with having "removed" the external shell.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar completes the Bereishit-to-Vayera sub-sequence of Part 2, which has traced the development of divine service from Noach's Chochmah-walking (maamar 85) through Avraham's Amidah-journey (maamar 86) to the culminating experience of direct Or HaSovev revelation (maamar 87). The three maamarim together form a progression: knowing God through elaborated understanding (Binah/Noach) → ascending toward God through the prayer-ladder (Chochmah-ascending/Avraham's Lech Lecha) → receiving God's direct encompassing presence at the threshold (Or HaSovev/Avraham's Vayera). This threefold structure will continue to organize the hemshech's treatment of the Avraham narratives and ultimately of all divine service: the soul that learns, ascends, and is then disclosed to — the full arc of Or Chozer reaching Keter and Or Yashar returning as encompassing revelation.
