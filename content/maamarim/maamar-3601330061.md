## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 1st day of Shavuot 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar constructs a precise metaphysics of why God's self-disclosure at Sinai used the restrictive Name Elokim — the Name of tzimtzum and concealment — rather than the Tetragrammaton (YKVK), which denotes unmediated divine light. In resolving this paradox, the Rashab advances the hemshech's analysis of the sefirot of Tohu by establishing that the root cause of their fragmentation was not merely "excessive light" but a specific mode of self-experience: rommemut (exaltation) as metziyut — selfhood experienced as an isolated positive presence.

### The Opening Move
The verse "And God (Elokim) spoke all these words" (Exodus 20:1) — the opening of the Ten Commandments — poses an acute question: the entire event of Matan Torah was a revelation of Or Ein Sof in the most literal sense ("God descended upon Mount Sinai," the people "saw" the sound, the Torah opened with Anochi YKVK). Why, then, does the verse describing this supreme revelation use precisely the Name associated with tzimtzum, concealment, and the limiting power?

### The Argumentative Arc
- The maamar continues from the previous chapter (paragraph 231 of the hemshech) by returning to the puzzle of Tohu: the sefirot of Tohu were in a state of mutual exclusion, unable to coexist — yet if each sefirah possessed total existential dominance (such that the other was "not there"), how could the other's presence cause conflict? If it truly has no standing, it should be invisible, not antagonizing.
- The Rashab introduces a crucial distinction: there are two modes of rommemut. The first is rommemut as an act of self-elevation — the being elevates itself above others, but this very posture implies it registers the other as real and threatening to its position. The second is rommemut atzmit (essential exaltedness) — a state so elevated that it is literally not in the mode of metziyut (experienced existence) at all, and therefore everything else simply does not appear as having metziyut either.
- Key example: a truly humble person does not "efface" others — rather, the other simply does not appear as a competing presence to begin with. Similarly, a person of great worldly stature who is self-absorbed sees every other person as a threat or reduction; but true divine majesty (as in Keter) simply does not experience limitation because it is not in the mode of experiential-selfhood.
- The sefirot of Tohu had the first kind of rommemut: each was "great" (in the mode of מלוכה, kingship, which is Shem ב"ן), but this very greatness was in the mode of metziyut — a sensed, registered reality. Therefore the other sefirot also appeared as sensed realities, hence as threats. This is why they "could not tolerate one another."
- The Rashab sharpens: before the tzimtzum, even the "thought" or "sketching" of creation in potential (גליף גליפו בטה"ע) was not in the mode of metziyut. The category of metziyut begins only with the tzimtzum. The sefirot of Tikkun are still in modes of metziyut — but their rommemut is of the second kind (tied to their bitul, self-nullification), so the other sefirot do not appear as metziyut-threats.
- The resolution to the Sinai question then follows: Elokim is used precisely because Matan Torah was the moment when Or Ein Sof descended not in spite of tzimtzum but through it — the revelation was so total that the Name of concealment itself became a vehicle of disclosure. Only Elokim — the Name that "manages" the dialectic between concealment and revelation — could properly describe the moment when infinite light entered finite vessels.

### What It Gets To
The maamar establishes that true divine immanence requires the Elokim-mode: not a bypass of tzimtzum but its transformation into a transparent medium. The paradox of Sinai (infinity revealed through finitude) is the paradox of Tikkun itself: a cosmos structured so that the limiting Name becomes the disclosive Name. The hemshech here articulates the philosophical grammar of how divine hiddenness and divine revelation are not opposites but phases of a single process.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar (first night of Shavuot 5664) deepens the hemshech's sustained analysis of Tohu and Tikkun by introducing the category of rommemut atzmit — essential exaltedness — which becomes a key conceptual tool for the rest of the discourse. The distinction between metziyut-rommemut and atzmit-rommemut maps directly onto the distinction between Shem ב"ן (Tohu) and Shem מ"ה (Tikkun), which the hemshech has been developing. Matan Torah is now readable as the paradigmatic moment of atzmit-rommemut entering the world — the moment when the divine self, precisely because it is not in the mode of threatened selfhood, can descend into the most finite structure (the letters of Torah) without being diminished.
