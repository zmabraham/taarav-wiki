## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Shelach 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar deploys Parshat Shelach — the episode of the twelve spies who scouted the Land of Israel and whose crisis of faith produced forty years of desert wandering — to advance the hemshech's analysis of the relationship between mochin (intellectual faculties) and midot (emotional attributes) in the domain of belief and action. The spies' failure is read not as a moral failure of courage but as a structural failure of sefirotic integration: their midot overwhelmed their mochin, not through excess of passion but through a subtle collapse of the Tikkun-orientation.

### The Opening Move
The narrative of "שלח לך אנשים" — "Send for yourself men to scout the land" — raises the fundamental question: the spies were the greatest men of Israel, one prince from each tribe. They had access to the highest levels of prophetic consciousness. How did they fail? More puzzling: the land's goodness was not in question — they themselves brought back its extraordinary fruit. The failure was in the interpretive move from "the land is good" to "nevertheless, we cannot go up because the people are too strong." What went wrong at the level of sefirotic structure?

### The Argumentative Arc
- The Rashab builds from the mochin-midot relationship developed in the Naso maamar: when mochin properly illuminate the midot, the midot naturally soften and become capable of Tikkun. But this process requires that the mochin be genuinely rooted in bitul — oriented toward divine truth rather than toward self-preservation.
- The spies' intellect was operating in the mode of self-referential analysis: they evaluated the land's challenges through the lens of their own midot (their sense of self-preservation, fear, assessment of military odds). This is mochin in the service of midot — intellect captured by the emotional drive rather than intellect illuminating and moderating emotion.
- The Rashab draws on the hemshech's earlier analysis of "seeing" versus "hearing" (developed more fully in Balak 5664): the spies "saw" the land (vayiru et ha'aretz) and their seeing was dominated by their own inner orientation. The seeing of the righteous elevates; the seeing of those dominated by self-interest degrades. The spies' physical eyes were intact, but their inner "eye" — the faculty that integrates what is seen with divine truth — was operating from within the Tohu-mode.
- The mitzvah of tzitzit, given at the end of this parasha ("and you shall see it and remember all the commandments of God"), is the rectification: tzitzit trains the eyes to see in the mode of bitul, to register the world not as a field of threats and opportunities for the self but as a field of divine commandments. The blue thread (techelet) specifically draws the eye toward the sky and the sea, dissolving the self-enclosed perspective into vastness.

### What It Gets To
The maamar establishes a spiritual anatomy of the spies' failure — and by extension of all failures of faith: they occurred not from lack of intellectual data but from a structural disorder in which midot captured mochin rather than the reverse. The Rashab's chiddush is that this Tohu-failure is not merely biographical (the spies were weak) but structural: the human soul contains within it the Tohu-dynamic as a permanent temptation, and tzitzit (along with all mitzvot of "remembering") is the Tikkun-technology that reorients vision toward bitul.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
Parshat Shelach 5664 takes the hemshech's abstract analysis of mochin-midot interplay (developed through Naso and Beha'alotcha) into the domain of historical narrative and practical mitzvah. This is characteristic of Taarav's movement: kabbalistic structure → sefirotic dynamics → practical spiritual life. The spy episode also introduces the theme of Eretz Yisrael as the spiritual correlate of the lowest world (Asiyah), the domain into which divine light must descend to fulfill the purpose of dira betachtonim — and shows that this descent is obstructed when the descending agent (the human soul) is operating in Tohu-mode rather than Tikkun-mode.
