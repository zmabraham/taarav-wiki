## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Bereishit 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: The maamar opens by citing the verse or teaching: "בראשית ברא אלקים את השמים ואת הארץ והארץ היתה תהו כו', וצ"ל מאחר שאומר ברא אלקים את השמים ואת הארץ איך אומר אח"כ והארץ היתה תהו, דתהו היינו שאינו דבר מה וכמו מאפס ותהו נחשבו והיינו שאינו דבר ומציאות, והלא ברא אלקים את השמים כו'" and raising a textual difficulty (קושיא, kushya): why does the text phrase the matter in this particular way? The specific question focuses on understanding how the language used points to a deeper Kabbalistic reality within the hemshech's ongoing analysis.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Bereishit 1909-10. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ בראשית, העת"ר corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Parshat Bereishit 5670, constructs an account of creation that reconciles two apparently contradictory statements: "God created the heavens and the earth" (implying they exist) and "the earth was Tohu" (implying it had no substance). The resolution arrives through a precise analysis of the relationship between Atzilut and Beriyah — specifically, how the lights of Atzilut relate to the created worlds, and what "creation from nothing" really means when the "nothing" (Tohu) follows immediately after the "something" (Bereishit bara).

### The Opening Move
The Rashab opens with a dual textual problem: (1) If God "created the heavens and the earth," why does the very next verse say "the earth was Tohu (formless void)"? Tohu means "without substance," as in Isaiah's "they are counted as nothing and formless void" — sheer non-entity. How can something be created and then be Tohu? (2) The heavens are described as being created in verse 1, but the actual creation of the sky (rakia) is only described later in verse 6 — so what does verse 1 mean? The Talmudic midrash adds a further puzzle: Rabbi Yishmael's school reads "Bereishit bara" as "bara shit" (He created six) — a cryptic reference to the six Primordial Foundations (שיתין, the cosmic substructures of the Temple). What is the connection between creation's opening word and these six?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **The Two Tzimtzumim (Contractions) of Malchut.** Recovering the hemshech's framework from the Shemini Atzeret maamar (the five Gevurot of Malchut), the Rashab now elaborates two specific tzimtzumim within Malchut that are necessary for creation to be possible. The first tzimtzum conceals the interiority and essence of the Or — leaving only the outer dimension (חיצוניות) of the light available to become the substrate of creation. The second tzimtzum takes this outer dimension and further structures it into the specific "letters and combinations" (אותיות וצירופים) that are the power-patterns generating each particular created being.
- **"ארוממך אלקי המלך": the necessity of exalting Malchut.** The Rashab reads the verse "I will exalt You, my God the King" (ארוממך אלקי המלך) — which appears as the maamar's second organizing text — as pointing to a theological obligation: because Malchut reaches the lower worlds only through extreme concealment of its divine interiority, there is a need for the divine service of "exaltation" (רוממות) — lifting Malchut back toward its source in Keter. This is the function of prayer (specifically the Amidah, which "plants" the soul in the divine and "raises" it back to the source).
- **The Rashab's account of Atzilut's relation to Beriyah.** Drawing on the Etz Chaim (particularly the gates of Partzufim and Orot), the Rashab establishes a refined position: the lights of Atzilut do not penetrate into Beriyah, Yetzirah, and Asiyah. What does penetrate is not the Or of Atzilut itself but the vessels (Kelim) of Malchut d'Atzilut, which "break through" (בקעו) the Parsa (screen between Atzilut and Beriyah) and become the Neshama (soul-light) for the worlds below. Even this penetration involves a significant diminishment: the Malchut that was complete in Atzilut becomes partial ("the King said to the moon: diminish yourself") by the time it enters Beriyah.
- **Why the Or of Atzilut must remain above.** The Rashab offers a precise structural argument: the Or of Atzilut terminates at Malchut d'Atzilut. What descends below is not the Or but the Kli (vessel), which serves as a source of light (נר"ן — Nefesh, Ruach, Neshamah) for the worlds. The Or proper of Atzilut functions as fire — it "does not mix" with the lower elements. This is why even the highest angels cannot see the "Glory" directly; they perceive only the outer radiance of the Kli.
- **"Bereishit bara shesh" — the six foundations.** The Talmud's cryptic reading ("He created six") points to the six primordial structures — possibly the six directions of spatial reality, or the six foundational sefirot (Chesed through Yesod) in their creative function. The Rashab reads this as pointing to the framework within which all specific created beings can exist: before the heavens or earth in their familiar forms, what was "created" first was the structural possibility of differentiated existence — a pre-formal framework, the cosmic "grid" into which all subsequent creation would be placed. This is why "the earth was Tohu" immediately after creation: the content is there but not yet articulated into distinct forms.
- **The "Six Foundations" as the שיתין of the Temple.** The six foundations eventually manifest in the Temple's substructure (the šitin — drainage channels beneath the altar). This is not incidental: the Temple is the earthly locus where the pre-formal six foundations are given a fixed physical habitation, completing the arc from primordial cosmic possibility to concrete material expression — the hemshech's central theme of dira betachtonim.

### What It Gets To
The maamar resolves the Bereishit paradox as follows: "God created the heavens and the earth" refers to the creation of the potential/formal framework (the "six foundations") that makes differentiated existence possible — this is a real creation, but it precedes the articulation of specific forms. "The earth was Tohu" then describes the state of this newly created potential before the articulating force of the divine speech (the subsequent "Let there be...") structures it into definite being. Tohu is not absence of creation but the state of created potential prior to articulation.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
Moving from the festival season into the weekly Torah cycle, this maamar inaugurates a new phase of Part 2 that will work through the early Bereishit narratives. The Rashab is not simply giving a drasha on the parasha; he is continuing to develop the hemshech's core architecture — specifically, the relationship between the potential (Atzilut, the pre-formal six) and the actual (Beriyah-Asiyah, the articulated world). The two tzimtzumim of Malchut analyzed here will undergird all subsequent discussions of how divine speech creates and sustains the physical world.
