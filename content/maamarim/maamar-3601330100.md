## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Beshalach 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar — on the Song of the Sea — builds the concept of Shira (song) as the supreme expression of Or Chozer in the human-divine relationship, and demonstrates through the grammatical anomaly of "az yashir" (he will sing, in the future tense where the past is expected) that the Song of the Sea establishes divine sovereignty (kisse'a) permanently — making this moment not merely a historical commemoration but the template for the final redemption and resurrection, and the paradigm for how a fully-realized Or Chozer stabilizes the divine throne.

### The Opening Move

The verse: "אז ישיר משה ובני ישראל את השירה הזאת לה' ויאמרו לאמר" — "Then Moses and the children of Israel will sing (future tense) this song to God and they said, saying..." The Rashab identifies three textual difficulties: (1) Why "az yashir" — the future tense "will sing" — when they actually sang in the past? (2) Why "ישיר" (singular, "he will sing") when the verse includes both Moses and all of Israel? (3) The Talmud (Sanhedrin 91b) derives from "az yashir" a proof for techiyas ha-mesim (resurrection of the dead) — what is the connection between the Song of the Sea and the resurrection? The Midrash Rabbah (Parshat 23) adds that the divine throne was not fully established until Israel sang the Song — "nechon kis'acha maz" (Your throne is established from then), with "az" (then) referring to the moment of the Song.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **"Az yashir" — the future tense points to Or Chozer reaching beyond the present moment**: the use of the future tense (yashir) rather than the past (shar or vayashiru) signals that the Song of the Sea is not a response to what has already occurred but an act that establishes something for all future time. The Rashab reads this as the defining characteristic of Shira: genuine song is not merely emotional expression but a creative act that reconfigures the divine-human relationship. Or Chozer at this level does not merely ascend to return light; it establishes a permanent new configuration.
- **The structure of Or Yashar and Or Chozer as revealed in the Crossing of the Sea**: the source text references the prior analysis of "ao u-tekav'h asah" (and man performs many devices of constriction) and the theme that God made many tzimtzumim (contractions) in order to achieve unity with humanity, while humanity must withdraw from all materiality to ascend through all the worlds. The Crossing of the Sea is the enacted form of this mutual movement: God splits the waters (Or Yashar acting from above) and Israel walks through on dry land (Or Chozer in its complete form — the human ascending through the divided elements).
- **"Az ישיר" — singular because it is Moshe's singular capacity to draw the entire nation into one Or Chozer**: the singular form "yashir" (he will sing) rather than the plural "yashiru" indicates that Moshe's Or Chozer at the Sea was of such a quality that it encompassed the entire nation — his individual ascent carried all of Israel in one unified movement. This is the role of the Nasi (leader/prince): his Or Chozer is not merely personal but national.
- **Techiyas ha-mesim (resurrection) encoded in the Song**: the Talmud reads "yashir" as proof of resurrection because the future tense implies that Moshe will sing again — in the future. The Rashab develops this: Or Chozer of the highest order (the level expressed in the Song of the Sea) does not terminate; it reaches a level that is above the boundary between life and death. When Or Chozer ascends to the level of pnimiyut ha-Keter, it touches the divine will that is "above" the limitation of mortality and therefore carries within it the seed of its own future expression — resurrection.
- **"Nechon kis'acha maz" — the divine throne is established through the Song**: the Midrash teaches that before Israel sang, God's throne was somehow "unstable" (like a king who is named "commander" before the victory, and "king" only after). The Rashab develops the hemshech's central concept here: the kisse (throne) corresponds to the level of Malchut in its fullest expression — divine sovereignty made actual in the lower worlds (the ultimate dira b'tachtonim). Before the Song, Malchut was present but not "nechon" (established/stable). The Song — the collective Or Chozer of the entire people — is what "establishes" the divine throne by completing the circuit: the Or Yashar that split the sea draws from Israel the Or Chozer of the Song, and that Or Chozer, upon reaching the level of Keter, returns to Malchut and gives it the quality of "nechon" — stable, permanent sovereignty.
- **"Ve'al demuht ha-kisse demuht ke-mar'eh adam" — the image of Man on the throne**: the source text pivots to an analysis of Ezekiel's vision of "the likeness of a human form on the throne," which the Rashab links to the Song's establishment of the throne. The "image of Adam on the throne" corresponds to Atzilut's structure of the divine "face" (partzuf) — the organized, human-like expression of divine light that becomes the basis for revelation in the lower worlds. The Song of the Sea is what makes this "image of Adam" fully actualized within the lower worlds.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the Song of the Sea is the paradigm event for how Or Chozer achieves its maximal expression: it is not merely a response to a miracle but an act that permanently alters the structure of the divine-human relationship by establishing the divine throne (kisse/Malchut) in its full stability. The future tense "yashir" signals that this song carries within itself all future songs — including the ultimate song of the Messianic redemption and the song of the resurrected. Or Chozer at this level does not merely rise and return; it establishes a new permanent baseline.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Beshalach maamar is one of the most theoretically dense in this period of the Taarav, because it brings together the hemshech's core themes at their highest pitch: Or Chozer achieving the level that establishes divine sovereignty (the dira b'tachtonim in its historical-national form), the connection between song/shira and Or Chozer's capacity to "reach beyond" temporal boundaries, and the link between the Song of the Sea and techiyas ha-mesim (resurrection as the ultimate Or Chozer reaching above mortality). The image of Adam on the throne — Atzilut's organized structure as revealed through the people's song — will anchor the subsequent analysis in Parshat Yitro, where the Sinai revelation is examined as the even-more-direct disclosure of that same divine structure.
