## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Beha'alotcha 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar uses the central image of Parshat Beha'alotcha — the lighting of the Menorah — to erect a detailed analysis of the relationship between Shem מ"ה (the Name of 45, principle of Tikkun) and Shem ב"ן (the Name of 52, principle of Tohu), focusing on how the "candles" of the sefirot are brought to illuminate "toward the face of the Menorah" — that is, how each individual sefirotic light is directed toward and unified with the central shaft, achieving the harmony of Tikkun through upward orientation.

### The Opening Move
The command "בהעלותך את הנרות" — "when you raise up the lamps/candles" (Numbers 8:2) — is the occasion. The Menorah's seven branches are lit so that the six outer candles incline toward the central shaft (the שמש, "sun-lamp"). Rashi cites the Talmud (Shabbat 22b) that the inner three candles lean toward the middle one. What is the inner significance of this directionality? Why must the lights face inward rather than illuminate outward? And what does it mean that Aaron's merit was that he did not deviate from this specification (Rashi: "to tell the praise of Aaron that he did not deviate")?

### The Argumentative Arc
- The Menorah's seven branches correspond to the seven midot (Chesed through Malkhut). Each midah in Tikkun must be "lit" from a source above itself and directed back toward that source — this is the Or Chozer dynamic within the sefirotic structure itself, not only between worlds.
- Shem מ"ה operates as the "central shaft" in the Menorah analogy: it is the integrating principle, the channel through which the supernal Or Ein Sof flows, and toward which each individual sefirah must orient. When the outer candles face inward (toward the shammash), they express their bitul toward the common source. This is the inner meaning of "raising" the lights — הַעֲלָאָה (elevation) here means directing their light upward and inward, not merely ensuring they burn.
- The hemshech's accumulated analysis of מ"ה and ב"ן now allows the Rashab to sharpen: ב"ן is the mode of each sefirah being "for itself" (each candle burning in its own direction); מ"ה is the mode of each sefirah being "toward the whole" (each candle turning toward the shammash). Aaron's service is the paradigm of Tikkun-consciousness precisely because he maintains this orientation without deviation.
- The practical dimension: the Levites' service (purified and "raised" in this parasha) is the functional analog at the human level — the spiritual functionary who serves the divine without private agenda, directing all energy toward the "central shaft" of divine unity.

### What It Gets To
The maamar arrives at a reading of Aaron's priesthood as the human instantiation of Shem מ"ה operating in the world: the priest who does not deviate from directing the lights toward the center is the person who has achieved the Tikkun-orientation in their own soul — all their faculties unified around divine service rather than self-expression. This is the meaning of "to tell the praise of Aaron" — not a biographical compliment but a structural claim about the nature of the elevated spiritual office.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
Parshat Beha'alotcha 5664 provides the visual-liturgical image for the abstract distinction between מ"ה and ב"ן that the hemshech has been developing: the Menorah is a spatial diagram of Tikkun. The fact that six outer candles orient toward the center physically embodies the principle that Tikkun = bitul of the individual sefirah toward the common sefirotic unity. This image will serve as a touchstone for the subsequent maamarim and links the hemshech's abstract metaphysics to the lived reality of Temple service and priestly consciousness.
