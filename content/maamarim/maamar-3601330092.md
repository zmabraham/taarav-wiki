## Context

This maamar was delivered during **19 Kislev (Yud-Tet Kislev) 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar — delivered on Yud-Tet Kislev, the Rosh Hashana of Chassidus — builds the fundamental structural distinction between tefillah (prayer) and bracha (blessing) as two inverse modes of divine-human engagement: tefillah ascends from below to above (mayin nukvin, Or Chozer), while bracha descends from above to below (mayin duchrin, Or Yashar), with the bracha of kohanim representing the apex of top-down divine influx — a drawing from pnimiyut ha-Keter itself.

### The Opening Move

The verse is Tehillim 55:19: "פדה בשלום נפשי מקרב לי כי ברבים היו עמדי" — "He redeemed my soul in peace from the battle against me, for the many were with me." The Rashab identifies a double difficulty: (1) What is the meaning of "redemption in peace" (pidyah b'shalom) — what kind of redemption is qualified as peaceful? (2) More puzzling, the verse says "because the many were with me" (ki b'rabbim hayu imadi) as the reason for the peaceful redemption. How does having many people with you cause a redemption in peace? The Gemara (Berachot) teaches that one who engages in Torah, gemilut chasadim, and prayer with the community is considered as if he redeemed God — but what is the inner logic connecting these three things?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The three activities in the Gemara correspond to three modes of divine-human connection**: Torah study is the channel of direct intellectual engagement with the divine will (corresponding to Or Yashar flowing through da'at); gemilut chasadim (lovingkindness) creates the relational bond between person and person that mirrors the relational bond between worlds (corresponding to the sefira of Chesed in its active form); prayer with the community is the quintessential act of Or Chozer — the collective elevation of mayin nukvin.
- **The critical distinction between tefillah and bracha**: tefillah is from below upward — anyone can pray, because anyone can elevate their own mayin nukvin. The one who prays is below the thing being requested, and the prayer acts by purifying and nullifying the self (zikuch v'bitul guf v'nefesh). Bracha, by contrast, requires the one blessing to be above what they are bestowing. Avraham received "v'heyeh bracha" — the capacity to bless was given to him — meaning one must stand at a higher level than the recipient to draw down a bracha.
- **Two grades of bracha**: the source text carefully distinguishes two types. The first type draws from the shresh v'mekor of the seder hishtalshelut — like Yaakov's blessing to Ephraim and Menashe, which could only reveal what was already latent in their root. The second type draws an or chadash (new light) that can alter the seder hishtalshelut entirely — like birkat kohanim, which draws from the Name Havaya itself, and can change outcomes even against the natural order.
- **Birkat kohanim as the model of pnimiyut ha-Keter**: the Rashab cites the phrase "כה תברכו" and notes that the letters of "תברכו" can be read as "בכתרו" — in His crown. This is a key kabbalistic reading: the kohanim's blessing draws specifically from pnimiyut ha-Keter — the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy, which are identified in the hemshech as the innermost dimension of Keter. "כה" (koh) is malchut, so the blessing draws from Keter's inner core into Malchut.
- **"Pidyah b'shalom" — the Alter Rebbe's liberation as a model of the cosmic template**: the Rashab connects the verse to the liberation (pidyon) of the Alter Rebbe from imprisonment, which occurred on Yud-Tet Kislev. The "peace" of the redemption refers to the complete resolution — shalom meaning completeness (shalem) — achieved when Or Chozer (the many who prayed, ki b'rabbim) ascends and meets the Or Yashar of bracha descending from above. The confluence of communal tefillah and divine bracha creates the "shalom" — the peaceful, complete integration — of the redemption.
- **"Yoduchah Hashem kol ma'asecha v'chasidecha y'varchu'cha"**: the source text's exegesis of this verse illuminates the difference between hoda'ah (acknowledgment/thanks) and bracha. Hoda'ah is available to all creatures (kol ma'asecha), while bracha belongs specifically to chasidecha — those who "do chesed with their Master" (hamitchaseid im kono), meaning those who stand in a sufficiently elevated position to draw down new light from above the seder hishtalshelut.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the "redemption in peace" is achieved specifically through the combination of communal prayer (Or Chozer from below) and the bracha of those who can draw from pnimiyut ha-Keter (Or Yashar from above). The "many" (rabbim) are not merely a quantitative factor but represent the collective Or Chozer of the community — and it is precisely this collective ascent that draws down the singular bracha from the highest source. This is why the Alter Rebbe's liberation on Yud-Tet Kislev was "in peace" — because both directions of the divine-human exchange were fully engaged.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The 19 Kislev maamar performs double duty in the hemshech: it advances the theoretical analysis of tefillah vs. bracha as complementary modes of Or Chozer and Or Yashar, while simultaneously embedding that analysis in the living history of Chabad — the liberation of the Alter Rebbe as the event that authorized the wide dissemination of Chassidus, which is itself the primary vehicle for elevating Or Chozer in the present era. The hemshech's systematic Kabbalistic framework is thus shown to have direct historical and existential stakes. The analysis of birkat kohanim as drawing from pnimiyut ha-Keter will become foundational for subsequent maamarim on the structure of Keter's inner dimensions.
