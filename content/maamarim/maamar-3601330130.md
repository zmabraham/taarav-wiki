## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Re'eh 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar uses the opening of Parshat Re'eh — "See, I place before you today a blessing and a curse" — to construct a distinction between two modes of divine service: one rooted in intellectual discernment (bein tov l'ra, distinguishing good from evil), and a higher mode that transcends the framework of judgment altogether. The maamar thereby advances the hemshech's ongoing project of mapping the relationship between Or Yashar (descending grace) and Or Chozer (ascending effort) onto the soul's capacity for avoda.

### The Opening Move

The verse "Re'eh Anochi noten lifneichem hayom bracha u'klala" (Deut. 11:26) — "See, I place before you today a blessing and a curse" — presents a grammatical puzzle: the verb "Re'eh" (see!) is singular, yet the continuation "lifneichem" (before you) is plural. This asymmetry, familiar to the Rashab's method, opens a question about the relationship between the individual soul's perception and the collective experience of divine address — and by extension, how individual avoda connects to a cosmic, suprarational level of divine service.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The singular-to-plural shift** signals that the starting point of divine service (Re'eh — singular perception) must be a faculty within the individual that then expands to collective consequence. This maps onto the hemshech's ongoing analysis of how a soul-level faculty (yechida) interfaces with the collective structures of the sefirot.
- **"Hayom" — today** is glossed in classic sources as pointing to Rosh Hashana (as the Rashab will revisit in maamar-3601330134 on Nitzavim), indicating that the "seeing" described is not ordinary visual perception but a moment of illuminated clarity — the Or Yashar breaking through at a time of divine openness.
- **Bracha u'klala** (blessing and curse) are not merely moral categories but represent the two poles of Or Yashar and Or Chozer: the blessing is the direct divine influx from above (yirah from above, Or Yashar), while the curse is the consequence of the soul's failure to generate Or Chozer — the ascending light that animates the created order.
- **The Sh"ach commentary** (referenced in maamar-3601330131 immediately following) is anticipated here: the "gates" (she'arim) through which blessing or curse enters correspond to the portals of the body-soul interface, and the maamar establishes that proper divine service requires governance (mishpat) at each gate. This sets up the Shoftim maamar's shiputim/she'arim framework.
- **Mitzvot as vessels (keilim)**: The maamar resolves that the singular "Re'eh" points to the yechida — the highest soul-root — while the plural "lifneichem" points to the practical keilim (vessels) through which the soul interfaces with the world. The blessing flows when Or Yashar descends through yechida into the vessels; the curse is the state of keilim without inner light.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes that the commandment to "see" is a command directed at the yechida-level of the soul: the capacity for direct, undifferentiated divine perception that precedes rational analysis. Bracha and klala are not contingent outcomes but structural possibilities that exist simultaneously in the soul's relationship with Or Ein Sof — and the task of the nitzavim (those who stand firm) is to transmute that dual possibility into a singular channel of Or Chozer ascending from the lower to the upper.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Parshat Re'eh opens the autumn sequence of maamarim (Re'eh, Shoftim, Ki Tetze, Ki Tavo, Nitzavim) that constitute a sustained arc within Hemshech Taarav's second volume. This maamar lays the conceptual foundation for that arc by establishing the soul's capacity for direct (yashar) divine perception as the basis for what follows: Shoftim will develop the jurisprudence of the body's "gates," Ki Tetze will examine the soul's "going out to war" (the conflict between Or Yashar and the klipot), Ki Tavo will address the entry into achieved wholeness, and Nitzavim will culminate in the paradox of standing unified before God on Rosh Hashana despite the diversity of soul-levels. Re'eh is thus the epistemological grounding: before one can build the system, one must establish what kind of "seeing" makes the system accessible.
