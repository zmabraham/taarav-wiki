## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Bamidbar, Erev Shavuot 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Bamidbar, Erev Shavuot 1903-04. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ במדבר, עח"ש, תרד"ע corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar erects the foundational architecture of Torah's own ontological status — its dual nature as both object that was nurtured by God (אמון = passive: the Torah was raised and formed beside Him) and as instrument through which God acts (אמון = active: craftsman-tool of creation). This dual identity maps precisely onto the distinction between Tohu and Tikkun, and into the five descending levels of Torah's presence through Keter, Chokhmah, Binah, Ze'ir Anpin, Malkhut, and the worlds of Beriyah-Yetzirah-Asiyah.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens on Proverbs 8:30 — "I was beside Him as an אמון, and I was שעשועים (delight) day by day, playing before Him" — and immediately surfaces a philological tension: Rashi reads אמון as passive ("I was raised/nurtured"), while the Midrash Rabbah (Bereshit 1:1) reads it as active ("I was His craftsman's tool"). The verse also uses ואהי' (a form suggesting continuous present-future, not simple past), and the Zohar on Acharei (62b) explicitly links the double אהי' of this verse to the divine Name אהי' אשר אהי'. How can the Torah be simultaneously passive (formed beside God) and active (forming the world through God)?

### The Argumentative Arc
- The Rashab identifies five distinct clauses in the verse, each corresponding to one of the five levels through which Torah descended: ואהי' אצלו אמון = Torah as it exists in Keter (the realm of essential divine delight, שעשועים עצמיים); ואהי' שעשועים יום יום = Torah in Chokhmah-Binah (the supernal "day," intellectual delight); משחקת לפניו בכל עת and משחקת בתבל ארצו = Torah in Ze'ir Anpin and Malkhut respectively; ושעשועי את בנ"א = Torah as it descends into the worlds of Beriyah, Yetzirah, and Asiyah.
- The key distinction emerges between the first level and all subsequent ones: at the Keter level, Torah participates in שעשועים עצמיים — the divine self-delight that is entirely internal and pre-revelatory, where Torah is not yet a tool or a teaching but the very mode of God's self-pleasure. All lower levels are delight-in-relation (divine delight as Torah plays before Him, or before creation), but Keter-Torah is delight-in-itself.
- The double אהי' (connecting to אהי' אשר אהי') signals this vertical span: the first אהי' points to Torah's unmanifest, essential dimension in the Ein Sof that precedes even Keter; the second to its manifestation as חכמה and the beginning of the sefirotic chain.
- The shem מ"ה (Name of 45) — developed extensively in surrounding chapters of the hemshech — is identified as the "path of Atzilut" (אורח האצי'), the channel connecting the Ein Sof beyond the tzimtzum to the sefirot of Atzilut. Shem מ"ה functions as the integrating principle that produces התכללות (mutual interpenetration) among the sefirot, because unlike Shem ב"ן (Name of 52, associated with Tohu), which is the principle of isolated selfhood (יש), Shem מ"ה is the principle of bitul (self-nullification), allowing opposites to coexist.
- The contrast between Tohu and Tikkun is introduced through this lens: the sefirot of Tohu were each in a mode of אנא אמלוך ("I alone shall reign") — each sefirah as an absolute, unmodified, self-referential מציאות (existential presence). This is why they could not contain one another and "died" (the Tohu-kings). Tikkun achieves the integration of opposites through Shem מ"ה, which introduces the principle of nullification into the sefirot without dissolving their individuality.

### What It Gets To
The chiddush is that Torah's verse about its own pre-creational status precisely maps the ontological hierarchy: Torah at the Keter level is not yet "Torah" in any revelatory or pedagogical sense — it is the form of divine self-pleasure before any subject-object distinction. Only from Chokhmah downward does Torah become something that "plays before Him," and only at the level of ושעשועי את בנ"א does it become the Torah of human engagement. The hemshech's deeper point: even the lowliest act of Torah study by a human soul touches — via Or Chozer — the highest שעשועים עצמיים, because that is what Torah, in its essence, always already is.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar, delivered on Erev Shavuot 5664, functions as the theological justification for Shavuot itself within the Taarav system. The hemshech has been building toward establishing how divine light operates across worlds; here, the Rashab establishes Torah as the vertical axis along which that light travels in both directions. Torah is not merely a code of law or even divine wisdom — it is the structural interface between Ein Sof and hishtalshelut, the medium through which שעשועים עצמיים becomes accessible to human souls. This sets up the subsequent Shavuot maamarim (61, 62, 63) to explore Matan Torah as the moment when that interface became experiential rather than merely structural.
