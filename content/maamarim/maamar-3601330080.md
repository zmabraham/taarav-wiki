## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Haazinu 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on Shabbat Parshat Haazinu 5670 (the Shabbat between Yom Kippur and Sukkot), uses Moshe's great song "Ha'azinu" — spoken by Moshe in the final days of his life — to develop the concept of how a perfectly nullified soul (בטל במציאות) functions as the supreme vehicle for divine speech, and how this maps onto the Kav (the line of Or Yashar) re-entering the vacated space after the Tzimtzum.

### The Opening Move
Haazinu is Moshe's final testament to Israel — not a legal code or narrative but a cosmic poem that sweeps from creation through Israel's history to the End of Days. The Rashab's opening question is characteristically structural: why does Moshe address the heavens and earth as witnesses ("האזינו השמים ואדברה ותשמע הארץ אמרי פי")? Witnesses in Torah law must be external to the matter being attested; can heaven and earth be "external" to a divine teaching? And why does Moshe speak to the heavens ("speak") with one verb and to the earth ("hear") with another — what is the difference?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **Heaven and earth as Or Yashar and Or Chozer.** The Rashab reads the pairing of heavens (שמים) and earth (ארץ) through the hemshech's master lens: heavens correspond to Or Yashar (the descending divine light) and earth to Or Chozer (the ascending reflected light from below). Moshe's song mediates between these two movements — it is simultaneously divine teaching descending (the heavens) and human response ascending (the earth).
- **The role of Moshe as the ultimate mediating vessel.** Moshe is described in Kabbalistic sources as "שמש" (sun) — the full vehicle of divine light. The Rashab develops: Moshe's self-nullification (ביטול) was so complete that there was no "Moshe-personality" interposing itself between the divine speech and Israel. The Torah speaks through him as through a perfectly transparent vessel. This is the inner meaning of "ואדברה" — "I will speak" — where the subject is ambiguous: is it Moshe or God? The answer is: both, because in Moshe's state of total ביטול, the distinction collapses.
- **Kav and Reshimu: the post-Tzimtzum structure.** The Rashab links this to the hemshech's analysis of the Kav. After the Tzimtzum, the vacated space (חלל) retains only the Reshimu — the faint impression of the infinite. The Kav then descends into this space, a directed beam that is less than the full pre-Tzimtzum light but is still the activating force of all subsequent reality. Moshe's function in the world is the human analog of the Kav: he is a concentrated beam of divine revelation penetrating the "vacated space" of the physical world, not flooding it with undifferentiated light but directed and structured — Torah.
- **The Shabbat between Yom Kippur and Sukkot.** This Shabbat occupies a unique temporal position: Yom Kippur has just drawn from Keter (as developed in the previous maamarim), and Sukkot is about to draw that revelation into physical structures (the Sukkah and the Four Species). The Haazinu Shabbat is the moment of transition — holding the Keter-revelation while preparing to channel it into the material world. Moshe's song, which similarly holds both the highest divine truth and the historical human reality, serves as the liturgical text for exactly this transition.
- **"Yitrof ka'matar likchi" — the descent of teaching as rain.** Moshe's image of Torah as rain descending on the earth further develops the Or Yashar / Or Chozer dynamic: rain descends (Or Yashar) and causes the earth to bring forth produce (Or Chozer — the earth's response rising upward). The teaching does not remain in the heavens; it penetrates the earth and is transformed by it into new life. This is the model for all transmission of divine wisdom into the world.

### What It Gets To
The maamar concludes that Moshe's total nullification of self enabled the Or Yashar to pass through him unimpeded into the world — making him the paradigmatic Kav-vessel. More broadly, the maamar establishes that genuine Torah transmission is always this structure: divine light descending through a nullified vessel into the physical world, where it generates Or Chozer upward. This is the mechanism by which the Keter-revelation of Yom Kippur will be "housed" in the Sukkah during the coming festival.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar bridges the Yom Kippur sub-section (inward, ascent to Keter) and the Sukkot sub-section (outward, descent into material vessels). The hemshech is showing that the spiritual calendar does not just cycle through disconnected modes but follows the logic of Or Yashar and Or Chozer: after the ascent of Rosh Hashanah / Yom Kippur, Sukkot is the descent of what was received into the physical world — and Haazinu's Shabbat, with its image of Torah as rain, provides the conceptual hinge.
