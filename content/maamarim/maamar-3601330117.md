## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 1st day of Shavuot 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar, delivered on the night of the first day of Shavuot 1909-10, constructs a layered account of why the revelation at Sinai employed specifically the Name Elokim (associated with concealment and tzimtzum) rather than the Name Havayah (associated with the infinite, boundless light of Ein Sof). The resolution discloses a new understanding of the relationship between the two divine Names as the two structural poles of the entire cosmos — and of human consciousness.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with a sharp textual paradox rooted in the Shema and the Sinai revelation: "וידבר אלקים את כל הדברים האלה לאמר אנכי ה' אלקיך" — God spoke at Sinai using the Name Elokim, yet the content of the speech begins with "I am Havayah your God." Why does the introductory frame use Elokim (the Name of tzimtzum and concealment) to introduce a revelation that was, by all accounts, the most total disclosure of the infinite divine light (Or Ein Sof) ever experienced? Additionally: in the first paragraph of Shema, the Name Havayah appears twice with Elokim sandwiched between them — what does this threefold structure mean? The puzzle is deepened by the halachic phrase before Kaddish, where two Havayah-names again bracket a pause. Why this particular architecture of divine Names?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Two levels of Havayah**: The Rashab establishes that the two instances of Havayah in the Shema correspond to two fundamentally different aspects of the same Name: the upper Havayah (Havayah ila'ah), which is the Ein Sof as it precedes and transcends the chain of worlds entirely, and the lower Havayah (Havayah tata'ah), which is the manifestation of the divine through the kav (the line of light) that permeates the world-system. These are not two separate divine beings but two modes of the same infinite reality.
- **Elokim as the tzimtzum-between**: Between these two Havayahs stands the Name Elokim, which the Rashab identifies with the tzimtzum itself — the withdrawal and concealment that makes the differentiation between upper and lower Havayah possible. Elokim is not merely "the divine in a limiting mode" but is the dynamic act of contraction (צמצום) that produced the space of the world-system. Its placement between the two Havayahs in the Shema reflects the ontological structure: infinite light above, tzimtzum-act in the middle, infinite light mediated through the kav below.
- **The double function of the tzimtzum**: The Rashab carefully distinguishes two effects of the tzimtzum. First, it conceals the unbounded Ein Sof light (the pnimiyut ha-or — the inner, ungraspable dimension) so that finite worlds can exist. Second, it separates and individuates the "outer" or world-directed dimension of the light (chitzoniyut ha-or), which then flows through the kav into the sefirot and worlds. Without this second function, there would be no structured divine light for the worlds to receive.
- **The Sinai paradox resolved**: At Matan Torah, there was a revelation of the innermost divine light — "וירד ה' על הר סיני," the descent of the Havayah-aspect of infinite divinity. Yet this revelation was framed with Elokim because the very act of speaking Torah to Israel required the tzimtzum-structure: the infinite had to pass through the "limiting membrane" of the Elokim-mode in order to be receivable by finite human souls. The divine speech at Sinai was therefore simultaneously the most total revelation and a structured, tzimtzum-mediated transmission.
- **The inversion: revealing concealment**: The Rashab cites the Zoharic teaching "מאן מנכון דחשוכא מהפך לנהורא" — who among you can transform darkness into light? This is the human task of avodah: through inner purification (zikuch), the opacity of the vessels (kelim) becomes transparent to the light within them. This is the Or Chozer dynamic: the human being, by refining the "darkness" (the tzimtzum-nature of the body and animal soul), actually inverts the tzimtzum from concealment into revelation — causing the Elokim-structure to become a vehicle for Havayah-light rather than a barrier to it.
- **"Know today and take to heart" (Devarim 4:39)**: The maamar transitions to the verse "וידעת היום והשבות אל לבבך כי ה' הוא האלקים בשמים ממעל ועל הארץ מתחת אין עוד," unpacking it as a Kabbalistic statement about the identity of Havayah and Elokim. The knowledge that "Havayah is Elokim" — that the infinite and the contracted are one — is not a theological proposition but a lived state of consciousness: the soul that has undergone the work of transforming darkness to light directly perceives this unity.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the revelation at Sinai using Elokim-framing was not a limitation of the revelation but its very mechanism: only through the tzimtzum-structure of Elokim could the infinite Havayah-light be "spoken" in a form receivable by Israel. The Shema's double Havayah bracketing Elokim is therefore a daily re-enactment of Sinai — the worshiper recapitulates the cosmic structure by declaring the unity of the Name. And the human task of avodah (divine service) is precisely to be the agent who inverts darkness to light, completing the circuit from Or Yashar (the divine light descending through the Elokim-tzimtzum) to Or Chozer (the human soul's ascent back toward the Havayah-infinite).

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar is a major structural contribution to the hemshech. The identification of Elokim with the tzimtzum as a dynamic bridge between two levels of Havayah is not a side point but a precise elaboration of the hemshech's core framework. Earlier sections established the mechanics of tzimtzum and kav; this maamar shows how those mechanics are encoded in the linguistic structure of the Shema and the Names of God — making the Kabbalistic system not a speculative overlay but the inner grammar of Jewish prayer and of the revelation at Sinai itself.
