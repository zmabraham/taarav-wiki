## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Shavuot 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Hebrew Title

> בס"ד. חה"ש, תער"ב, לפ"ק

## Summary

**Opening Question**: When Israel preceded 'we will do' before 'we will hear' at Sinai, the angels bound two crowns on each person — one for 'we will do' and one for 'we will hear.' Why two separate crowns? If the crowns are for the combined declaration, why aren't they one crown? And why does it say the angels bound the crowns because Israel preceded 'we will do,' yet then specifies one crown for each phrase separately?

**Central Concept**: The nature of Keter as the faculty that encompasses both will and intellect, and how the two crowns represent the dual dimension of Keter: (1) Ta'anug (divine pleasure/delight) corresponding to Neshama, and (2) Ratzon (will) corresponding to the motivating force of action. The relationship between the three crowns of Torah, Kehunah, and Malchut in Pirkei Avot versus the two crowns at Sinai.

**Resolution**: The two crowns given at Sinai correspond to the two inner dimensions of Keter itself — Ta'anug and Ratzon — which are the roots of Nefesh HaBehamit and Nefesh HaElohit respectively. The crown for 'we will do' (Naaseh) corresponds to the crown of action/will, and 'we will hear' (Nishma) to the crown of understanding/delight. Together they represent the complete unification of divine will with divine wisdom.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar erects the foundational architecture of Keter as a *dual-dimensional* intermediary: it is simultaneously Ta'anug (divine delight/pleasure) and Ratzon (will), and this duality maps onto the two crowns given at Sinai — not as a redundancy but as a structural necessity. The maamar establishes that the highest level of any system contains two inward faces before it turns outward.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens from the Talmudic account of Israel's declaration *Naaseh V'Nishma* at Sinai — "We will do and we will hear" — and the angels descending to bind two crowns on each person, one for *Naaseh* and one for *Nishma*. The paradox is sharp: if the crowns are the reward for *preceding* "do" before "hear," shouldn't that be one crown for the combined act? And why does the Mishnah in Avot count three crowns (Torah, Kehunah, Malkhut), while here only two appear? The opening move is exegetically precise: the text says the crowns were given *because* they preceded Naaseh to Nishma, yet then enumerates them separately — the two formulations are in tension, and that tension is the engine of the entire analysis.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Three meanings of Keter are surveyed** (from the Pardes Rimonim's Sha'ar HaKeter): Keter as *waiting/anticipation* (המתנה — the etymological sense of "tarry"), as *crown/circlet* (עטרה — the literal sense), and as *encircling/surrounding* (סיבוב). Each meaning maps onto a different aspect of Keter's role: the waiting sense points to Keter's pre-emergent, pre-disclosed state; the crown sense points to how it rests above all the Sefirot without being localized in any; the surrounding sense points to its all-encompassing nature as Or Mekif.

- **Ratzon (will) is differentiated from all lower soul-powers** as an Or Mekif (surrounding light) rather than an Or Pnimi (inner/penetrating light). Every other faculty — intellect, emotion — enters a specific organ and is delimited by it. Will alone encompasses all limbs equally, governing without being contained. The proof-text is Psalms 5:13 — "As a shield, will crowns him" (כצנה רצון תעטרנו) — the shield surrounds from without, not within.

- **Keter as intermediary (ממוצע, mamtze'et)** between Ein Sof and the Sefirot is then argued: before the arousal of will — even the most interior self-directed will — there is pure hiddenness. The arousal of will is already a *turning toward*, a hint of relationship, making Keter the hinge between the utterly transcendent and the structured divine order. The source text's distinction between Ratzon that is aroused (התעוררות הרצון) and the pre-will state of pure concealment (העלם והבדלה לגמרי) is crucial here.

- **The two inner dimensions of Keter are then distinguished**: (1) Ta'anug, the divine delight that is purely self-referential, not yet directed outward — analogous to a king's pleasure in his own majesty (מתענג בתענוג פשוט ועצמי) — and (2) Ratzon proper, the will that already bears relational directedness. Ta'anug corresponds to Neshama; Ratzon to the root of action and Nefesh HaBehamit as the ground of the physical world.

- **The three crowns of Avot are mapped onto the three Sefirot-columns**: Keter Kehunah on the right column (Chesed), Keter Malkhut on the left column (Gevurah, since Malkhut is built from Gevurot), and Keter Torah on the central column — standard tripartite axial structure of Atzilut. The two Sinai crowns, by contrast, correspond to the two inner faces of Keter itself (Ta'anug and Ratzon) that precede and exceed the three-column structure. This is why the Sinai crowns are a separate category from the Avot crowns.

- **Naaseh and Nishma are correlated**: "We will do" (Naaseh) corresponds to Ratzon/will — action is the expression of will directed outward; "We will hear" (Nishma) corresponds to Ta'anug/delight — hearing and absorption are the expression of interior receptivity and pleasure in the divine. The precedence of Naaseh before Nishma is not a pragmatic posture but a metaphysical one: it reflects the soul's alignment with the Ratzon-face of Keter, which governs before the Ta'anug-face comes to rest.

### What It Gets To

By the end, the maamar has established that Keter is not simple but contains an inner dialectic between two pre-Sefirot dimensions — Ta'anug and Ratzon — and that this dialectic is what the two Sinai crowns encoded. The three crowns of Avot operate at the level of the Sefirot's three columns; the two Sinai crowns operate at a deeper level, inside Keter itself before it projects outward. This distinction between inner-Keter structure and outer-Sefirot structure becomes a load-bearing beam of the entire hemshech.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This opening Shavuot maamar of Taarav establishes the hemshech's foundational thesis about Keter as intermediary — a theme that will recur in increasingly complex registers throughout the 20-year discourse. It is the first maamar to map Ta'anug and Ratzon as the two faces of Keter, a mapping that the hemshech will deploy repeatedly when analyzing how divine light passes from Ein Sof into structured reality.
