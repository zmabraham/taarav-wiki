## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Shoftim 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds a detailed account of the soul's "gates" (she'arim) as the loci where divine light must be governed by judgment (mishpat), extending the hemshech's account of Or Yashar and Or Chozer into the psychology and physiology of embodied divine service. It establishes that the soul's seven sense-portals are not merely physical apertures but structured interfaces between the human person and supernal reality, each requiring its own form of discernment (shofet) to function as a channel rather than a breach.

### The Opening Move

The opening cites "Shoftim v'shotrim titen lecha b'chol she'arecha" (Deut. 16:18) — "You shall appoint judges and officers at all your gates" — and immediately cross-references the Sh"ach commentary (Shiftei Kohen al HaTorah), which identifies the "she'arim" (gates) with the body's seven sensory portals: two eyes, two ears, two nostrils, and the mouth. The Sefer Yetzirah (and the Raavad's commentary) confirm this reading: "v'she'arim b'nefesh — and gates in the soul." The immediate question (kushya) is threefold: Who is the judge that judges the gates? What is the nature of mishpat (judgment) at a bodily portal? And what is the connection — cited from Sanhedrin — between the injunction to appoint judges and the prohibition against planting an asherah (idol-tree) adjacent to the altar?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The seven gates as sefirot-correlates**: The maamar establishes that the seven bodily portals correspond to the seven lower sefirot (Chesed through Malchut). Each gate is a site where Or Yashar descends from the upper realm into embodied perception: the eyes receive chokhmah-illumination, the ears receive binah-understanding, and so on. Without a "judge" — an internalized faculty of discernment — the gate transmits indiscriminately, allowing the klipa (husk/obstruction) to enter alongside divine light.
- **The Sanhedrin passage's deeper logic**: The Gemara says that appointing an unworthy judge is like planting an asherah near the altar. The maamar interprets this via the hemshech's framework: an unworthy judge (shofet she'eino hagun) is one whose own inner discriminating faculty has been compromised — meaning the Or Chozer that should arise from embodied human discernment is corrupted at its source, producing an inverted light (or she'eina hagun) that resembles the asherah — an external, displaced object of worship erected precisely where divine presence should arise organically.
- **The Midrash Rabbah's "lishpot et ha'aretz"**: The maamar cites Midrash Rabbah (Parshat Bo 15): "Yaaloz sadai — this is the world; v'chol asher bo — these are the creatures; before whom? Before God; for what? To judge the earth." The judgment (mishpat) of Rosh Hashana is identified as the cosmic correlate of the local mishpat at the body's gates — both are sites where Or Yashar meets Or Chozer, and both require a shofet who is hagun (aligned with supernal light).
- **Two dimensions of the "judge"**: The Rashab distinguishes between the shofet as an external appointment and the shofet as an internalized principle of discrimination. At the level of the body's gates, the internal judge is the neshamah's capacity for hitbonenut (sustained contemplation) that screens what enters through perception. At the cosmic level, the judge is Malchut — the Sefirah that governs the interface between Ein Sof and the created order, exercising mishpat (structured limitation) so that Or Yashar enters creation at a regulated pace rather than overwhelming it.
- **The oneg (pleasure) framework from the source text**: The source text (appearing in the continuation collected under 3601331343) reveals that the maamar reaches a resolution grounded in the concept of oneg (divine pleasure): the ultimate "judge" at each gate is the faculty of pleasure (oneg) — specifically, the highest pleasure associated with the completion of divine intention in creation (dira betachtonim). When the gate's perception aligns with this oneg — when what is seen, heard, or spoken resonates with the supernal pleasure in creation's completion — the Or Chozer generated is genuine and rises to the level of Keter. When the gate's perception is governed by lower, self-referential pleasure, the Or Chozer is corrupted (the asherah-analogy).
- **Three levels of biru'im (refinements)**: The continuation text maps three levels of rectification work (biru'rim): (1) biru'r mi'toch ha'psoles — extracting the holy spark from dross, which operates through the body's gates in ordinary avoda; (2) biru'r ha'ochel mi'toch ha'ochel — the more subtle refinement of already-elevated material (corresponding to the higher gate-faculties of the inner senses); and (3) a level that transcends biru'r entirely, corresponding to the soul's yechida-level, which does not refine but simply radiates divine unity through the gates without the mediation of discernment.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the appointment of "shoftim v'shotrim" at every gate is not merely a social-legal injunction but an instruction in the architecture of the soul's avodah: the neshamah must install its highest discriminating faculty at every bodily portal so that each act of perception becomes an act of Or Chozer — light ascending from below. The Sanhedrin's asherah-warning teaches that a corrupted discriminating faculty does not merely fail to generate Or Chozer — it generates an anti-light, a downward pull that mirrors the structure of Or Yashar but inverts its direction. The "shoftim" (judges) are thus the embodied form of Or Chozer's governance principle.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar directly follows the Re'eh maamar's establishment of the soul's capacity for direct divine "seeing" (yechida-level perception) and now operationalizes that capacity across all seven body-gates. The hemshech is in the process of demonstrating that the divine service (avoda) outlined in Deuteronomy's final discourses corresponds precisely to the cosmic structure of sefirot, partzufim, and Or Yashar/Or Chozer that the earlier volumes established theoretically. Shoftim's "judges and officers at your gates" is the practical-institutional form of what the hemshech has been calling the Or Chozer mechanism: structured, governed ascent of divine light from the lowest (embodied) level to the highest (Keter). The oneg-framework introduced here will be further developed in subsequent maamarim examining how divine pleasure (ratzon/oneg) operates as the ultimate discriminating principle in both cosmic and human avodah.
