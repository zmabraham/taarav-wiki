## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Balak 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

The specific question focuses on understanding how the language used points to a deeper Kabbalistic reality within the hemshech's ongoing analysis.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Balak 1902-03. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ בלק, תער"ג corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar builds a precise theory of two types of cause-and-effect relationship (עילה ועלול, illah ve-alul) — one in which the effect is an "other" (מהות נבדל) from its cause, and one in which the effect is a genuine outgrowth/extension of its cause — and uses this distinction to explain what the Patriarchs achieved and why the foundation of the world required specifically them as its basis.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens with Bilaam's oracle: "כי מראש צורים אראנו" — "For from the top of rocks I see him." The Midrash Rabbah interprets this as referring to the Patriarchs: "מראש צורים אלו האבות" (the rocks are the Patriarchs). The Midrash then presents a striking parable: God sought to establish the world (לכונן) but could not find a foundation until the Patriarchs stood. Like a king seeking to build a city on watery ground and finding a great rock on which to anchor the entire city — so the Patriarchs were the "rock" that allowed God to "anchor" the cosmos. The maamar raises multiple difficulties: What does it mean that God "sought to establish the world and could not find" — does God lack for anything? What does "לכונן" mean, distinct from "creating"? Why does the Midrash say "the wicked did not permit" — how could the wicked prevent the divine will? Why does it say the Patriarchs "merited" (זכו) — what specifically did they merit? And why does the Midrash use the unusual divine name "HaMakom" (The Place)?

### The Argumentative Arc
- The hemshech has established that within the Sefirot of Atzilut, the flow of Or Yashar from Sefirah to Sefirah serves two different purposes: (a) Or for the sake of Atzilut itself (to constitute Atzilut as a world), and (b) Or for the sake of Beriyah-Yetzirah-Asiyah (BYA, the lower worlds). These are qualitatively different flows even within the same Sefirot.
- Using the four levels of mineral/vegetable/animal/speaking (דצח"מ, domem/tzomeyach/chai/medaber) as an analogy even within the intellectual realm: within intellect itself, there is the intellect-of-action (like a mineral, external), the intellect-of-nature (like a plant, growing organically), the intellect-of-speech (like an animal, responsive), and the intellect-of-pure-cognition (like a human/medaber, self-reflective). This four-level structure exists within each Sefirah of Atzilut too.
- Two types of causal relationship: (1) The effect is a "separate essence" (מהות נבדל) from the cause — they share only the formal level (both are "spiritual"), but the effect has its own substance distinct from the cause's substance. An analogy: the intellect and the emotions are in a causal relationship, but the emotions are a separate type of entity from the intellect — they do not share the same substance. (2) The effect is an "extension" (התפשטות) of the cause — like a branch from a tree, which is the same substance as the tree just in a different location. The branch does not have a separate essence from the trunk.
- The Or that flows from Atzilut to BYA is of type (1): it reaches the lower worlds as a "separate essence" — the lower worlds are genuinely different types of being from Atzilut, not simply diminished Atzilut. The flow within Atzilut itself can be of type (2): Sefirot-to-Sefirot within Atzilut can be an extension-type flow, where the lower Sefirah is a genuine outgrowth of the higher.
- The letters of thought, speech, and action (מחדו"מ, machshavah/dibbur/ma'aseh) are then analyzed as a third type of cause-effect — the "letter" (אות) as the power of revelation/disclosure (כח ההתגלות) that is itself distinct from what it reveals. A bird can be taught to mimic sounds but is not "speaking" — because it lacks the כח ההתגלות, the interior power of self-disclosure, that makes human language genuinely revelatory. The letters of speech are the power of disclosure inherent in the soul.
- This gives the maamar its key move: the Patriarchs achieved a causal connection to the cosmic structure of type (2) — their service was an "extension" type, where their actions genuinely extended the divine essence into the world rather than merely creating a causally connected but ontologically separate effect. They were the "rock" — not an instrument God used but the substance God used, a genuine extension of the divine building-intention into worldly form.
- The divine name HaMakom (The Place) is explained: "place" signifies the ontological "location" of a thing — its rank and intrinsic value, not its spatial position. The Patriarchs "found their place" (הכינו מקום) in the cosmic structure, meaning they established a genuine ontological position within the divine order from which the world's own place could be grounded.

### What It Gets To
The maamar establishes that the Patriarchs' founding role was not primarily about their moral excellence (though that was present) but about their ontological function: they were the first beings in the lower world to exist in a type-2 causal relationship with the divine — as genuine extensions of the divine essence into the world, rather than merely as creatures connected to the divine by a causal chain of type-1 (separate essences linked). This is the meaning of "מראש צורים" — from the apex of the "rocks" (the firm foundation), the entire structure of Israel becomes visible.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar provides the hemshech's fullest treatment of the cause-effect taxonomy applied to divine-human relationships, and resolves a standing question in the hemshech: how can finite beings (the Patriarchs) genuinely "found" the cosmos if they are ontologically separate from the divine source? The answer — that the Patriarchs existed in a type-2 (extension) causal relationship — allows the hemshech to explain how Torah and mitzvot (which the Jewish people inherited from the Patriarchs) are not merely "connected" to the divine but are genuine extensions of it, making the dira betachtonim not a metaphor but an ontological reality.
