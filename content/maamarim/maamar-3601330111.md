## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Shemini 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs one of the hemshech's most technically precise analyses of the tzimtzum's twofold effect on divine light. Using the "eighth day" (Yom HaShemini) of the Mishkan's inauguration as its frame, the Rashab establishes that the tzimtzum did not perform a single act but two distinct operations on the Or: (1) it caused the Encompassing light (Or Ein Sof Bli Gvul) to withdraw from the created space and become concealed within its source; and (2) it caused the light that was destined to flow into the worlds to become separated from the inner dimension of the Or and exist independently — a separation that both diminished the Or's quality and, by the mechanism of the Kav (line of light) entering through a vacated space, made it possible for the Or to differentiate into individuated Sefirot-levels.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with the verse: "ויהי ביום השמיני קרא משה לאהרן ולבניו ולזקני ישראל" — "And it was on the eighth day that Moses called Aaron and his sons and the elders of Israel." Three exegetical difficulties are raised: (1) Aaron and his sons had been confined to the Ohel Moed for the seven days of the inaugural ceremony (ומפתח אהל מועד לא תצאו שבעת ימים) — so why did Moses need to "call" them, as if summoning them from elsewhere? (2) Why were the elders of Israel summoned, since nothing in the subsequent narrative addresses them specifically? (3) The Talmud (Shabbat 87b) states that the eighth day received "ten crowns" (עשר עטרות) — what is the nature of these ten crowns, and why specifically on the eighth day?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The tzimtzum's first effect — withdrawal of Or Ein Sof Bli Gvul**: Before the tzimtzum, the Or Ein Sof filled the space that would become the "world" (the Chalal, the vacated space). The tzimtzum's first effect was the withdrawal of this Encompassing light (Or Ein Sof in its Bli Gvul dimension) into its source — it was not destroyed but concentrated back into the Ein Sof's essential core. When this Or was present, everything in the space perceived G-dliness as the dominant reality; after its withdrawal, the material/created reality (Yesh) became the dominant perception. The first effect of tzimtzum is thus the reversal of polarity: from G-d-obvious to G-d-concealed.
- **The tzimtzum's second effect — separation of the Or relating to the worlds**: The Or that had been destined to flow through the worlds (the proto-Or of the Sefirot) had previously been entirely embedded in and unified with the pnimiyut (inner dimension) of the Or Ein Sof. The tzimtzum's second effect was the separation of this world-relating Or from its embedding in the pnimiyut: it was isolated as the Reshimu (impression) within the Chalal. This separation diminished the quality of the Or doubly — (a) it was separated from the pnimiyut that elevated it; (b) the tzimtzum caused it to enter via a "leap" (dilug — the Kav's entry into the Chalal without gradation), so it lacked the continuous chain that would have existed without the tzimtzum.
- **The result: Or in a state of divisions (hitchalekut)**: The double effect of the tzimtzum brings the Or into a state of divisions — not the intrinsic divisions of different Sefirot-levels (which is a positive differentiation), but the fragmented, externalized divisions that result from the Or encountering the "opposite" (the Chalal, the space of non-divine appearing). The Or's encounter with the Davar Hanegdi (the opposing principle) causes it to splinter into partial perspectives rather than unify into a single revelatory whole.
- **The Kli (vessel) as the tzimtzum's positive product**: The same tzimtzum process that fragments the Or also produces the Kelim (vessels). The Rashab follows the Etz Chaim: from the thickening (hit'abut) of the Or — its encounter with the Chalal's resistance — the vessels are formed. The vessels are the tzimtzum's positive contribution: without the resistance of the Chalal, the Or would remain undifferentiated and there would be no vessel capable of receiving it in a structured way. The Kli is the fragmentation of the Or transformed into a structured receptacle.
- **The eighth day and the ten crowns**: The number eight (shemoneh) transcends the seven-day cycle of creation and is associated in Kabbalistic thought with the level above nature — the Or that precedes and encompasses the natural order (Sheva = the seven Sefirot of the emotional range; Shemoneh = Binah and above, or Keter). The "eighth day" of the Mishkan's inauguration is the first day on which the Mishkan is fully operational — the first day of the Or's permanent dwelling in the completed structure. The Talmud's "ten crowns" (עשר עטרות) correspond to the ten Sefirot as they appeared simultaneously on that day in their fully operational configuration — not the sequential Sefirot of the chain of hishtalshelut, but all ten simultaneously, which is the signature of the Sovev Kol Almin's encompassing presence. The "calling" of Aaron (who represents Chesed — the first Sefirah of the emotive range) and the elders of Israel (who represent the full community of Sefirot in their human instantiation) signals that all ten levels of the divine structure were simultaneously activated on that day.
- **Or Zarua l'tzaddik — the light planted for the righteous**: The Rashab introduces the verse "אור זרוע לצדיק" (light is planted for the righteous, Psalms 97:11) and the metaphor of seed-planting: just as a seed's inner spiritual essence (the potential of the fruit) must be "buried" in the earth and pass through dissolution before it can germinate into a new plant, so the Or must be "planted" — enclosed in vessels, subjected to the tzimtzum's concealment — before it can be revealed in the structured form of the Sefirot. The eighth day is when the Or Zarua (planted light) first germinates into full visible bloom.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the "ten crowns" of the eighth day are the ten Sefirot appearing simultaneously (not sequentially) because the Mishkan's completion has reversed the tzimtzum's second effect: by providing a perfect vessel-structure (the Mishkan as Kli) that can hold the Or without fragmenting it, the Or can now appear in all its dimensions at once. This is what the eighth day achieves that the seven preparatory days could not: the full simultaneous revelation of the Or through the completed vessel.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Parshat Shemini provides the hemshech with its most precise technical vocabulary for the tzimtzum's dual mechanism and the Or Zarua principle. The tzimtzum is not merely "withdrawal" — it is a two-stage operation whose second effect (the separation and degradation of the world-relating Or) is precisely what the Mishkan is designed to repair. The "eighth day" is the hemshech's paradigm for what full dira betachtonim looks like in practice: all ten Sefirot simultaneously present, the Or no longer fragmented by the tzimtzum's opposition, because the Kelim (Mishkan-vessels) are now adequate to the Or. This analysis will reverberate through the subsequent maamarim on purity/impurity (Tazria-Metzora), Yom Kippur, and the counting of the Omer.
