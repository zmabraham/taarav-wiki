## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Miketz, Shabbat Chanukah 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar — delivered on Shabbat Chanukah, when two distinct illuminations overlap — builds the fundamental distinction between two modes of divine light and their respective spatial orientations: the light that flows from above-to-below (Or Yashar, associated with Shabbat candles: inside the house, on the right) vs. the light that shines outward to reveal divinity within darkness (Or Chozer, associated with Chanukah candles: outside the house, on the left). The geometry of candle placement is the entryway to a full analysis of how these two modes of illumination relate to one another and to the structure of the worlds.

### The Opening Move

The halacha states: "Ner Chanukah, mitzvah l'hanicha al petach beito mibachutz" — the Chanukah lamp must be placed at the outer doorway, outside the house, specifically on the left. Shabbat candles, by contrast, are placed inside the house on the right. The Rashab identifies this as a deep structural puzzle: both sets of candles are sacred lights — why do they occupy opposite positions? If both express divine illumination, why should one shine outward-left and the other illuminate inward-right? The halakhic distinction is not merely technical but encodes a fundamental teaching about two distinct dynamics of divine light.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The distinction between "from below to above" and "from above to below" is the master key**: the source text opens by restating the hemshech's core thesis: the ascent-mode (malin l'matla l'ma'la — bottom to top, Or Chozer) is determined by the quality and preparation of the receiving vessel; the descent-mode (malin l'ma'la l'matta — top to bottom, Or Yashar) flows from the very essence of Ein Sof, not calibrated to vessel capacity but from the divine will itself.
- **Shabbat candles = Or Yashar (inside, right)**: Shabbat lights serve shalom bayit — the peace and harmony of the home. This is Or Yashar operating in its proper domain: it illuminates the interior (the inside of the house = the interior of the seder hishtalshelut), flows through the right side (chesed, mercy, the natural direction of divine outpouring), and creates internal harmony among the vessels. This light does not need to confront the darkness outside because it is by nature a completing and harmonizing force.
- **Chanukah candles = Or Chozer / Or Makif (outside, left)**: Chanukah commemorates the victory of the few over the many, the pure over the impure — a victory achieved not by natural superiority but by the power of Or Chozer penetrating into and transforming the domain of the kelipot. The placement outside (in the domain of the "street," the public domain that is governed by the kelipot's influence) and on the left (the side of Gevura, judgment, the restrictive power that is required to carve out a space for holiness within the impure) reflects Or Chozer's mandate: to shine into the darkness from without.
- **The miracle of the oil — one day's oil lasting eight days — is Or Makif breaking the natural order**: natural oil can sustain flame for a determined duration; the miracle of eight days represents light that flows from a level above the natural seder hishtalshelut — from Or Makif, which is not bound by the vessels' carrying capacity. This is why Chanukah has the unique power of "pirsum ha-nes" (publicizing the miracle) — it is Or Makif becoming visible, which is by definition extraordinary.
- **The left side / Gevura as the axis of Or Chozer**: in the hemshech's framework, Or Chozer ascends through the left column of the sefirot — Gevura, Hod, Binah — because these sefirot represent the contractive, bounding function that gives the ascent its structure. Or Yashar descends through the right column — Chesed, Netzach, Chokhmah — because these represent expansive, giving energy. The Chanukah candle on the left side is thus not a minor detail but a precise mapping of Or Chozer's structural axis.
- **The double occasion (Shabbat Chanukah) as the meeting of Or Yashar and Or Chozer**: when Shabbat and Chanukah coincide, the two lights must coexist — Shabbat candles inside-right, Chanukah candles outside-left. This spatial juxtaposition is the physical enactment of what the hemshech has been building: the complete circuit of Or Yashar and Or Chozer operating simultaneously, each in its proper domain, creating together the fullest possible revelation of divine light.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the apparently minor halakhic question about candle placement encodes the entire Kabbalistic architecture of Or Yashar and Or Chozer: their different directions, different sides, different domains of operation, and different relationships to the natural order. Shabbat light perfects the interior; Chanukah light penetrates the exterior. Together on Shabbat Chanukah, they represent the ideal state of avodah — the simultaneous flow of divine grace inward and the human elevation of sparks outward.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This is one of the Taarav's most practically grounded maamarim, demonstrating that the abstract framework of Or Yashar/Or Chozer is not merely theoretical but is encoded in the halakha's every detail — the very geometry of candle placement. The maamar also introduces a key distinction that will be developed further: the difference between the light that harmonizes the existing order (shalom bayit, Shabbat) and the light that transforms the order by penetrating into what lies outside it (pirsum ha-nes, Chanukah). This distinction between interior-harmonizing and exterior-transforming light becomes central to subsequent discussions of how the dira b'tachtonim is ultimately achieved: not merely by making the existing vessels more luminous, but by extending the light into the domain that was previously its outside.
