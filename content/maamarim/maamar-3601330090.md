## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayetze 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar develops the concept of the sulam (ladder) — Yaakov's dream ladder — as the precise structural model for how Or Chozer operates: a graduated, continuous ascent from the lowest point of reality ("mutzav artzah," grounded in earth) to the highest ("rosh magi'a hashamaymah," reaching heaven), demonstrating that the upward movement of Or Chozer is not a leap but a meticulous step-by-step elevation through the entire chain of worlds.

### The Opening Move

The central verse is "ויחלם והנה סולם מוצב ארצה וראשו מגיע השמימה והנה מלאכי אלקים עולים ויורדים בו" — Yaakov dreamed of a ladder planted in earth with its top reaching heaven, and angels of God ascending and descending upon it. The Rashab poses a well-known question: the order of the angels' movement is "ascending" first, then "descending" — when logic would suggest they should first descend to the earthly realm and then ascend. Why is the ascending direction given primacy?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The ladder corresponds to the structure of Or Chozer in its complete form**: the sulam encodes the principle that Or Chozer (ascending light generated by human avodah) is primary — it goes "up" first — because the entire purpose of the descent of divine light (Or Yashar) is to make the ascent possible. The descent of angels is in service of the ascent.
- **"Mutzav artzah" — the ladder must be grounded**: a critical teaching here is that Or Chozer cannot be an escape from the material world but must begin precisely there. The ladder's base is planted in aretz (earth), meaning the avodah must start from the most concrete, embodied level of existence — the world of Asiyah — and ascend from there.
- **The angels represent different "columns" of the seder hishtalshelut**: those ascending are the forces of human avodah (mayin nukvin, feminine waters) rising through the worlds; those descending are the divine influx (mayin duchrin, masculine waters) responding to that rise. The simultaneous traffic encodes the responsiveness of the divine system.
- **"Rosh magi'a hashamaymah" — the top reaches Keter**: the ladder's summit is not merely the upper worlds but specifically the level of Keter — the crown that connects the finite chain of worlds to Ein Sof. This is the Rashab's key move: Yaakov's sulam is not a generic description of spiritual ascent but a precise diagram of how Or Chozer reaches all the way to Keter-level and brings down a commensurate Or Yashar.
- **The dream context points to the Or Makif dimension**: Yaakov experiences this as a dream (chalom), and in the hemshech's developing vocabulary, the dream state corresponds to Or Makif — the surrounding light that hovers above the vessels of waking consciousness. The revelation comes specifically through the dream because Or Makif is not accessible to the vessels of Or Pnimi in their waking, bounded state.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes the sulam as the master image for graduated ascent through the entire seder hishtalshelut — each rung corresponding to a world (Asiyah, Yetzira, Beriah, Atzilut) — and concludes that the angels ascending first signals the ontological priority of Or Chozer: the entire divine system is configured so that the human ascent draws down the commensurate divine descent, not the reverse.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The sulam maamar occupies a pivotal position in the Taarav because it provides the structural image that unifies the hemshech's central themes: the ladder visualizes the complete circuit of Or Yashar descending and Or Chozer ascending through the identical rungs of the hishtalshelut. The hemshech will continue to elaborate on how each world processes this circuit differently, and how the neshama's avodah at the level of Asiyah sets the entire ladder in motion from its earthly base.
