## Context

This maamar was delivered during **2nd day of Rosh Hashana 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: On Rosh Hashanah (2nd day of Rosh Hashana 1902-03), the Rashab addresses the tension between divine kingship (מלכות, Malchut) and divine transcendence: if God is truly infinite and beyond all relationship with creation, how can there be a meaningful 'coronation' (הכתרה) of God as king? What does it mean to declare 'the Lord reigns' when God transcends all categories of governance?

**Central Concept**: The distinction between God's essential being (עצמות, Atzmus) and God's revelation as king (מלכות, Malchut) — Malchut is the Sefirah through which the infinite becomes relationally accessible to created beings without compromising the divine transcendence. The Rashab develops how the concept of 'king' points precisely to the aspect of divinity that interfaces with those it governs.

**Resolution**: The 'coronation' of Rosh Hashanah refers not to a change in divine being but to a heightened revelation of Malchut — the dimension of divine kingship that is always present but becomes more accessible to finite perception on this day. The Or Chozer of the collective soul's prayer on Rosh Hashanah uniquely draws down this revelation.

## Deep Dive

### Opening Kushya

The maamar opens with a double question on the verse אֲנִי לְדוֹדִי וְדוֹדִי לִי הָרוֹעֶה בַּשּׁוֹשַׁנִּים (Shir HaShirim 6:3), citing the Zohar's reading that "ba-shoshanim" means "she-shonim ba-halachot" — those who study halachot. The Rashab presses: מה שייך ענין ששונים בהלכות לאני לדודי כו' — "what connection is there between studying halachot and the relationship of 'I am to my beloved'?" And secondly: what does "ro'eh" (shepherd/pasturing) have to do with roses? Shepherding belongs to flocks, not flowers.

### Argumentative Arc

The maamar spends its central sections (perakim 72–74) establishing a correspondence between five levels in the blessing Birkas Yotzer ("Elokei olam... b'rachamecha ha-rabbim... rachem aleinu... adon ozeinu... tzur mishgaveinu...") and five levels previously analyzed in Birkas Shir She-amar. These map onto: (1) Atzmus Ein Sof — named only as "Boreh," the Creator, since only the act of yesh me-ayin reveals His true self-sufficiency (מציאותו מעצמותו); (2) the essential light of Ein Sof as source of all makifim — "b'rachamecha ha-rabbim"; (3) the simple Ratzon (will) as makif of Yechidah — "rachem aleinu"; (4) the makif of Chayah — "adon ozeinu"; and (5–7) the three collective makifim of BYA. The principle invoked is נעוץ תחלתן בסופן: Malchut, lowest of the sefirot, is rooted more deeply in Atzmus than Chochmah is, precisely because the power of yesh me-ayin belongs to Atzmus alone and Malchut alone inherits it within the chain of worlds.

Perek 75 then identifies "adon" (master) with the masculine aspect of Malchut — אדון נקרא המלכות כשיש לה סימני דכורא — and develops the concept of "galus ha-nefesh" (exile of the soul). When the Nefesh HaBahamis dominates, the Nefesh HaElokis is in a state of exile, unable to arouse love and awe from within itself: וענין החלישות הוא שכחות הנה"א נחלשים. The remedy is Torah, called עוז — it provides the strength (oz) to break free: והתורה נותנת כח ועוז לנפש האלקית להתגבר.

### Chiddush and Resolution

The maamar resolves its opening question: "Ani l'Dodi" — the soul's movement toward the beloved — is the avodah of atara'ta d'l'tata (arousal from below), but this arousal is only possible "ba-shoshanim she-shonim ba-halachot" — through Torah study, which supplies the strength the weakened soul lacks. And "ha-ro'eh ba-shoshanim" refers to the divine raa'va d'liba — a supernal arousal that descends in response, since the Torah is expounded through the thirteen attributes of mercy (נדרשת בי"ג מדות שהן י"ג מדה"ר). The shepherd-rose image thus encodes the full teshuvah arc: Elul (שכחי עמך — lower teshuvah, self-restraint from habitual materiality) prepares for the higher teshuvah of Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur. אני לדודי in Elul; ודודי לי — the divine response — through the Torah's shepherding light.
