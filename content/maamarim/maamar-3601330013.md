## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Matot-Masei 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

**Opening Question**: The maamar opens by citing the verse or teaching: "וידבר משה אל ראשי המטות לבנ"י לאמר זה הדבר אשר צוה ה' כו'" and raising a textual difficulty (קושיא, kushya): why does the text phrase the matter in this particular way? The specific question focuses on understanding how the language used points to a deeper Kabbalistic reality within the hemshech's ongoing analysis.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Matot-Masei 1901-02. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ מטו"מ, תער"ב corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the precise account of *Ma'aleh u'Matah* (above and below) as a function of *rov u'mi'ut ha-or* (abundance and diminishment of light) within the graduated structure of the Sefirot — establishing that "above" and "below" are not metaphors for ontological dignity but technical descriptors of light-quantity, and that this graduated diminishment is the direct mechanism by which the Sefirot and worlds can *emanate* (hishtalshelut) from one another without the lower being identical to the higher or completely other.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens from the verse in Parshat Matot (Numbers 30:2): "Moses spoke to the heads of the tribes of the children of Israel, saying: This is the word which God has commanded." The question is twofold: why preface the laws of vows with "this is the word which God commanded" — what does this introductory formula add that "God spoke to Moses saying" did not already convey? And why were the laws of vows addressed specifically to the heads of the tribes (*rashei ha-matot*), when all other laws were addressed to all of Israel? The heads of tribes represent specific graduated channels — the word reaches the people through the structured tribal hierarchy, not undifferentiated. This hierarchical mediation is the maamar's structural theme.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The foundation from preceding maamarim**: the sefirot ha-genuzot were individuated through the Tzimtzum, producing the differentiated Sefirot of Atzilut. Each Sefirah now has its own distinct presence (meziut) and its own light-quantity. The question that now arises: in what sense is one Sefirah "above" another?

- **Ma'aleh u'Matah defined as rov u'mi'ut**: the source text (chapter 37) makes the central claim explicit: "above and below" in the Sefirot means that the higher Sefirah has *more* light (rov ha-or) and the lower has *less* (mi'ut ha-or). Chochmah is above Binah not because it is more real or more divine in some abstract sense, but because within Chochmah the divine light radiates more fully — the point of Chochmah (nekudat ha-haskalah) contains concentrated luminosity before expansion. In Binah, the same light spreads into understanding but in doing so becomes less concentrated — and this spreading-and-thinning is the movement "downward."

- **The crucial proof-case: non-hierarchical revelation (Tohu)**. The world of Tohu (chaos, pre-creation) had no ma'aleh u'matah — all Sefirot radiated with equal intensity (shaveh). This sounds like it should be superior, yet the world of Tohu shattered (shevirat ha-kelim). The maamar's argument: equal-light distribution without graduated diminishment is incompatible with stable structure. Without some Sefirah having *less* light than another, there is no impetus for the lower to receive from the upper — no directionality, no stable chain. Ma'aleh u'matah is what makes hishtalshelut possible. Tohu's failure was not too much light but too much light *distributed equally* — a flat, structureless profusion that could not sustain vessels.

- **The mechanism of emanation (hishtalshelut)**: how does Binah emanate from Chochmah? The source text (chapter 38) details: Chochmah must diminish its light in its own lower levels (netzach-hod-yesod of Abba, the outer registers of Chochmah's self-expression) for Binah to receive and stand as a distinct level. If Chochmah's light were uniformly maximal throughout itself, Binah could not be called forth from it — it would simply be absorbed back into Chochmah's profusion. The diminishment creates the differential that enables the new Sefirah's individuation. Similarly, Malkhut — the lowest Sefirah (sof kol ha-dargot, "end of all levels") — must have the most diminished light within Atzilut so that Beriah can emerge from it.

- **"This is the word which God commanded"**: the double formulation is read as pointing to both the Or Yashar dimension (the word as it descended from divine speech — "God commanded") and the or-level of Malkhut/speech as such, which is already a diminished emanation. The laws of vows are specifically addressed to the *rashei ha-matot* (tribal heads) because vows operate at the Malkhut-of-speech level: they are binding specifically through the human word, which is the most diminished light-expression (the analog of Malkhut in the human soul). The tribal leaders, who mediate the law to the people, enact structurally the graduated ma'aleh u'matah of the Sefirot-chain.

- **Why diminishment is not deficiency**: the maamar carefully forestalls the conclusion that mi'ut ha-or (light-diminishment) implies deficiency or imperfection. The analogy: a king's word as spoken to his minister has less of the king's presence than his thought — yet the minister's grasp of the spoken word may be more effective for governing the kingdom than direct unmediated royal thought. Each level has its own completeness within its mode. The diminishment is what enables structured governance; it is not a flaw in the cosmic design.

### What It Gets To

The maamar establishes with precision that the Sefirot's gradation is a *light-quantity* gradation, and that this gradient is the direct enabling condition of the chain of worlds (hishtalshelut). Without ma'aleh u'matah, there is no Beriah, Yetzirah, or Asiyah — the graduated diminishment of light from Keter all the way to Malkhut is what creates the ontological space for created worlds to exist. This is the hemshech's systematic treatment of Or Yashar's descent-mechanism.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar completes the hemshech's analysis of the Sefirot-internal structure that was opened with the Chukat maamar's first discussion of ma'aleh u'matah. Together these two maamarim (Chukat and Matot) constitute the hemshech's complete treatment of how Or Yashar descends through the Sefirot by graduated diminishment. Having now established the descent-mechanism in full, the hemshech is positioned to turn toward Devarim (Deuteronomy) and begin the analysis of how Or Chozer — the upward return movement — operates through human divine service.
