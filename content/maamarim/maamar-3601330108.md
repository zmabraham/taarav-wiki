## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Tzav, Shabbat HaGadol 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar — delivered on Shabbat HaGadol, the great Shabbat before Pesach — weaves two threads: (1) the specific urgency encoded in the word "tzav" (command with alacrity, beyond ordinary mitzvah) as it applies to the korbanot-laws of Parshat Tzav, and (2) the imminent-redemption dimension of Shabbat HaGadol. The construct being built is the relationship between the Mishkan's eternal fire (esh tamid — a fire that never goes out on the Mizbeach) and the soul's continuous drive of ratzo (yearning-ascent), demonstrating that the fire and the yearning are phenomenologically identical: both are Or Chozer in its most concentrated, continuously active form.

### The Opening Move

Parshat Tzav opens: "צַו אֶת אַהֲרֹן וְאֶת בָּנָיו לֵאמֹר" — "Command Aaron and his sons." The Rashab notes the Sifra's teaching that the word "tzav" (as opposed to the usual "daber" or "emor" — speak or say) carries the sense of ziruz (alacrity, urgency) both for the moment and for all future generations. The question is: why do these particular laws — the olah and the perpetual fire — require urgency beyond ordinary mitzvah-obligation? What is it about the olah and the eternal fire that makes slackness specifically dangerous here?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Tzav and ziruz as the Or Chozer's essential quality**: The urgency (ziruz) encoded in tzav corresponds to the nature of Or Chozer itself: unlike Or Yashar, which descends through patient, graduated steps (tzimtzum after tzimtzum, world after world), Or Chozer's ascent has an inherently urgent, non-patient character — it is ratzo (running, yearning), the soul's instinctive upward thrust. The Sifra's teaching that tzav implies urgency "for generations" (l'dorot) means that this urgency must be built into every generation's access to the Mishkan-service — it cannot become routine or mechanical.
- **The olah (burnt offering) as complete Or Chozer**: The olah is the korban that ascends entirely — no part of it is eaten by priests or offerers; it is all consumed on the altar and rises as smoke. This makes the olah the purest formal expression of Or Chozer: nothing remains below. The Rashab contrasts this with shelamim (peace offerings) where parts remain at each level (offerer, priest, altar), representing Or Chozer that is partial and graduated. The olah's totality of ascent corresponds to the level of Or Chozer that originates not from individual Sefirot but from the level of Yechida — the soul's most essential root-point.
- **The eternal fire (esh tamid) and the soul's continuous ratzo**: The command "a fire shall be kept burning on the altar continuously, it shall not go out" (Vayikra 6:6) is the Rashab's primary textual anchor. The esh tamid is not merely a practical requirement (that the sacrificial fire not be extinguished) but a cosmic structural requirement: the Or Chozer must never cease. If the ascending movement stops even momentarily, the circuit between Or Yashar and Or Chozer is broken and the Mishkan-structure collapses. The eternal fire is the physical sign that Or Chozer is a permanent structural feature of reality, not an intermittent occurrence.
- **Shabbat HaGadol and the pre-Pesach suspension of routine**: Shabbat HaGadol adds a specific dimension: it is the Shabbat on which, in Egypt, the Israelites first took the Pesach lamb (separating it from the Egyptian flock on the 10th of Nissan, holding it for four days). This act — holding a living animal for sacrifice — is the paradigm of Or Chozer in its preparatory stage: not yet the full ascent (which happens at the Pesach seder's sacrifice) but the drawing-near (karov) of the material world toward its moment of elevation. The Shabbat HaGadol adds to the Tzav-maamar the temporal dimension: the urgency of tzav operates specifically in the period of preparation before the great moment of redemption.
- **The Kohen's obligation of the morning and evening fire-service**: The priests must add wood to the Mizbeach fire every morning and every evening. The Rashab reads the morning fire-service as corresponding to Or Yashar's descent at the start of each cosmic "day" (the illumination from above), and the evening fire-service as corresponding to Or Chozer's intensification at the point of Or Yashar's apparent withdrawal. The twice-daily maintenance mirrors the twice-daily rhythm of Ratzo V'Shov: the forward surge (morning) and the return-sustain (evening).

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the "tzav" of this parasha encodes the hemshech's most important practical principle: Or Chozer is not self-sustaining. Unlike Or Yashar, which flows from Ein Sof's infinite source without requiring human initiative, Or Chozer requires conscious, urgent, continuous human effort — the esh tamid of the Mizbeach must be maintained by the priests. Divine dwelling in the lower world (dira betachtonim) is not a static achievement but a dynamic process requiring constant renewal from below.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Tzav-Shabbat HaGadol maamar introduces a crucial corrective to any passively-received reading of the hemshech's cosmological structure. The Rashab has spent many maamarim building the Mishkan-model of divine dwelling; here he insists that the model is not self-executing. The esh tamid principle — sustained urgency from below — is what transitions the hemshech from pure cosmological description toward the avodah dimension: the specific practices (the perpetual fire, the morning and evening service) through which human beings participate in maintaining the divine-cosmic circuit. This prepares directly for the Pesach maamarim that follow.
