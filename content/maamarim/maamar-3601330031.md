## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayishlach 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs the distinction between *kochot ha-nefesh* (the soul's powers/faculties — intellect and emotions) and *levushei ha-nefesh* (the soul's garments — thought, speech, and action) as two categorically different relationships between the soul and its expressions. Mapped onto the sefirot-system, this distinction completes the hemshech's typology of revelation-and-concealment by showing that the soul's *powers* function as transparent kelim (analogous to the sefirot of Atzilut) while the soul's *garments* function as opaque levushim (analogous to the lower worlds' concealing structures).

### The Opening Move

The parasha of Vayishlach opens with Yaakov sending *malachim* (messengers/angels) to Esav before their fateful reunion. The homiletical question: why does the Torah use the word *malachim* — a term that everywhere else denotes supernatural angels — for what are apparently human messengers? The Rashab locates the answer in the dual meaning of *malakh*: an angel is, by definition, a being whose entire existence is defined by its *shlichut* (mission) — it has no independent existence apart from its task. This is precisely the definition of the soul's *kochot* (powers): they are not the soul itself but are entirely in the soul's service, defined by what they do for the soul.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Kochot as levushim miyuhadim (unified garments)**: The soul's powers — intellect (*sekhel*) and emotions (*midot*) — are not the soul's essence but are its *levushim miyuhadim* (unified garments). They are "garments" because they are not the *atzem ha-nefesh* (essential soul); but they are "unified" garments because, unlike the levushim of thought, speech, and action, they cannot be separated from the soul and exchanged. The intellect that a given soul has *is* that soul's intellect — it cannot be replaced by a different intellect while the same soul remains.

- **Evidence of non-essentiality: change through life-stages**: The maamar's proof that the kochot are not the soul's essence: they change as the person matures (*yemim yedaberu, u-rov shanim yodi'u chokhmah* — "Days shall speak, and many years shall make wisdom known," Job 32:7). A child's intellect and emotions are small; they grow with maturity. But a true essence (*atzem*) cannot change — if the kochot changed, they are not the atzem. They are revealed through the body's vessels (the brain and heart) and calibrated by the size and readiness of those vessels — another mark of non-essential status.

- **Levushim de-makhashavah, dibbur, ma'aseh (thought, speech, action)**: By contrast, the three *levushim* (garments proper) — thought, speech, and action — are categorically external to the soul. They can be "exchanged": the same intellectual content can be expressed in different words, or the same intention can produce different actions. They are not *of the soul's substance* but vehicles through which the soul expresses itself in the world. Letters (the building blocks of thought and speech) are inherently concealing — they communicate to those who can decode them, but the letters themselves are not the meaning.

- **Kelim of Atzilut = kochot ha-nefesh**: The sefirot-vessels of Atzilut are *meyukhadim* (unified) with the Orot within them in exactly the same sense as the soul's kochot are unified with the soul. They are not the Orot themselves but are entirely oriented toward them, calibrated by them, and inseparable from them. This is why Atzilut is called the world of *giluy* (revelation): its vessels are transparent instruments of the Or, not opaque coverings of it.

- **Lower worlds = levushim de-makhashavah, dibbur, ma'aseh**: The worlds of Beriyah, Yetzirah, and Asiyah correspond to the three *levushim* of thought, speech, and action respectively. They are structures genuinely external to the Atzilut-Or; they can conceal or reveal, depending on whether they are used in alignment with Or Pnimi. Human divine service in these worlds is the work of aligning the external garments with the soul's internal powers — making the levushim into transparent expressions of the kochot rather than opaque barriers to them.

- **Yaakov's malachim as kochot**: Yaakov's sending of *malachim* to Esav thus encodes the entire kochot/levushim structure. Yaakov (representing the soul's essential level, connected to the Yechida) sends his *kochot* (the malachim who are entirely his emissaries, defined by his mission) into the world of Esav (representing the external levush-world of chitzoniyut). The kochot enter the external world not to become identified with it but to transform it — to align it with its source in the penimiyut ha-kli.

### What It Gets To

The maamar completes the hemshech's analytical framework for understanding the soul's relationship to its own expressions: kochot (unified with the soul, transparent instruments) and levushim (external to the soul, potentially opaque). This framework is the direct soul-analogue of the sefirot's or/kli structure and the Atzilut/lower-worlds distinction. The full mapping is now complete: soul/Atzmus → kochot/sefirot-kelim of Atzilut → levushim/lower-world structures.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Maamarim 27–30 developed the sefirot-side of the or/kli typology — the three meanings of sefirah, ayin/yesh, and penimiyut/chitzoniyut ha-kli. This maamar now provides the soul-side of the same typology, formally establishing the parallelism between the soul's internal structure and the cosmic structure of the sefirot. With both sides of the analogy fully developed, the hemshech now has the complete conceptual apparatus for its sustained argument about how human divine service — operating through the soul's kochot and levushim — actually effects transformations in the cosmic sefirot-structure, thereby realizing the *dira betachtonim* in practice.
