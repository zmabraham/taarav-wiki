## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Ki Tetze 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs a Kabbalistic reading of "going out to war" (ki tetze lamilchama) as the soul's engagement with the realm of klipa and sitra achra — not as an external enemy but as the internal condition of Or Yashar's descent into a world of concealment. The maamar advances the hemshech's account of how Or Chozer is generated specifically through the soul's confrontation with resistance, building the argument that genuine uplift (or chozer) is only possible when the soul has truly "gone out" — fully entered — the domain of lower-world conflict.

### The Opening Move

The verse "Ki tetze lamilchama al oyvecha — When you go out to war against your enemies" (Deut. 20:1) generates the opening paradox: the Torah promises "u'netano Hashem Elokecha b'yadecha — and the Lord your God will deliver them into your hand." If the divine deliverance is guaranteed, why must one "go out" to fight at all? The very structure of the verse — going out precedes the promise — raises the question of what ontological work is accomplished by the soul's act of descent and engagement, before the divine victory manifests.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **"Ki tetze" as descent of the divine soul (nefesh haElohit)**: The Rashab reads the "going out" not as the body's physical deployment but as the soul's volitional departure from its native state of divine illumination into the mixed-light world of Asiyah. This maps onto the hemshech's account of Or Yashar's descent: just as Or Yashar "goes out" from Ein Sof through progressive tzimtzumim into the lower worlds, the divine soul "goes out" from its source (yechida, the highest soul-level) into the realm of the animal soul (nefesh habehamit) and the body.
- **The enemy (oyev) as the domain of concealment**: The "enemies" (oyvecha) are identified as the forces that obstruct the Or Chozer — specifically, the densification of matter and the domination of self-referential desire (ta'avah) that prevents the soul's light from ascending. This is not a moral dualism but an ontological description of how Or Yashar, having descended through many tzimtzumim, reaches a level where it is so compressed that the soul experiences it as concealment rather than revelation.
- **The prerequisite of full engagement**: The hemshech's key move here is that Or Chozer — the ascending, reflected light — can only be generated from the precise level to which Or Yashar descended. A soul that avoids the conflict (remains in the "camp," b'machaneh) generates no Or Chozer from that lower level; only a soul that fully "goes out" — that accepts the embodied, conflictual condition — can reflect light back from that depth. This is the metaphysical warrant for the priority of practical mitzva-performance in the physical world over purely contemplative practice.
- **Shiluach ha'kan (sending away the mother-bird) as paradigm**: The parasha's famous mitzva of shiluach ha'kan (sending away the mother-bird before taking the eggs, Deut. 22:6-7) is the culminating practical case: the mother-bird's departure represents the Or Yashar's temporary concealment (tzimtzum) so that the "eggs" — the lower sparks needing elevation — can be taken (elevated) by the human agent. The reward "l'ma'an yitav lach v'ha'arachta yamim — that it may go well with you and you may lengthen your days" is interpreted as the elongation of the Or Chozer process, the extension of divine light into the temporal dimension through the soul's sustained engagement with the lower world.
- **Amalek as the negation of Or Chozer**: The portion's command to "blot out the memory of Amalek" (Deut. 25:19) is read through the hemshech as the destruction of the principle that denies Or Chozer — Amalek's name gematria (240) relates to the concept of "safek" (doubt, 240), the doubt that human action in the lower world can genuinely elevate. Amalek's spiritual category is the claim that the soul's "going out to war" produces no Or Chozer — that the descent is only a descent and not the necessary precondition of ascent. The command to erase Amalek is thus the command to eliminate this metaphysical doubt from the soul's avodah.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that "ki tetze lamilchama" is not a contingency (if you happen to go to war) but a structural description of the soul's condition in the lower worlds: the divine soul is always already "going out" — always in the condition of Or Yashar's descent encountering resistance. The guarantee of "u'netano Hashem b'yadecha" is not a promise of easy victory but a description of the ontological outcome: when the soul fully accepts its going-out (rather than seeking premature return/ratzo before the shov is complete), the divine light is "delivered into the hand" — it becomes accessible, graspable, at the level of Malchut/Asiyah. This is the maamar's chiddush: divine presence in the lower world (dira betachtonim) requires the soul's complete willingness to be in the lower world.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Ki Tetze occupies a critical position in the autumn-parasha arc: where Re'eh established the soul's capacity for direct perception (yechida-seeing) and Shoftim established the governance of the body's gates, Ki Tetze now confronts the hardest question: what happens when the soul is fully embedded in the conflict of the lower world, beyond the clean architecture of gates and judges? The hemshech answers by making the descent itself the engine of ascent: Or Chozer is generated precisely by the soul's willingness to go out fully. This prepares the ground for Ki Tavo's image of "arrival" — the moment when the soul that has fully gone out finally arrives at its destination — and Nitzavim's paradox of "standing firm" despite the diversity of soul-levels.
