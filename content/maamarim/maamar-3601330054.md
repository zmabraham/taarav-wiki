## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 2nd day of Sukkot 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds the fundamental distinction within *Or Makif* (surrounding/encompassing light) between two categorically different modes: the *Or Makif* that is surrounding only from the *perspective of the receivers* (i.e., they cannot absorb it, but it is in principle relatable to them) and the *Or Makif* that is surrounding *in essence* (it is intrinsically beyond any reception by any created vessel, now or ever). This distinction is developed through the anatomy of Keter: *Gulgalta* (the skull/cranium of Arich Anpin) is the site of Or Makif in the first mode; *Moha sheb'Keter (Mo"s)* (the brain within the skull, the inner dimension of Keter) is the site of Or Makif in the second mode. The sukkah-tree paradox is the entry point.

### The Opening Move

The halakhic rule states: "One who makes his sukkah under a tree is as if he built it inside the house" — meaning, it is invalid. The puzzle is intense: the *sechach* (sukkah-covering) of a sukkah is itself understood as *Or Makif* (encompassing light), and the Midrash explicitly derives the mitzvah of sukkah from Abraham's invitation to "rest under the tree" at Mamre. God says to Abraham: "Because you said 'rest under the tree,' I will repay your descendants — in the desert, I spread the cloud-covering; in the settled land, they will dwell in sukkot; and in the future, 'a sukkah shall provide shade by day'." If the sukkah is the payback for the tree-resting, why is a sukkah *under a tree* invalid?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The two types of Or Makif established**: The maamar establishes that both a tree and a sukkah represent *Or Makif*, but they are not the same type. The tree that Abraham offered his guests (*hashean tachat ha'etz*) is a "lower" Or Makif — one that wraps around the receiver from above but in a mode that is still *relatable* to the receiver's level. The sukkah's *sechach* is a "higher" Or Makif — one that carries a more transcendent quality of encompassing. When the sukkah-Or-Makif is subordinated to the tree-Or-Makif (sukkah built beneath the tree), the higher is obscured by the lower, and the mitzvah is negated.

- **Gulgalta vs. Mo"s**: This maps precisely onto the anatomy of Keter. *Gulgalta* (the outer cranium of Arich) is the outer Keter — *Or Makif* in the mode of relating-but-not-penetrating: the light of Atik is present in Gulgalta only as an encompassing presence that does not enter the vessel. *Mo"s* (Moha sheb'Keter, the inner brain of Keter) is the inner Keter — *Or Makif* in the mode of intrinsic concealment, where the *penimiyut* of Atik is embedded. The distinction between the two is not quantitative (more or less light) but qualitative (the type of Or Makif and the nature of the concealment).

- **Or Makif of Or Yashar vs. Or Makif of Or Chozer**: The maamar introduces a further refinement: Gulgalta's Or Makif is characterized as the encompassing light of Or Yashar — it descends from above and surrounds from outside. Mo"s's Or Makif, by contrast, is characterized as the encompassing light of Or Chozer — it is the hidden interior that can only be reached by an ascending movement from below that penetrates the outer shell. This links the inner anatomy of Keter to the hemshech's central Or Yashar/Or Chozer dynamic.

- **The Midrash's three historical sukkot**: The Midrash's three moments of divine covering — cloud in the desert, sukkot in the settled land, and the future eschatological shade — correspond to three levels of Or Makif. The cloud in the desert was a mode of Or Makif from Gulgalta, encompassing and protecting but external. The sukkot in the settled land draws from Mo"s, an Or Makif of inner essence. The future "sukkah for shade by day" (Isaiah 4:6) represents a fully manifested penimiyut Keter — the Radla-level of Or Makif revealed without any concealment.

- **Inner vs. outer dimensions of teshuvah and joy**: The maamar connects these two Or Makif types to two modes of divine service: the *teshuvah* of Yom Kippur (which just concluded) accesses Mo"s/inner Or Makif; the *simchah* (joy) of Sukkot accesses Gulgalta/outer Or Makif. The sequence is significant: inner depth (Yom Kippur) precedes and enables the outer joy (Sukkot). Sukkot's joy is the outer radiance of what was accessed inwardly on Yom Kippur.

### What It Gets To

The maamar arrives at a complete account of why the tree invalidates the sukkah: the tree represents the external Or Makif (Gulgalta, Or Yashar mode), and when it is superimposed on the sukkah's Or Makif, the deeper, interior Or Makif (Mo"s, Or Chozer mode) is blocked. The mitzvah of sukkah requires the interior Or Makif to be operative — which it can only be if the sukkah's sechach has direct, unobstructed access to the sky, i.e., to the source of the encompassing light from above without any intermediate screen.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar is one of the hemshech's most technically precise treatments of the Or Makif typology. It advances the hemshech's anatomy of Keter by distinguishing its two major sub-structures (Gulgalta and Mo"s) in terms of their Or Makif modes, and it demonstrates that this distinction is not merely theoretical but is encoded in the halakhah of sukkah. The previous Shabbat Teshuva maamar identified Mo"s as the site of teshuvah's Or Chozer; this maamar shows that the festival of Sukkot continues to engage Mo"s, now through the modality of encompassing joy rather than penetrating return.
