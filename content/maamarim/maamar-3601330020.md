## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Nitzavim 1901-02** in the year **תער"ב** (1901–02), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

The specific question focuses on understanding how the language used points to a deeper Kabbalistic reality within the hemshech's ongoing analysis.

**Central Concept**: This maamar continues the hemshech's development of the relationship between Or Yashar (direct divine light) and Or Chozer (reflected light), applying it to the specific occasion of Parshat Nitzavim 1901-02. The Rashab develops the Kabbalistic framework of Sefirot and Partzufim to illuminate the Torah's discussion of the relevant theme.

**Resolution**: The chiddush reached in this maamar shows how the specific quality of divine service associated with ש"פ נצבים, תער"ב corresponds to a specific aspect of the Or Chozer dynamic — revealing how the human soul's action in the lowest world (Asiyah) draws divine light from the highest Keter-level, thereby advancing the hemshech's central theme of divine habitation in the lower realms (dira betachtonim).

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar constructs the theory of two levels of *ratzon* (will) — the manifest will (*ratzon ha-galui*) and the concealed will (*ratzon ha-ne'elam*, or *koach ha-ratzon*) — and maps these onto two types of Or Mekif: a general encompassing light (mekif ha-klali, the *moha de-setima* of Adam Kadmon) and a particular encompassing light (mekif ha-perati, the Keter of Atzilut). The verse *"Atem nitzavim hayom kulakhem"* becomes the vehicle for this bipartite analysis, with Rosh Hashana as the ritual context in which both levels become simultaneously accessible.

### The Opening Move

The opening verse — *"You are all standing today before the Lord your God"* (Deut. 29:9) — is introduced with the well-known homiletical gloss that wherever the Torah says *hayom* ("today"), it refers to Rosh Hashana. The difficulty the Rashab presses is two-fold: (1) why does the verse use the reflexive *nitzavim* (the Niphal form, "being stood," suggesting the people are *acted upon* from above) rather than the active *omdim* ("standing")? And (2) why does the verse detail all the sub-categories of Israel (*"your heads, your tribes, your elders…"*) immediately after saying *kulakhem* ("all of you") — what does the specification add to the universal?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Two categories of Or Mekif**: The maamar begins by recapping the distinction established in maamar 18 between the general Or Mekif (*moha de-setima* of Adam Kadmon) and the particular Or Mekif (Keter of Atzilut). The general Or Mekif encompasses all worlds equally (*be-hashva'ah*) because it precedes any differentiation — in it all worlds exist without distinction. The particular Or Mekif, Keter of Atzilut, is the site where the divine will becomes particularized enough to generate the specific existence of each world as its own world.

- **Why a particular will is needed**: Even though the general Or Mekif contains all worlds, it cannot *by itself* produce the actual differentiated existence of any particular world — because in the general Or Mekif all worlds exist without distinction (*be-lo hitkhalelut klal*). A *ratzon perati* (particular will) is required for each world to have its own specific existence. This is why Keter of Atzilut exists as a distinct level: it is the site of the divine particular will for the world of Atzilut specifically.

- **Two kinds of ratzon**: The core conceptual innovation introduced here is the distinction between two types of will. The first is the *ratzon galui* — the manifest will, where the soul actually inclines (*ha'ata'ah ve-hamshakhah*) toward a desired object with genuine longing and orientation. The second is the *ratzon ne'elam* (or *koach ha-ratzon*) — the concealed will, which is not yet an inclination toward anything but rather the settled internal resolution (*hiském ve-hahlata*) that it would be fitting to will a certain thing. This concealed will is not "desire" in any usual sense; it is pre-desire, the condition of the possibility of desire.

- **Analogy of father and son**: The source text provides the anchor analogy of the *ratzon atzmi* — the essential will of a father toward his son (or a son toward his father). This is not desire *for* something but an elemental drawing that derives purely from their essential mutual identity (*mitziut ha-atzem*). It operates without any *kavvanah* (intention) or *mevukash* (sought-after goal). This essential will is the closest human analogue to how God wills Israel — *"raq ba-avotekha hashak Hashem"* — not because of Israel's merits but because Israel is rooted in God's very essence.

- **Nitzavim and the two levels**: The word *nitzavim* (passive-reflexive: "being stood") indicates that on Rosh Hashana all of Israel receives from the Or Mekif ha-klali — the general encompassing light — and is "stood up" (*me'umdim mi-le-ma'alah*) in a mode that transcends their own initiative. The listing of all sub-levels (*"your heads, your tribes…"*) then maps onto the Or Mekif ha-perati: each sub-category of Israel corresponds to a specific level within the world of Atzilut that receives a particularized divine will. The universal and the particular are thus two simultaneous modes of Rosh Hashana's revelation.

- **Dira betachtonim grounded in ratzon atzmi**: The maamar concludes by grounding the desire for *dira betachtonim* in the *ratzon atzmi* — the essential, motiveless will. The Midrashic statement *"nitkaveh ha-Kadosh Barukh Hu lihyot lo dirah ba-takhtonim"* is read as expressing not a functional desire (God wanting the *result* of divine service) but an essential one: the will for a lower-world dwelling is of the same order as the father's essential will for his son — without reason, without goal, purely from the essential identity between God and Israel.

### What It Gets To

The maamar has established that the Rosh Hashana experience operates on two simultaneous levels: (a) the collective *nitzavim* — Israel being "stood" by the general Or Mekif, transcending all internal differentiation; and (b) the detailed enumeration of levels — Israel receiving the particular Or Mekif of each sub-level of Atzilut, enabling each distinct spiritual capacity to be renewed from its own particular divine root. The deepest conclusion is that God's desire for *dira betachtonim* is not a functional preference but an expression of essential (*atzmi*) will — and this essential will is what the collective Or Chozer of Rosh Hashana prayer actually touches.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

Maamarim 18–19 established the distinction between Or Mekif and Or Pnimi and their relationship through Or Chozer. This maamar now makes the decisive analytical step of differentiating *within* Or Mekif — introducing the critical pair of mekif ha-klali and mekif ha-perati. This bipartite structure will be the scaffold for the hemshech's subsequent analysis of how different levels of the soul (Nefesh through Yechida) correspond to different levels of encompassing light, and why Yechida — the highest soul-level, corresponding to the general Or Mekif — is the locus of the soul's essential bond with *Atzmus Ein Sof*.
