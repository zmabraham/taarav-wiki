## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Vayeshev, 20 Kislev 1902-03** in the year **תער"ג** (1902–03), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar — delivered on 20 Kislev, the anniversary of the Alter Rebbe's liberation from czarist imprisonment in 5559 (1798) — uses the occasion of גאולת הרב הזקן (the Alter Rebbe's redemption) to develop a precise account of how Or Yashar and Or Chozer interact within the structure of the Sefirot, grounding the hemshech's abstract light-mechanics in the redemptive significance of pnimiyut ha-Torah itself.

### The Opening Move
The maamar opens with the dual occasion: Parshat Vayeshev and 20 Kislev. Vayeshev presents Yosef's descent into Egypt — the paradigmatic narrative of descent for the sake of ultimate elevation. This is juxtaposed with the Alter Rebbe's liberation, which the Rashab treats not as a historical event alone but as a cosmic moment in which pnimiyut ha-Torah (the inner dimension of Torah) was vindicated and its transmission secured. The entry point matters because the question of whether pnimiyut ha-Torah has a legitimate place in Jewish life is precisely a question about whether Or Chozer — the light that ascends from below — can reach and draw down from the highest levels.

### The Argumentative Arc
- The descent of Yosef into Egypt is mapped onto the structure of Or Yashar: divine light descending into constricted, even hostile, circumstances. The degradation is not accidental but purposive — the lowest point is the hinge of the highest elevation.
- The Alter Rebbe's imprisonment is interpreted as a parallel cosmic descent: the forces of klipah (the opposing forces arrayed against the spread of Chassidus) sought to suppress the Or Chozer that pnimiyut ha-Torah generates. The liberation is thus the victory of Or Chozer over the forces that block its ascent.
- The hemshech's earlier analysis of Tzimtzum and the Kav (the "thread" of light that enters the vacated space) is here applied to show that pnimiyut ha-Torah occupies precisely the position of the Kav — a measured, bounded channel through which infinite light penetrates finite reality.
- The practical implication: one who learns pnimiyut ha-Torah generates an Or Chozer that ascends through all the worlds (Asiyah, Yetzirah, Beriyah, Atzilut) and draws additional Or Yashar downward — a reciprocal intensification that is the mechanism of the divine dwelling below (dira betachtonim).
- The Sefirah of Keter is identified as the ultimate destination of Or Chozer and the ultimate source of the renewed Or Yashar — Keter being the juncture between the world-system and Ein Sof itself.

### What It Gets To
The maamar establishes that the liberation of the Alter Rebbe was not merely a historical redemption but a redemption of Or Chozer itself — proof that the inner Torah's light can and does ascend to the highest reaches of Keter. This provides the hemshech with a concrete historical-theological anchor for its abstract claim that human engagement with divine wisdom generates an upward movement of light that transforms the structure of the worlds.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
Coming after earlier maamarim that established the theoretical mechanics of Or Yashar and Or Chozer, this maamar supplies the first major historical-redemptive illustration of those mechanics, demonstrating that the hemshech's light-theory has real events as its referents and that the Chabad transmission of pnimiyut ha-Torah is itself a cosmic act with measurable consequences within the divine structure.
