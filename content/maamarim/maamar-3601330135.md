## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 2nd day of Rosh Hashana 1905-06** in the year **עתר"ו** (1905–06), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar — delivered on the night of the second day of Rosh Hashana 5666 (1905-06) — builds an account of the metaphysical structure of Rosh Hashana by resolving the apparent contradiction between two liturgical claims: that Rosh Hashana is "the beginning of Your works" (techilat ma'asecha) and simultaneously only a "remembrance of the first day" (zikaron l'yom rishon). The resolution introduces a crucial distinction between two forms of divine da'at (knowledge) — Da'at Elyon (supernal knowledge) and Da'at Tachton (lower knowledge) — and then explains why the shofar is required in addition to verbal declaration of Malkhuyot.

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with the piyut from the Rosh Hashana liturgy: "Zeh hayom techilat ma'asecha, zikaron l'yom rishon — This day is the beginning of Your works, a remembrance of the first day." The tension is precise: if Rosh Hashana is merely a remembrance (zikaron) of the first day of Creation (which was 25 Elul, not Rosh Hashana — Rosh Hashana being the sixth day, when Adam was created), how can it also be the actual "beginning of Your works"? And conversely, if it truly is a renewed beginning each year ("ha'yom harat olam — today the world is conceived"), why does it need to be framed as mere memory? A second question, from the Gemara (Rosh Hashana 16a): "Imru l'fanai Malkhuyot k'dei shetamlichuni aleichem — Recite before Me kingship-verses so that you crown Me over you... and with what? With the shofar." If the verbal recitation of Malkhuyot verses is what draws down divine kingship, why is the shofar additionally required?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Two forms of Da'at — the Zohar's framework**: The maamar opens its analysis via the Zohar Chadash (Idra Zuta 291a): "Da'at vadai hu ha'da'at b'da'at kol paltaroi itmalin — Da'at is certainly the Da'at through which all the divine chambers are filled" — and cites the verse "B'da'at chadrim yimale'un — Through knowledge the chambers are filled" (Prov. 24:4), where "chadar" (chamber) is an acronym for Chesed-Din-Rachamim. The two forms of Da'at — Da'at Elyon (supernal knowledge, the knowledge from the perspective of Ein Sof that transcends all worlds) and Da'at Tachton (lower knowledge, the knowledge that establishes and governs the world's reality) — correspond to two ways in which God "knows" creation and creation "knows" God.
- **Da'at Elyon and the "techilat ma'asecha"**: Da'at Elyon is the divine perspective from which the entire process of creation is held as a single undifferentiated intention. From this vantage, every Rosh Hashana is genuinely "the beginning" — not a repetition or a memory, but a fresh actualization of the primordial creative impulse. The hemshech develops this by connecting Da'at Elyon to the Kabbalistic concept of the "ratzon harishon" (the first, essential will) that precedes all temporal sequence. Rosh Hashana as "techilat ma'asecha" is thus Rosh Hashana as the annual re-emergence of this primordial will into active creation.
- **Da'at Tachton and "zikaron l'yom rishon"**: Da'at Tachton is the knowledge-from-below, the human soul's cognitive relationship with the divine that operates within the framework of temporal sequence and historical memory. From this perspective, Rosh Hashana is indeed "a remembrance" — it recapitulates the first day of Creation's intention (Adam's creation, his kingship over creation, his standing before God in the Garden) rather than being that day itself. The human liturgical act of Malkhuyot-recitation operates at this level: it is a da'at tachton act — a cognitive recognition, a remembrance, a verbal ordering of the soul's relationship to divine kingship.
- **Why the shofar is needed**: The shofar transcends the faculty of da'at altogether. The maamar argues that verbal recitation (Malkhuyot) draws down divine kingship through the medium of da'at tachton — the human intellect's structured acknowledgment of divine sovereignty. But this only generates Or Chozer from the level of da'at (which is itself a limited, conceptually mediated form of ascent). The shofar operates below and above da'at simultaneously: its sound bypasses conceptual mediation entirely and activates the neshamah's pnimiyut — specifically, the faculty of ratzon (will) and oneg (delight) that precedes all intellectual formulation. The shofar's tekiah corresponds to Or Yashar's primordial descent (the single unbroken tone of divine will before creation), shevarim to the breaking/tzimtzum, teruah to the fragmented post-tzimtzum state of sparks, and the final tekiah gedola to the Or Chozer that re-integrates all fragments into divine unity.
- **The ontological structure of created non-being (Yesh min Ayin)**: The continuation of the source text (at the level of collected sections) enters into a profound analysis of the ontological status of created beings: created beings are "yesh" (something) in their own experience, but their yesh is ontologically unfounded — they can be negated (and will be, in cosmic terms), and therefore in the deepest sense they are "ayin" (nothing) even while they appear to be yesh. This is the Rashab's engagement with the question: what is the ontological foundation of created reality? The answer is that created reality is founded not in itself but in the divine "Ayin ha'amiti" — the true nothingness that is Ein Sof's essential self-sufficiency — which alone has genuine being (Yesh ha'amiti). Rosh Hashana is the day when this truth is made experientially accessible to created beings through the shofar's sound.

### What It Gets To

The maamar resolves its dual opening problem: (1) "Techilat ma'asecha" refers to Da'at Elyon's perspective — each year Rosh Hashana is genuinely a new beginning because Da'at Elyon holds creation in an eternal present of divine will; "zikaron l'yom rishon" refers to Da'at Tachton's perspective — the human soul's cognitive act of remembrance that reconnects it with the primordial creative impulse. Both are simultaneously true because both forms of da'at are simultaneously active on Rosh Hashana. (2) The shofar is needed because Malkhuyot-recitation (Da'at Tachton) alone does not reach the level of the "techilat ma'asecha" — it accesses divine kingship only at the level of its governance-manifestation (Malchut as structure), not at the level of its primordial will-foundation (Keter as ratzon). The shofar sound activates Da'at Elyon in the human soul, drawing down the Or Yashar of primordial divine will and generating the Or Chozer of genuine teshuvah (return to the source).

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar opens the 1905-06 (5666) section of Part 1 of Hemshech Taarav and establishes the Rosh Hashana framework that will govern the subsequent maamarim through the High Holiday season and into the winter Torah-portion cycle. The two-Da'at structure (Elyon/Tachton) is foundational to the hemshech's account of how the soul's avodah connects cosmic metaphysics (the sefirot, Or Yashar/Or Chozer) to human religious experience. It also introduces the distinction between verbal-intellectual avodah (Malkhuyot) and trans-intellectual avodah (shofar) that will recur throughout the hemshech's analysis of teshuva, joy (simcha), and the soul's yechida-faculty. The maamar's ontological digression on Yesh min Ayin and the non-being of created reality prepares the ground for the Sukkot maamarim' exploration of Da'at Elyon/Tachton in the context of the four species and the singular intimacy of Shemini Atzeret.
