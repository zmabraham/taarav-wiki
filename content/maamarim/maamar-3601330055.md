## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Shabbat Chol HaMoed Sukkot 1903-04** in the year **תרד"ע** (1903–04), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar builds a comprehensive account of the distinction between *korbanot* (animal offerings) and *ketoret* (incense) as corresponding to two different modes of divine service — and therefore two different divine responses — by way of the two altars (outer and inner) and their psychological analogs (external and internal dimensions of the heart). The deeper construct is the anatomy of *birur* (refinement/clarification) at two levels: the *birur* of *or pnimi* (inner light) through emotional-intellectual service, and the *birur* of *or makif* (encompassing light) through the incense service that corresponds to *ratzoh v'dauv* (will and will-beyond-will).

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens with Psalm 117's peculiar logic: "Praise YHVH, all nations (*kol goyim*)... for His kindness has overwhelmed us (*ki gavar aleinu chasdo*)." Why should the nations praise God because of kindness shown to Israel? Their own interest in God's goodness toward Israel would seem at best peripheral. The Rashab reads this as pointing to a hidden dynamic: God's *chesed* toward Israel is not merely a transaction between God and Israel but a cosmological event whose benefits overflow to all seventy nations. The Sukkot offering of 70 bulls — one for each of the 70 nations — is the structural link: Sukkot's service encompasses all of humanity.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Two altars, two modes of service**: The outer altar (mizbeach hachitzon) in the courtyard corresponds to the *chitzoniut halev* (the outer dimension of the heart) — the love of God generated through intellectual apprehension and deliberate reflection (*avodah she'al pi ta'am v'daat*). The inner altar (mizbeach hapnimi, the golden altar of incense) corresponds to *penimiyut halev* — the inner, spontaneous, pre-intellectual dimension of the heart that responds directly to the divine *otherness* without mediation of understood ideas.

- **Korban as the inner light's birur**: Animal offerings achieve the *birur* of *or pnimi* of *kelipat nogah*: through the burning and transformation of the animal's physical substance on the altar, the inner sparks of holiness embedded in the created world's matter are extracted and elevated. This is the *avodah* (service) of reason-based love — it separates holy from profane within the bounded realm of physical existence.

- **Ketoret as the encompassing light's birur**: Incense achieves the *birur* of the *or makif* of *kelipat nogah* — the dimension that is not contained within the created world at all but hovers around it as an encompassing presence. The eleven spices of the incense (*achad asar sammanei haketoret*) correspond to the eleven *kittrin d'meisavuta* (crowns of impurity) — the spiritual counterpart of the ten holy sefirot plus their systemic "excess" (the eleventh crown that represents the fundamental disjunction of the sitra achra). Incense nullifies this outer encompassing layer of impurity.

- **Why incense is higher than korban**: The Zohar states explicitly that incense is more precious to God than any other service (*chavivu almai da'ketoret*), even more than prayer. The reason is now Kabbalistically grounded: prayer corrects the *or pnimi* dimension of the soul's relationship with God; incense corrects the *or makif* dimension — the encompassing layer of connection that is too exalted to enter directly into the soul's conscious life. The incense on Yom Kippur, before the ark in the Holy of Holies, represents the maximum penetration of the Or Chozer into the innermost sanctum of divine being.

- **The 70 bulls and universal encompassing**: The 70 bulls offered over the seven days of Sukkot, corresponding to the 70 nations, are the *korban*-level service directed at the encompassing divine light that covers all of humanity. Even the nations' praise of God (*hallelu et YHVH kol goyim*) becomes comprehensible in this framework: when God's chesed overcomes Israel, it is an event in the *or makif* dimension — a divine encompassing that extends beyond the domain of Israel's particular relationship with God and spills over to universal significance.

- **Inner and outer Or Makif distinguished**: The maamar refines the Or Makif typology further: the outer Or Makif (Gulgalta/korban level) is "surrounding only relative to the receivers" — it could in principle be absorbed if the vessels were larger; the inner Or Makif (Mo"s/ketoret level) is "surrounding in essence" — it is intrinsically beyond any vessel's capacity for absorption and can only be accessed through the Or Chozer of teshuvah and the ketoret service.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that Sukkot's unique liturgical achievement is the simultaneous activation of both Or Makif levels: the 70 bulls activate the outer Or Makif (encompassing light for the nations), while the incense service activates the inner Or Makif (encompassing light for the domain of holiness itself). This is why Sukkot is called *zman simchateinu* — the time of our joy: the joy of Sukkot is the experience of the Or Makif in both its modes surrounding and penetrating simultaneously, which is the experiential analog of the divine encompassing of creation that the festival encodes cosmologically.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar develops the most detailed treatment thus far of *korban* vs. *ketoret* as a structural distinction — one that will recur throughout the hemshech. It also extends the Or Makif typology established in the prior Sukkot maamar (Gulgalta vs. Mo"s) to the plane of *avodah* (divine service), showing how each type of Or Makif corresponds to a specific mode of human approach to God. The 70-nations dimension opens the hemshech to its universal scope: the project of *dira betachtonim* is not limited to Israel's private sanctification but aims at the transformation of all of created reality.
