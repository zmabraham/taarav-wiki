## Context

This maamar was delivered during **2nd day of Rosh Hashana 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: On Rosh Hashanah (2nd day of Rosh Hashana 1909-10), the Rashab addresses the tension between divine kingship (מלכות, Malchut) and divine transcendence: if God is truly infinite and beyond all relationship with creation, how can there be a meaningful 'coronation' (הכתרה) of God as king? What does it mean to declare 'the Lord reigns' when God transcends all categories of governance?

**Central Concept**: The distinction between God's essential being (עצמות, Atzmus) and God's revelation as king (מלכות, Malchut) — Malchut is the Sefirah through which the infinite becomes relationally accessible to created beings without compromising the divine transcendence. The Rashab develops how the concept of 'king' points precisely to the aspect of divinity that interfaces with those it governs.

**Resolution**: The 'coronation' of Rosh Hashanah refers not to a change in divine being but to a heightened revelation of Malchut — the dimension of divine kingship that is always present but becomes more accessible to finite perception on this day. The Or Chozer of the collective soul's prayer on Rosh Hashanah uniquely draws down this revelation.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built
This maamar, delivered on the day of the second day of Rosh Hashanah, continues directly from the night discourse (maamar 77), now deepening the analysis of Malchut's inner and outer faces and developing the mechanism by which the soul's Rosh Hashanah prayer achieves what regular prayer throughout the year cannot: direct access to the dimension of divine being that transcends all differentiated governance.

### The Opening Move
The maamar picks up the paradox of divine kingship from maamar 77 but approaches it from the daytime perspective — the moment of actual proclamation in the Musaf Amidah (the Mussaf prayer of Rosh Hashanah with its Malchuyot, Zichronot, and Shofarot). The central question intensifies: the Musaf prayer declares "מלוך על כל העולם כלו בכבודך" — "reign over the entire world in Your glory." This implies that divine kingship is not yet fully actualized in creation, that there is a "not yet" to divine governance that the prayer seeks to actualize. But if God is already infinite and governs all, what remains to be actualized?

### The Argumentative Arc
- **The "not yet" of Malchut.** The Rashab develops the crucial distinction between Malchut as it exists in Atzilut (pure potential for governance, not yet active in the lower worlds) and Malchut as it descends into Beriyah, Yetzirah, and Asiyah (actual governance, but now increasingly attenuated and concealed). In Atzilut, Malchut is "royal potential" — like a king who has not yet addressed his subjects. In the lower worlds, governance is active but the King's inner face is hidden. The prayer seeks a revelation of inner Malchut even within the lower worlds.
- **The שם-dimension at its most interior.** Extending the analysis of שם from maamar 76: when the prayer proclaims "ה' מלך," it is not asserting a fact about the King's external rule but invoking the Name from its innermost dimension — the Name as it exists within the royal essence before it projects outward. This innermost Name-dimension is normally inaccessible to created beings; Rosh Hashanah's intensity of collective prayer creates the Or Chozer that reaches it.
- **Why the second day is qualitatively distinct from the first.** The Rashab elaborates that the first day of Rosh Hashanah primarily concerns the outer dimension of Malchut (חיצוניות המל') — the aspect facing the created worlds and ordering their existence. The second day reaches the inner dimension (פנימיות המל') — the aspect of Malchut that remains within Atzilut and is continuous with Keter. Hence the discourses of the second night and day address correspondingly deeper strata of the divine-soul relationship.
- **The Shofar as Or Chozer mechanism.** The Rashab gives the Shofar blast its Kabbalistic function: the Shofar's sound, rising from the chest (a compressed, squeezed cry — "צעקה פשוטה מעומקא דלבא"), bypasses the differentiated structure of articulated speech (which is always already channeled through the five places of articulation). It is an undivided sound — קלא פשוטה — and as such it is the acoustic analog of the Or Chozer ascending without differentiation from the lowest point to the highest source. The Shofar does not "speak" specific content; it carries the totality of the soul's relation to its source.
- **The practical implication: Atzmus in the lower worlds.** The culmination of the Musaf prayer — "and He shall reign over all the world in His glory, as it is written: God shall be King over all the earth" — expresses the hemshech's central goal: not merely governance of the lower worlds through attenuated intermediaries, but the actual disclosure of divine essence (עצמות) within the material realm. This is the dira betachtonim at its most complete: Atzmus dwelling below.

### What It Gets To
The maamar establishes that the second day of Rosh Hashanah's divine service reaches the innermost face of Malchut — the dimension of divine kingship that is still within Atzilut, continuous with Keter, and not yet projected into governance of the lower worlds. The Shofar's Or Chozer and the collective soul's prayer together create the conditions for a disclosure of this interior Malchut into the very realm of Asiyah, fulfilling, at least momentarily, the hemshech's central aspiration.

### How This Fits the Hemshech
This maamar completes the Rosh Hashanah sub-unit of Part 2, having analyzed the two days as a progression from outer to inner Malchut. The hemshech will continue into Shabbat Teshuva, Haazinu, and Sukkot, each applying these same structural concepts to a different moment of the Tishrei season. The Rashab is building a complete phenomenology of the High Holy Day period as a systematic engagement with the deepest levels of the divine-cosmic structure.
