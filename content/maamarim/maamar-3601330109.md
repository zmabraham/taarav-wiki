## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 2nd day of Passover 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

This maamar — delivered at the seder table on the second night of Pesach — constructs one of the hemshech's most penetrating analyses of how divine revelation (geula/redemption) is possible in a world created by tzimtzum. The central construct is the relationship between the two modes of divine light: the Mimaleh Kol Almin (Or Yashar, which fills all worlds in a measured, graduated manner) and the Sovev Kol Almin (the Encompassing light, which transcends all worlds and is not subject to tzimtzum). The matzah, eaten because there was "no time to leaven," becomes the vehicle for demonstrating that the highest divine revelation operates precisely through the removal of time and process — through what transcends the graduated chain (hishtalshelut).

### The Opening Move

The Haggadah's explanation of why we eat matzah is paradoxical: "matzah zo she'anu okhlim — al shem mah? al shem shelo hispik bitzkatam shel avoteinu lehachmitz ad shenigla aleihem melech malkhei hamelakhim." We eat this matzah because our ancestors' dough did not have time to leaven before G-d redeemed them. The Rashab identifies multiple difficulties with this: (1) matzah is a mitzvah commanded before the exodus, so its reason cannot be purely historical contingency — even if the dough had leavened, the obligation would stand; (2) the timing is off: the obligation to eat matzah falls before midnight (with the Paschal lamb), while the redemption (the exodus) happened after midnight; (3) why does the Haggadah add "ki gorshу mi'Mitzrayim" (because they were expelled from Egypt) as if the expulsion and the non-leavening are causally linked? These difficulties force a deeper reading of what the matzah's non-leavening actually signifies.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The prior maamar's foundation — Oved Hashem draws down Shem Havaya**: The Rashab recalls the analysis from the previous maamar in the hemshech (d"h "Tzav et Aharon"): the purpose of divine service (avodah) is to draw down the revelation of Shem Havaya in the world, since Havaya (the four-letter Name) is the Name that transcends time — Haya, Hoveh, Yihyeh simultaneously (was, is, will be — all at once). The world, by contrast, is constituted by the sequential nature of time and by spatial limitation. Tzimtzum created both: the withdrawal of Or Ein Sof created the conditions for time (sequence) and space (distinction).
- **The tzimtzum as the source of chametz**: Chametz (leavened bread) is dough that has been given time to rise — its leavening is literally the product of time working on matter. In the hemshech's framework, chametz is the emblem of the world as constituted by the tzimtzum-governed chain of hishtalshelut: a world in which everything takes time, develops through graduated process, and is shaped by temporal sequence. Matzah — which cannot rise because it is not given time — is thus the emblem of what transcends the tzimtzum's time-structure.
- **Two modes of divine light and why only one can redeem**: The Or that flows through the hishtalshelut (Mimaleh Kol Almin) is bound by the time-structure that tzimtzum created — it descends from world to world, Sefirah to Sefirah, moment to moment. This Or cannot redeem, because redemption (the exit from Mitzrayim — literally "the straits," the constrictive boundaries) requires an Or that is not itself subject to the same constrictions. The redemption required the Sovev Kol Almin — the Or that encompasses all worlds without being limited by any of them, that stands in relation to time as simultaneously past, present, and future.
- **Why the dough had no time to leaven — the Sovev's transcendence of hishtalshelut**: "Ki gorshу" — they were expelled with such speed that there was no time for the dough to leaven. The speed (chipazon — haste) of the exodus is not an accidental feature but an essential characteristic of the Sovev Kol Almin's action in the world: because the Sovev does not operate through the graduated sequence of hishtalshelut, when it acts, it acts instantaneously, without the "time" that the hishtalshelut-governed Or requires. The matzah's non-leavening is thus the physical trace of the Sovev's action — the absence of process in matter mirrors the transcendence of process in the divine light that caused it.
- **Why before midnight, yet the redemption was after midnight**: The matzah is eaten before midnight; the exodus was at midnight and after. The Rashab resolves this by distinguishing two stages of the Sovev's action: the preparation (before midnight) in which the Sovev's light enters the Israelite camp and is "received" through the matzah-eating — this is the Or Chozer moment, the elevation of matter to receive the Encompassing light — and the redemption itself (at midnight) which is the Or Yashar of the Sovev breaking through into the world. The eating of matzah before midnight is the human preparation (Or Chozer) that draws down the Sovev's light; the exodus after midnight is the Sovev's own initiative (Or Yashar) breaking the Egyptian-tzimtzum.
- **The critical innovation — the Sovev is not blocked by tzimtzum**: The key Kabbalistic claim of the maamar is this: the tzimtzum that conceals the Or Ein Sof from the world conceals only the Mimaleh dimension — the Or that was destined to flow through the worlds in graduated form. But the Sovev Kol Almin was never "inside" the created space to begin with; it encompasses rather than fills, so the tzimtzum's contraction of the inner space did not diminish it at all. The Ein Sof after tzimtzum is present everywhere equally — in the lowest material world no less than in the highest spiritual realm — because it is the Sovev, not the Mimaleh, that constitutes its constant presence.

### What It Gets To

The maamar arrives at the conclusion that every genuine redemption — historical, personal, cosmic — operates through the Sovev Kol Almin's irruption into the tzimtzum-governed world, and that matzah eating is the human practice most precisely aligned with this irruption. The matzah, eaten before the process of time can act on it, aligns the human body with the supra-time dimension of the Sovev. This is why the mitzvah of matzah was commanded before the exodus: not as a commemoration of what happened but as the preparatory act that draws down the light that makes the exodus possible.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar is one of the hemshech's most significant theological statements. It introduces the Sovev/Mimaleh distinction as the key to understanding how redemption and divine revelation operate differently from ordinary divine sustenance. The prior maamarim had established the Mishkan and the korbanot as the structures through which Or Yashar (Mimaleh) reaches its dwelling below; this Pesach maamar now introduces the Sovev as a category that stands entirely outside that graduated structure — and redemption as the moment when the Sovev breaks through. This opens toward the hemshech's eventual treatment of the Messianic era as the epoch when the Sovev's constant presence will be openly manifest.
