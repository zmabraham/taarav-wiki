## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Parshat Eikev 1909-10** in the year **העת"ר** (1909–10), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק שני (Part 2)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Parshat Eikev contains the command "ועתה ישראל מה ה' אלקיך שואל מעמך כי אם ליראה" (And now Israel, what does God ask of you but to fear Him), the second paragraph of the Shema, and the promise of blessing for observing the commandments. This maamar, coming at the end of the summer-fall sequence of 5670, synthesizes the hemshech's year-long analysis by developing the concept of "eikev" (heel) — the lowest and least conscious part of the body — as a key to understanding how divine service in the lowliest and most mundane modes of existence (corresponding to the "heel" level) actually connects to the highest divine level (Keter/Atzmut).

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens on the name "עקב" (eikev), which literally means "heel" and is also a conditional conjunction ("because/if"). The parasha opens with "And it shall be, if (eikev) you listen to these laws and keep and do them..." — Rashi famously comments that the word "eikev" indicates the commandments that people "tread upon with their heels" — the minor, seemingly insignificant commandments that people treat as unimportant. The Rashab's question: why are these heel-commandments promised the greatest reward? What is the Kabbalistic significance of the "heel" — the lowest, most insensible part of the body — in the structure of the soul and its divine service?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The heel as the lowest soul-level in contact with the ground**: The Rashab develops the anatomical metaphor: the heel is the part of the body that makes direct contact with the ground (the earth — the material world) and bears the body's full weight, yet it has the least sensation of any part of the body. It feels neither pleasure nor pain the way the hand or face does. In the Kabbalistic body-soul correspondence, the heel corresponds to the lowest mode of divine service — those mitzvot that are performed habitually, routinely, without emotional engagement or conscious spiritual intention. They are the "foot-soldiers" of religious life: unglamorous, unsung, but bearing the full weight of the body's physical engagement with the earth.
- **The paradox: lowest sensation, highest root**: The heel's lack of sensation — which appears to be a deficiency — is, in Kabbalistic analysis, a concealment of its highest root. The heel corresponds to the Yechida-level of the soul — the innermost, most essential dimension of the neshamah — precisely because it is so deeply embedded in the material reality that no "ordinary" spiritual sensation can reach it. The heel doesn't "feel" spirituality because it is itself a manifestation of the soul's root above all sensation: the Yechida, which is connected to Keter-Atzmut, is above the differentiated feeling of the lower soul-levels. Its divine service is not felt as spiritual precisely because it operates from the deepest, most essential level.
- **The "trampled commandments" as Keter-connections**: The commandments that people "tread upon with their heels" — treating them as unimportant — are precisely those whose root is in Keter. Keter-level mitzvot have no obvious rational justification (they are in the class of chukim, supra-rational decrees) and produce no felt spiritual experience in their performance. They are "trampled" because they engage the heel-level of the soul — the level that is so close to the divine essence that its performance is effortless and unremarkable. The greatest reward promised for these mitzvot (the eikev reward) corresponds precisely to this: the Keter-level divine will that originates these commandments promises a Keter-level return.
- **The kav and the reshimu — their trace in the heel**: The Rashab develops a refined analysis of the kav (the post-tzimtzum line of divine light) and the reshimu (the impression/residue left in the empty space after the tzimtzum). The reshimu is the trace of the pre-tzimtzum Or Ein Sof that remained after the withdrawal — it is not the light itself but its impression, "like the impression left by the ruler in the wax." In the body-analogy, the reshimu is the heel: the least luminous, most material part that nevertheless carries the deepest impression of its divine source. The heel's service — performed without feeling, by rote, by pure habit — is in fact the "service of the reshimu" — drawing on the deepest embedded trace of the divine in the soul's most material dimension.
- **"And now Israel, what does God ask but fear" — yirah as heel-level service**: The verse's "what does God ask but fear (yirah)" — which the Gemara (Berakhot 33b) elaborates as "everything is in heaven's hands except for the fear of heaven" — is read by the Rashab as pointing to the heel-level of service. "Yirat shamayim" (fear of heaven) in its most basic form is not a felt emotion (that is "ahavah" — love) but a structural orientation of the whole person: the awareness that one stands before the divine in every moment, including in the most mundane and "heel-level" activities. This basic yirah is what God "asks" — it is the ground-level of all service, the heel that must make contact with the earth in order for the whole body to stand upright.
- **The connection to the hemshech's central theme**: The Rashab closes the argument by connecting "eikev" to "dira betachtonim": the divine project of inhabiting the lowest realms is accomplished through the "heel-service" — the mitzvot performed in the most ordinary, material, unremarkable circumstances. It is precisely the heel that makes contact with the "tachtonim" (lowest realms — represented by the earth underfoot) and it is through this contact that the divine presence most deeply inhabits the material world. The "heel-commandments" — those performed mechanically, routinely, without soaring spiritual experience — are the very mechanism by which the infinite divine light is drawn into the most material level of existence.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that "eikev" — the heel — is the key to the entire project of divine service: the commandments performed at the heel-level (mundane, habitual, seemingly unremarkable) are those whose roots are in Keter and Atzmut, and whose performance draws the highest divine light into the deepest material reality. The promise of reward "eikev" (because/if you observe these commandments) is not a conditional promise but an ontological statement: if you engage the heel-level of service — the deepest, most material, most habitual mode of mitzvah-performance — you reach the highest divine level. The last is the first; the heel is the crown.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar serves as the summer-fall capstone of the year 5670. The hemshech has traced the structure of divine light from the highest levels (Keter/Atzmut) downward and has analyzed how Or Chozer operates from human service upward. The Eikev maamar now provides the synthesis: the point where Or Yashar and Or Chozer meet is not at some middle level of spiritual experience but at the very bottom — at the "heel" — where the most material human action performs the most essential divine will. This inverts the intuitive hierarchy of spirituality (where elevated feeling equals elevated connection) and replaces it with the hemshech's characteristic Chassidic insight: essential connection (atzmut) operates through the most essential — and therefore most "ordinary" — modes of divine service. The year 5670 of the hemshech thus ends with a profound statement about where divinity truly dwells: in the heel, in the earth, in the most prosaic and least celebrated of religious acts.
