## Context

This maamar was delivered during **Night of 2nd day of Sukkot 1905-06** in the year **עתר"ו** (1905–06), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Delivered on the night of the second day of Sukkot 5666, this maamar builds the hemshech's account of Da'at Elyon and Da'at Tachton as two forms of "Ayin" (divine nothingness) that correspond to two forms of "Yesh" (being): the divine Yesh ha'amiti (true being, which is God's own self-existence) and the created Yesh hanivra (the derived being of creatures). The four species of Sukkot serve as the organizing frame: the question "why these four species in particular?" becomes an entry point into the deeper question of how different aspects of creation participate differently in the movement from concealment (ayin) to disclosure (yesh).

### The Opening Move

The maamar opens on "Ulekachtem lachem b'yom harishon pri etz hadar — And you shall take for yourselves on the first day a fruit of a splendid tree" (Lev. 23:40), and immediately raises the question: why were precisely these four plant-types (etrog, lulav, myrtle, willow) selected for the mitzva from among the entire plant kingdom? The Midrash Rabbah is immediately cited: "'B'yom harishon' — this is the 15th of Tishrei, but you say 'b'yom harishon'?" — the Midrash is troubled that the Torah calls the 15th of Tishrei "the first day" when the New Year began on the 1st, suggesting that this "first day" signals something more than calendar sequence. The maamar links this to the prior discussions of Da'at Elyon/Tachton from the Rosh Hashana maamarim.

### The Argumentative Arc

- **The two forms of Ayin from the source text**: The Rashab's foundational move, stated explicitly in the source text, is that "the Ayin (divine nothingness) that mediates between two forms of Yesh divides into two." There is Da'at Elyon (upper knowledge/perspective), in which the Ayin is the divine nothingness that is in truth the highest being — "Ayin b'etzem, b'bechina kela — nothingness in essence, in the aspect of total annihilation." This is the Ayin of the Divine Self as it transcends all creation — the perspective from which God's being is the only real being, and created Yesh is "kela" (nothing) by comparison. The Ayin of Da'at Tachton, by contrast, is the nothingness from the created being's perspective: the created Yesh perceives itself as "something" but its Ayin (its non-self-sufficiency, its radical dependence on the divine will for its continued existence) is what defines its ontological status. This is "Ayin metzad ha'Yesh" — the nothingness of the created Yesh, which is not nothingness-in-itself but nothingness-in-relation (nothingness relative to the divine Yesh).
- **The four species as four modes of creation's participation in divine Ayin**: The maamar maps the four species onto this ontological framework. The etrog (citron, which has both taste and fragrance) corresponds to those dimensions of creation in which both the "outer" (ta'am/taste = outer expression) and "inner" (re'ach/fragrance = inner quality, that which cannot be fully contained in external form) are present — this is creation at its most transparent to divine being. The lulav (palm, which has taste but no fragrance) corresponds to dimensions of creation that express divine will externally but without fully revealing the inner divine quality. The myrtle (fragrance but no taste) corresponds to the inverse: inner divine quality present without external expression. The willow (neither taste nor fragrance) corresponds to creation at its most opaque — the level at which divine presence is most concealed.
- **"Yom harishon" as the day of Da'at Elyon's revelation on earth**: The maamar's resolution of the Midrash's puzzle ("why call the 15th of Tishrei 'the first day'?") is that Sukkot's 15th of Tishrei is the first day of the year's Or Yashar fully entering the lower worlds in its post-Yom Kippur, purified form. Yom Kippur (the day before Sukkot begins) establishes the Or Chozer's maximum ascent (to Pnimiyut Atik); Sukkot's 15th of Tishrei is the day when that maximally-elevated Or Chozer now returns as Or Yashar descending back into creation — carrying within it the quality of Da'at Elyon (the Ayin-perspective) that it achieved in its Yom Kippur ascent. This is why it is called "the first day" — it is the first day of the new year's Or Yashar, purified and elevated by the Yom Kippur Or Chozer, now descending as a qualitatively new illumination.
- **The taking of the four species as the integration of all levels of creation**: Taking all four species together — binding the willow's opacity with the etrog's transparency, the myrtle's inner fragrance with the lulav's external expression — is the act of integrating all four levels of creation's participation in divine Ayin into a single unified gesture. This is the hemshech's account of how Sukkot "completes" the Rosh Hashana/Yom Kippur Or Chozer cycle: Rosh Hashana draws the Or Yashar down to the level of Malchut's governance; Yom Kippur's Or Chozer rises to Pnimiyut Atik; Sukkot's four-species integration brings all four levels of created being simultaneously into the circuit of Or Chozer.
- **Atzilut as Da'at Elyon; Biya as Da'at Tachton from the source text**: The maamar makes the important claim (elaborated in the continuation sections) that in the Atzilut-world (the world of divine emanation), the perspective is Da'at Elyon: "Kula kamei k'la chashiv — before God all is as nothing," since Atzilut is divine reality and within it the divine perspective obtains. But the worlds of Beria-Yetzirah-Asiyah operate from Da'at Tachton: they experience themselves as beings, as Yesh entities, whose connection to divine Ayin must be mediated through the structures of Sefirot and hishtalshelut. The Sukkot mitzva of the four species — taken in the world of Asiyah (physical plants, physically grasped) — is the act by which Da'at Tachton (created Yesh) reaches up to touch Da'at Elyon (divine Ayin), through the integration of all four levels of creation.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the four species were chosen because they represent the complete spectrum of creation's modes of relating to divine Ayin: from the willow's opacity (total concealment, Da'at Tachton at its most distant from Da'at Elyon) to the etrog's transparency (relative nearness to Da'at Elyon). Taking all four together is not merely a liturgical act but an ontological statement: that the created order, in all its modes of concealment and revelation, is capable of Or Chozer — of generating an ascending movement that reaches Da'at Elyon. The "first day" (yom harishon) of Sukkot is the day when Or Yashar descends through all four levels simultaneously, and the taking of all four species is the act that receives and integrates this total descent into a unified Or Chozer ascending from the very bottom (willow = Asiyah's densest matter) to the very top (etrog = the level that touches Atzilut's transparency).

### How This Fits the Hemshech

The Leil 2 Sukkot maamar is the hemshech's most explicit articulation of the two-Ayin / two-Yesh framework established in the Rosh Hashana maamarim. Where Rosh Hashana established the framework abstractly (Da'at Elyon as the divine perspective, Da'at Tachton as the creaturely perspective), Sukkot now provides its concretization in the physical world: the four plant-species are the material embodiment of the abstract ontological spectrum from concealment to revelation. This concretization prepares directly for the Shemini Atzeret maamar's account of the singular intimacy of the divine-Israel relationship, which requires first establishing (via Sukkot's four species) that all levels of creation — not merely the spiritual elite — are included in the Or Chozer circuit.
