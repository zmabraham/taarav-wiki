## Context

This maamar was delivered during **2nd day of Rosh Hashana 1905-06** in the year **עתר"ו** (1905–06), as part of the ongoing hemshech (continuous discourse) that Rebbe Shalom DovBer of Lubavitch (the Rashab) delivered over two decades. It forms part of **חלק ראשון (Part 1)** of the hemshech.

The Hemshech Taarav is the longest chassidic discourse ever composed, spanning from 5662 (1901-02) through 5680 (1919-20). Each maamar continues the thread of the previous, building a comprehensive Kabbalistic-Chassidic system centered on the nature of divine light, the structure of the sefirot, and the mission of the Jewish soul.

## Summary

**Opening Question**: On Rosh Hashanah (2nd day of Rosh Hashana 1905-06), the Rashab addresses the tension between divine kingship (מלכות, Malchut) and divine transcendence: if God is truly infinite and beyond all relationship with creation, how can there be a meaningful 'coronation' (הכתרה) of God as king? What does it mean to declare 'the Lord reigns' when God transcends all categories of governance?

**Central Concept**: The distinction between God's essential being (עצמות, Atzmus) and God's revelation as king (מלכות, Malchut) — Malchut is the Sefirah through which the infinite becomes relationally accessible to created beings without compromising the divine transcendence. The Rashab develops how the concept of 'king' points precisely to the aspect of divinity that interfaces with those it governs.

**Resolution**: The 'coronation' of Rosh Hashanah refers not to a change in divine being but to a heightened revelation of Malchut — the dimension of divine kingship that is always present but becomes more accessible to finite perception on this day. The Or Chozer of the collective soul's prayer on Rosh Hashanah uniquely draws down this revelation.

## Deep Dive

### The Construct Being Built

Delivered on the morning of the second day of Rosh Hashana 5666, this maamar builds the hemshech's definitive account of the distinction between Atzmus (divine essence/selfhood) and Malchut (divine kingship as relational mode). It uses the paradox of the Rosh Hashana coronation — how can an infinite, self-sufficient God be "crowned" by finite creatures? — to demonstrate that Malchut is not a diminution of divine transcendence but the precise structural interface through which infinite divine will reaches finite beings without compromising its infinity. The maamar continues directly from the previous night's introduction of the two Da'ot, now focusing specifically on the Sefirah of Malchut.

### The Opening Move

The central tension is stated with philosophical precision: God is Atzmus — absolute, self-sufficient, essentially unrelated to anything external. The very concept of "king" requires a relationship: a king is king only in relation to subjects. If God is truly Atzmus — pure self-being — then the Rosh Hashana declaration "Hashem melech, Hashem malach, Hashem yimloch l'olam va'ed" (the Lord reigns, has reigned, will reign forever) is ontologically problematic. What could it mean for an Infinite Being to "become king" (hitmalich)? And if God was already king before Rosh Hashana, what additional act does the coronation (haktara) perform?

### The Argumentative Arc

- **Malchut as the structural interface, not a change in divine being**: The Rashab's first move is to establish that the "coronation" of Rosh Hashana does not describe a change in God's inner being but a change in the mode of divine self-expression toward creation. Atzmus — divine essence — is utterly beyond relationship. But within divine self-disclosure, there is a specific mode (Malchut) whose entire function is to be in relationship: "ein melech belo am — there is no king without a people." Malchut is the one aspect of divinity whose actualization (in the sense of its coming into its proper mode of expression) requires the existence and acknowledgment of creatures.
- **The paradox of "ein melech belo am" and divine self-sufficiency**: The hemshech develops the tension: if Malchut requires subjects, and subjects are created beings, then the actualization of Malchut is (in some sense) dependent on creation. But this seems to compromise divine self-sufficiency (Atzmus). The resolution is that Malchut's "requirement" of subjects is not a deficiency in God but a structural feature of how God chose to self-express: the divine will (ratzon) included within itself the creation of a domain in which Malchut could be recognized and responded to. The Or Chozer generated by creatures' acknowledgment of divine kingship is therefore not something God needs to be complete but something God desired in order to realize a particular quality of divine self-expression — specifically, the quality of dira betachtonim (divine dwelling in the lowest realm).
- **Or Chozer as the mechanism of hatkara (coronation)**: On Rosh Hashana, the collective prayer-act of the Jewish people generates a massive Or Chozer: the simultaneous ascent of countless souls' acknowledgment of divine kingship. This collective Or Chozer is what the Rashab calls "haktara" — the crowning — not because it installs something new in God but because it activates the Malchut-dimension of divinity in a way that could not be activated from above (from the side of Or Yashar alone). Or Yashar descends from Atzmus; Or Chozer ascends from below; their meeting-point — the kav (line) of divine self-disclosure — is where Malchut is realized. Rosh Hashana is the annual moment when this circuit is completed most fully.
- **The two days of Rosh Hashana as Or Yashar / Or Chozer**: The maamar suggests (following a pattern established in the hemshech) that the two days of Rosh Hashana correspond to Or Yashar and Or Chozer respectively. The first day is the day of Or Yashar — divine kingship descending from above, Atzmus expressing itself as Malchut. The second day (on which this maamar is delivered) is the day of Or Chozer — the human community's ascending response, the collective act of haktara that completes the circuit. This is why the second day has a distinctive character: it is not merely a repetition of the first but the reflection and return of what the first initiated.
- **Malchut's two aspects — governance (hanhagah) and inner royal dignity (malkhut atzmi)**: The Rashab distinguishes between Malchut as governance (the Sefirah's function of ordering and sustaining the created order — this is its external aspect) and Malchut as inner royal dignity (the irreducible quality of being-king that exists prior to any exercise of governance — this is its internal aspect, closer to Atzmus). The haktara of Rosh Hashana draws specifically from the inner aspect of Malchut — the divine royal dignity that is always present but becomes perceptible to creatures only through their own act of recognition.

### What It Gets To

The maamar concludes that the "coronation" of Rosh Hashana is the activation of Malchut's inner-royal-dignity dimension (as opposed to its governance-function). This activation requires Or Chozer from below — specifically, the Or Chozer generated by the collective act of "imru l'fanai Malkhuyot" (reciting kingship-verses). The shofar (established in the night's maamar as the trans-intellectual activator) opens the channel through which this Or Chozer can reach the level of Malchut's pnimiyut. The conclusion advances the hemshech's thesis that the highest divine disclosures (pnimiyut Atik) are reached not through the intellectual ascent of Or Yashar but through the volitional/experiential Or Chozer of human avodah.

### How This Fits the Hemshech

This maamar forms a pair with the preceding night's discourse: together they constitute the hemshech's complete Rosh Hashana theology — night establishing the two-Da'at structure and the shofar's function, day establishing the Atzmus/Malchut distinction and the mechanics of haktara. The distinction between Atzmus and Malchut, between divine self-sufficiency and divine relational expression, is one of the hemshech's master-distinctions, recurring throughout all twenty years of the discourse. This 5666 formulation is among the most systematic: it establishes that Or Chozer is not a secondary or derivative divine benefit but the necessary activating condition for the highest divine self-disclosure (pnimiyut Atik / dira betachtonim). This will be the theological foundation for the Shabbat Teshuva maamar's account of teshuva as Or Chozer par excellence.
